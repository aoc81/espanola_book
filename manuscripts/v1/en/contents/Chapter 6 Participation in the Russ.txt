Chapter 6: Participation in the Russian Invasion of Ukraine (2022–present)
From the siege of Azovstal to the trenches of Chasiv Yar: the baptism of fire of an irregular formation

The true test of any military formation does not lie in its organizational structures or recruitment strategies, but in its performance under the extreme conditions of real combat. For Española, the Russian invasion of Ukraine launched on February 24, 2022 provided the ultimate laboratory to validate or refute theories about the effective militarization of extremist subcultures. Its participation in some of the most intense and prolonged battles of the conflict has generated an operational record that allows for the evaluation of both the strengths and limitations of its unique organizational model.
Mariupol: The Baptism of Fire at Azovstal
Española’s first significant participation in the large-scale invasion occurred during the siege of Mariupol, particularly in operations against the Azovstal steel plant, which became the final redoubt of Ukrainian resistance. This battle not only marked the formation’s entry into the contemporary conflict, but also established operational patterns that would characterize its later participation in the war.
Azovstal represented an extraordinary tactical challenge: a massive industrial complex with extensive underground tunnels, improvised fortifications, and highly motivated defenders from Ukraine’s Azov Regiment. For Española, this confrontation had both military and symbolic dimensions, given that its founding narrative was explicitly constructed as a counterforce to Azov.
Operations at Azovstal demonstrated both the brigade’s capabilities and its limitations. On one hand, its experience in organized urban violence, derived from its origins in football hooliganism, translated effectively to combat in complex urban environments. Spanish Brigade fighters showed familiarity with coordinated movement in confined spaces, maintenance of group cohesion under enemy fire, and the ability to operate in situations where traditional command structures were inadequate.
However, the siege also revealed the limitations of an irregular formation facing professional defenders in prepared positions. Unlike stadium confrontations that had shaped the previous experience of many fighters, Azovstal required the sustainment of intensive operations over weeks, coordinated with heavy artillery and air support beyond the brigade’s independent capabilities.
One particular incident during the fall of Mariupol has become part of the brigade’s folklore: the appropriation of lion cubs from the city zoo during the chaos of the final battles. Far from being a minor anecdote, this episode illustrates important features of Española’s organizational culture. The lions, which remain a prominent feature in the brigade’s media materials, function as symbols of dominance and exoticism that reinforce the formation’s distinctive identity.
Southern Fronts: Kherson and Zaporizhzhia
After Mariupol, Española was redeployed to the Kherson and Zaporizhzhia fronts, where the operational conditions differed significantly from the intensive urban combat at the steel plant. These theaters were characterized by more stabilized front lines, reconnaissance fighting, and operations emphasizing infiltration and sabotage rather than massive frontal assaults.
This transition allowed the brigade to more effectively deploy the specialized skills developed during its reorganization as a PMC. The reconnaissance units, direct heirs of Orlov’s “Skull and Bones” experience, showed particular effectiveness in deep reconnaissance operations requiring small groups operating independently behind enemy lines.
The southern fronts also provided opportunities for the brigade to develop capabilities in electronic warfare and drone operations, specialties that had become crucial for military effectiveness in the Ukrainian conflict. Española’s investment in training 100 drone operators, announced in February 2023, reflected adaptation to the emerging tactical realities of the modern battlefield.
However, these fronts also exposed the formation to new types of vulnerabilities. Dispersed operations required greater independence of small units, but also reduced the group cohesion that had constituted one of the brigade’s main strengths. Additionally, the positional warfare characteristic of these sectors provided fewer opportunities for the kind of aggressive initiative that had distinguished the formation in Mariupol.
Vuhledar: Facing Prepared Defenses
Española’s involvement in the fighting for Vuhledar represented one of the most significant tactical challenges faced by the formation. This mining city, situated on a natural defensive elevation and extensively fortified by Ukrainian forces, required types of operations for which the brigade was less prepared.
Russian attempts to capture Vuhledar were characterized by repeated frontal attacks against well-prepared defensive positions—a type of operation that maximized the disadvantages of irregular formations like Española while minimizing their strengths. The effectiveness of the brigade in reconnaissance and infiltration operations provided little value when the primary tasks required coordinated advances across open terrain under directed artillery fire.
The casualties suffered during these battles were particularly significant for a formation whose effectiveness depended on accumulated experience and personal relationships forged over years of association. The loss of veterans not only reduced immediate tactical capabilities, but also eroded the mentorship networks that allowed for the effective integration of new recruits.
Bakhmut: The Meatgrinder
The battles in Bakhmut, where Española operated in parallel with Wagner Group formations, provided crucial insights into the relative capabilities of different models of irregular military organization. The intensity and duration of these battles—which stretched over months of devastating urban warfare—created conditions that favored formations with strong group cohesion and the ability to maintain morale under extreme conditions.
The presence of Andrey Solomatin, the former Russian national footballer who had joined the brigade in August 2023, during operations near Bakhmut illustrated both the opportunities and risks associated with the formation’s high public profile. His participation provided valuable media credibility, but also attracted attention that could compromise operational security.
The fighting in Bakhmut also demonstrated the importance of the technical specializations the brigade had developed. Drone operations became crucial for reconnaissance and artillery targeting, while electronic warfare capabilities provided tactical advantages in the saturated communications environment that characterized these intensive urban battles.
Avdiivka: Prolonged Siege Operations
Española’s involvement in the battle for Avdiivka in early 2024 marked another evolution in the formation’s operational capabilities. This heavily fortified city required types of siege operations that combined tactical patience with selective aggression—demands more effectively aligned with the brigade’s strengths.
The operations in Avdiivka also coincided with significant organizational changes in Española, particularly its progressive integration into the Ministry of Defense’s Volunteer Corps. This transition provided improved access to artillery support and coordination with conventional units, but also imposed operational restrictions that limited the tactical flexibility that had characterized the brigade’s earlier operations.
Chasiv Yar: The Most Recent Battles
Española’s most recent combat engagements have occurred in the direction of Chasiv Yar, where the formation has faced some of the most complex operational challenges in its history. This area, characterized by dense urban terrain and sophisticated Ukrainian defensive positions, has required tactical adaptations that reflect the ongoing evolution of the conflict.
The death of Pavel “Joker” Apalkov, deputy commander of the brigade’s sniper platoon, on November 5, 2024 during these battles, illustrated both the importance of technical specializations and the human costs associated with prolonged operations. Apalkov represented the kind of technical expertise that had allowed the brigade to maintain operational relevance as the conflict evolved into more sophisticated forms.
The fighting in Chasiv Yar has also highlighted the growing importance of electronic warfare and drone operations, areas in which the brigade had invested considerably in capacity development. However, these battles have also underscored the limitations of irregular formations when facing defenders equipped with modern weapons systems and extensive professional training.
Significant Casualties and Their Organizational Impact
Vyacheslav Subbotin: The Loss of a Key Leader
The death of Vyacheslav Subbotin, head of Española's Reconnaissance Division, in combat in Donbas on March 3, 2024, represented a particularly significant loss for the formation’s leadership structure. Subbotin, known by his nickname “Vykhodnoy,” embodied the characteristic ideological contradictions of the brigade: a self-declared neo-Nazi whose military expertise was valued independently of his extremist affiliations.
Subbotin’s loss attracted additional media attention precisely because his death underscored the tensions between operational effectiveness and extremist ideology that characterize the formation. His role as head of reconnaissance had been crucial to the brigade’s successful operations, but his ideological identity also complicated the official Russian narrative regarding the nature of the conflict.
The death of mid-level leaders like Subbotin presents particular challenges for irregular formations such as Española. Unlike conventional military units where roles can be replaced through standardized training systems, irregular formations depend on individual leaders whose authority derives from respect earned through demonstrated competence and courage.
Pavel “Joker” Apalkov: Loss of Technical Specialization
The death of Pavel Apalkov in the battles of Chasiv Yar illustrated a different type of organizational vulnerability: the brigade’s dependence on technical specialists whose skills take years to develop. As deputy commander of the sniper platoon, Apalkov represented not only immediate combat capability, but also the institutional expertise necessary to train future generations of specialists.
Sniper operations require unique combinations of technical skills, psychological discipline, and tactical knowledge that cannot be rapidly replicated through accelerated training programs. The loss of individuals like Apalkov therefore represents long-term degradation in operational capabilities that may be difficult to remedy.
Attacks on Infrastructure: The Crimea Incident
Between June 23 and 24, Russian milbloggers claimed that two Ukrainian Neptune missiles had struck the command post of the 88th Volunteer Brigade “Española” in Crimea. According to these reports, the base had previously been evacuated, suggesting that Ukrainian intelligence had identified the location but that Russian security measures had mitigated the potential impact.
This incident, regardless of its complete veracity, illustrates vulnerabilities faced by military formations in the era of modern warfare, where static assets can be identified and targeted through combinations of technical and human intelligence. For Española, whose social media presence provides considerable information about locations and activities, these risks are particularly significant.
The apparent targeting of command facilities also suggests that Ukrainian forces have identified the brigade as an asset valuable enough to justify the allocation of intelligence and precision targeting resources. This attention may reflect both the formation’s operational effectiveness and its symbolic value in the wider context of the conflict.
Assessment of Operational Effectiveness
Española’s participation in multiple theaters and types of operations provides sufficient data to assess the effectiveness of its distinctive organizational model. In intensive urban combat such as at Azovstal and Bakhmut, the brigade demonstrated capabilities rivaling professional military formations—particularly in maintaining cohesion under extreme conditions and tactical adaptability.
However, in operations that required coordination with heavy weapons systems or the sustainment of operations over extended periods, the formation’s limitations became more apparent. Dependence on individual enthusiasm and group cohesion, while effective for short-term intensive operations, proved insufficient for sustained actions requiring sophisticated logistics and technical support systems.
The technical specializations developed by the brigade—particularly in electronic warfare and drone operations—have shown significant value, but have also created dependencies on individual expertise that make the formation vulnerable to the loss of key personnel. This vulnerability contrasts with conventional military systems where technical knowledge is more broadly distributed through formal training institutions.
Lessons and Adaptations
Española’s operational experience has generated several important lessons about the militarization of extremist subcultures. First, the effectiveness of such formations depends heavily on matching operational requirements with organizational strengths, rather than attempting to adapt irregular formations to roles that favor conventional military structures.
Second, the sustainability of such formations requires balancing the maintenance of a distinctive identity that generates effectiveness with the development of institutional systems for knowledge transfer that reduce dependency on individual expertise.
Third, the integration of irregular formations within conventional military structures can provide access to resources and coordination, but also constrains the flexibility that constitutes one of their primary advantages.
