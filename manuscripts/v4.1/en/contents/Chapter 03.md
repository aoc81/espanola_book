# Chapter 3 — From Terrace to Trench

In the summer of 2023, a journalist writing for Daily Sabah visited what had once been a football stadium in Mariupol. The city had been under Russian occupation since the spring of 2022, following a siege whose brutality had made it one of the defining episodes of the war. Inside the damaged ground, Ukrainian club symbols had been removed or covered over. In their place, according to the report, were insignia associated with Russian clubs — CSKA, Zenit — and with Española itself. A photograph from the scene showed fighters described as the formation's members posing in what had been a Ukrainian sporting space, now reordered under a different set of symbols.[^ch03-008]

The image — stadium reclaimed, Ukrainian markings displaced, Russian supporter insignia made permanent by occupation — is too neat to be accidental. Española's people understood what they were doing. The stadium was not simply a convenient shelter or a backdrop for a photograph. It was a statement about which world had prevailed, and the terms of that statement were drawn directly from the language of supporter culture: whose club, whose turf, whose symbols on the walls. The war, in this image, was being settled in the grammar of the terrace.

That grammar had been in formation for years. The social achievement that Española claimed for itself — and that reporting and external analysis largely corroborated — was not merely the recruitment of football supporters. It was something more specific: the assembly of men who came from rival clubs, with all the tribal memory and mutual antagonism that entailed, into a formation that could function under a single identity. A CSKA core mixed with Spartak-affiliated men and Zenit figures and others does not, in ordinary peacetime life, mix without friction. Years of organized confrontation had made those boundaries deeply felt. To suspend them required something more powerful than an administrative directive.[^ch03-001]

What Española offered, according to the brigade's own channels and the reporting built around them, was a new hierarchy of belonging. The old rivalries were not abolished — that would have been both impossible and tactically foolish, since the same intensity that made supporter culture cohesive also made it resistant to top-down erasure. Instead, they were subordinated. There was a larger enemy now: Ukraine, Azov, the Western-backed "denazification" narrative inverted. There was a larger stage: not the Luzhniki curve or the south stand at the Petrovsky, but Donbas, Mariupol, Bakhmut. And there was a larger story: the brotherhood of men who had already demonstrated, through years of organized violence, that they could be counted on when things got difficult.[^ch03-003]

The mechanism, understood from the outside, looks almost obvious. Almost. What made it work in practice was the care taken to honor the old identities while transcending them. Callsigns preserved individual roughness. Insignia mixed the pirate aesthetic of the formation's brand with club-associated references that specific individuals could claim as their own. The founder's CSKA biography gave the project authenticity from within the supporter world without requiring everyone to abandon their own club loyalties at the door. And the language of the channels — heavy with the idiom of supporter culture, full of references that would be opaque to outsiders but immediately legible to the initiated — kept the formation's internal climate warm even for men who had once fought each other on the streets.[^ch03-004]

None of this should be described as harmonious. The open record is too thin to map every internal fault line inside the formation, and it would be naïve to assume that years of deeply socialized rivalry simply dissolved under the pressures of war. Club affiliation almost certainly remained meaningful inside Española — as social memory, as the basis of personal networks, as a source of fine-grained status distinctions invisible to anyone looking from outside. The claim worth making is more limited. The formation built an architecture in which those distinctions mattered less than membership in the named brotherhood. That is a real organizational achievement, and it did not happen automatically.[^ch03-005]

What the terrace transferred to the trench was not only a set of bodies. It was a set of practices. Men accustomed to planning and executing organized confrontations in environments with their own rules, their own surveillance risks, and their own internal hierarchies brought all of that with them. The capacity to move quickly in small groups, to recognize signals and act on them, to maintain cohesion under pressure and scrutiny — these were not skills acquired in a training center. They were already present, accumulated through years of activity that the outside world had understood mainly as a public-order problem. The war offered a context in which the same skills could be presented as military virtue.

The Mariupol stadium photograph shows the end of that journey in miniature. Men who had spent years defining themselves through club symbols were now in a stadium from which one country's club symbols had been removed, posing under another country's signs. The code was the same. The game had changed entirely.
