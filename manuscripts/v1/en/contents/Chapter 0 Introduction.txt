Española: Russian Hooligans in Ukraine
An open-source analysis of the militarization of extremist subcultures in the Russian-Ukrainian conflict
Introduction: The Phenomenon of Española

On November 7, 2024, the British government added a peculiar name to its list of sanctions: PMC Española and its leader, Stanislav Orlov. For casual observers of the conflict in Ukraine, the designation might have gone unnoticed amidst the dozens of Russian entities sanctioned weekly since February 2022. However, for those who have closely followed the evolution of irregular Russian military formations, these sanctions represented the official recognition of a phenomenon as fascinating as it is disturbing: the transformation of football hooligans into an active paramilitary force on the Ukrainian front.The “Spanish Brigade”—formally known as the 88th Reconnaissance and Sabotage Brigade—constitutes one of the most extraordinary cases of military adaptation in the 21st century. It is not simply another Russian private military company (PMC), but rather a hybrid formation that has managed to convert the organized violence of football stands into real military capability, while maintaining a complex network of loyalties ranging from oligarchs to intelligence services.
The Paradox of Denazification
Perhaps no aspect of Española is as revealing—or ironic—as its ideological composition. In a conflict that the Kremlin justifies as a “denazification operation” in Ukraine, one of its most active formations is composed of individuals who openly embrace neo-Nazi symbology. The unit’s formal name itself, “88th Brigade,” is an explicit reference to the neo-Nazi code “Heil Hitler,” while prominent figures such as Mikhail “Pitbull” Turkanov display swastika tattoos.This contradiction is not merely anecdotal, but rather illustrates a broader reality about the nature of contemporary authoritarianism: the state’s capacity to instrumentalize even those forces that, in theory, contradict its official narrative. The Española functions as a microcosm of the inherent tensions in the Russian imperial project, where military pragmatism outweighs ideological considerations.
Methodology and Sources
This research is based on an exhaustive analysis of open sources compiled between 2022 and 2024, including content from social networks (Telegram, VKontakte), reports from Russian and international media, leaked official documents, and testimonies from combatants. The methodology used follows the standards established by Bellingcat for information verification in conflict environments, with particular emphasis on cross-checking data and geolocating visual content.The main challenge has been navigating the extensive propagandistic output of the brigade itself, which maintains active channels on multiple digital platforms. This self-exposure, unusual in conventional military formations, provides a unique window into its internal workings, but requires careful filtering to separate operational reality from image construction.
A Laboratory of Hybrid Warfare
Española transcends its specific military importance to become a case study on the evolution of contemporary warfare. Its hybrid structure—at once an independent PMC and an extension of the Russian state apparatus—reflects the increasing blurring of lines between state and non-state actors in modern conflicts.Even more significant is its role as an experiment in the mobilization of marginal subcultures. The relative success of the Española in channeling the organized violence of football hooliganism toward military objectives suggests a model that could be replicated in other contexts. The implications of this militarization transcend Ukraine’s borders, offering lessons on how authoritarian states can leverage seemingly disconnected social currents to project power.
Structure of the Analysis
This study is structured into six main parts, each designed to examine a specific aspect of the Española phenomenon. We begin with the historical origins and organizational transformation, then delve into its ideological composition and recruitment strategies. The subsequent sections analyze military operations, financing networks, and finally international controversies and repercussions.Throughout the analysis, particular attention is paid to the visual and testimonial documentation produced by the brigade itself. This approach allows not only for a reconstruction of the facts, but also an understanding of how the Española constructs and projects its identity, both towards potential recruits and Russian society in general.
Contemporary Relevance
The phenomenon of Española cannot be understood as an isolated case. Its emergence coincides with the rise of multiple Russian paramilitary formations, from the infamous Wagner Group to lesser-known units such as the Uran battalion or the Soyuz unit. However, the Española stands out due to its specific social base and its exceptional media transparency.Understanding this formation is crucial to analyzing not only the development of the Ukrainian conflict, but also broader transformations in Russian society. The Russian state’s ability to mobilize traditionally marginal subcultures suggests both strengths and vulnerabilities in its power structure, with implications that extend far beyond the Ukrainian theater.In the following pages, we will unravel the complex web that connects the stands of Russian football with the trenches of Ukraine, revealing how an urban subculture was transformed into an instrument of hybrid war in the 21st century.
