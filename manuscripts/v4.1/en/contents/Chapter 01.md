# Chapter 1 — Before the War: The Stadium as a Political Incubator

On 11 December 2010, several thousand people gathered in Manezhnaya Square, less than two hundred meters from the Kremlin wall, after the killing of Yegor Sviridov, a Spartak Moscow supporter stabbed to death in a street altercation involving men from the North Caucasus. The initial gathering was a memorial. Within hours it had become something else. Nationalist slogans. Fighting. Attacks on people who looked Caucasian or Central Asian. The police, caught between competing imperatives, contained what they could and let much of the rest pass. By any measure, what unfolded in Moscow's most symbolically loaded public square was the largest racially motivated riot in modern Russian history.[^ch01-001]

The riot did not come from nowhere, and it did not simply dissipate. It came from a subculture that had been building organizational density for two decades, and it left behind a cleaner picture of what that subculture was actually for.

Russian football hooliganism had developed since the 1980s and intensified through the 1990s in ways that mirrored, and sometimes directly borrowed from, English and Eastern European models. By the 2000s, the landscape included not merely enthusiastic supporters but organized firms with names, hierarchies, and reputations that traveled far beyond their home cities. Groups associated with CSKA Moscow — including the Red-Blue Warriors — and with Spartak Moscow's Fratria orbit, as well as organized formations around Zenit Saint Petersburg, Lokomotiv Moscow, Dynamo, and other clubs, represented something more structured than casual audience aggression. Fights were planned and reviewed. Status was carefully maintained. The most serious participants trained in combat sports. Violence was not random. It was social capital, accumulated through confrontation and remembered in group hierarchy.[^ch01-002]

What distinguished the Russian milieu from its Western equivalents, however, was a more explicit entanglement with nationalist and xenophobic politics. Reporting on supporter culture in the 2000s and into the 2010s returned repeatedly to a consistent overlap: the football firm, the nationalist street movement, and the hard edge of the far right were not always the same thing, but they were frequently in conversation. Manezhnaya made that conversation public. The chant that moved from the stadium to the square on that December night — a phrase demanding Russia for Russians — did not begin there. It had a long prior life on the terraces.[^ch01-003]

This matters for understanding what came later, but only if kept within precise limits. Not every organized supporter was an ideologue. Not every hooligan was a political actor. The subculture contained ordinary sports passion, working-class masculinity, city loyalty, and the social pleasures of belonging to a tight group in a large city. To flatten all of it into proto-fascism would be to misread the environment and, consequently, to misread how a formation like Española was eventually able to draw from it.

The more accurate claim is narrower. What the supporter milieu normalized, across a long period and through repeated practice, were forms of belonging and collective action that could later be redirected. Small-group cohesion built through sustained confrontation. The capacity to recognize symbols instantly and act on them. Hierarchy enforced by reputation rather than formal rank. Tolerance for physical risk as proof of commitment. A masculine honor code in which loyalty was measured through the willingness to stand and fight. None of those traits belonged exclusively to football. All of them would prove transferable.[^ch01-003]

The Russian state's relationship to this world was never straightforward. Authorities monitored it, occasionally repressed it, and sometimes quietly co-opted it. The political utility of organized supporter networks was not missed: they were disciplined male collectivities that could be mobilized around nationalist themes faster than any conventional civic structure could manage. After Manezhnaya and subsequent disturbances, the state moved more deliberately to sort, register, and control the supporter ecology. Fan ID — biometric registration of stadium attendees — was piloted and eventually extended, reaching fuller implementation in the 2020s. For many in the subculture, it was experienced as a kind of bureaucratic suffocation: the state colonizing the one space that had operated by its own rules.[^ch01-004] [^ch01-005]

That pressure had an effect the authorities may not have fully calculated. It did not eliminate the subculture. It pushed some of its energy out of the stadium and into less easily monitored channels. Men who had spent years defining themselves through club rivalry, physical confrontation, and subcultural loyalty were offered fewer and fewer arenas in which that definition still worked. When war came, it offered something else: a larger enemy, a higher story, and a context in which the same habits of belonging and aggression could be presented not as a public-order problem but as a patriotic resource.

That is not a mechanical argument. There was no conveyor belt from the Red-Blue Warriors to the trenches outside Mariupol. The individuals who ended up inside Española made choices, and those choices were shaped by many things besides their supporter past. But the cultural infrastructure — the codes, the emotional repertoire, the organizational memory — that the football world had built was real, and it made the passage less surprising than it would otherwise have been. A man who has spent years inside a world that prizes loyalty over legality, confrontation over comfort, and collective identity over individual interest does not require retraining for war. He requires a reason and a myth.[^ch01-006] [^ch01-007]

Stanislav Orlov came from inside that world. What he eventually built drew on it. The stadium was not incidental background to Española's story. It was the first laboratory.
