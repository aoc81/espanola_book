Chapter 1: The Foundations of “Ispanec”
From the CSKA Moscow stands to the battlefields of Donbas

In the cold stands of Luzhniki Stadium, amidst the deafening chants and red and blue flares, few people could have foreseen that one of the most dedicated ultras of CSKA Moscow would, decades later, become the commander of one of the most controversial paramilitary formations in the Russian-Ukrainian war. Yet the trajectory of Stanislav Orlov—from his youth as a member of the notorious “Red-Blue Warriors” to his role as founder of Española—perfectly illustrates how violent subcultures can be channeled toward broader geopolitical goals.
The Ultra Who Became a Soldier
Stanislav Orlov was born in a time of radical transformation for Russia. By the time he was drafted into the Russian army to serve in the Airborne Forces in 1999, the young CSKA ultra had already been shaped by years of organized violence in Moscow stadiums. His assignment to the “hot zones” of the North Caucasus, including the Second Chechen War, was no coincidence: Russian military authorities had begun to recognize the military potential of individuals hardened by football hooligan culture.
The 1999-2009 Chechen-Russian war served as a laboratory for a generation of fighters who would later emerge as key figures in subsequent hybrid conflicts. Orlov, according to sources close to the brigade, distinguished himself with his leadership ability and aptitude for reconnaissance operations, skills that would later prove fundamental in his career as a paramilitary commander.
“The experience in Chechnya changed everything,” states a 2023 analysis of Russian veterans in irregular formations. “These men learned that modern war is not limited to conventional clashes between regular armies, but includes a complex matrix of state, paramilitary, and civilian actors.”
The Road to Donbas
Orlov’s transition from military veteran to irregular fighter began in February 2014, when the events in Maidan, Kyiv, triggered a chain of reactions that would transform the European geopolitical landscape. For the Kremlin, the prominent participation of Ukrainian football fan organizations in the protests represented both a threat and an opportunity: if hooligans could mobilize against Russian interests, they could also be used in support of them.
Orlov’s decision to travel to Donbas in 2014 must be understood within this broader context. As documented by Western intelligence reports, hundreds of Russian fighters—many of them with backgrounds in football hooliganism—crossed the border into Ukrainian territory during this period⁵. For individuals like Orlov, hardened by years of combat both in stadiums and in warzones, the conflict in eastern Ukraine provided an opportunity to apply their skills in a theater that combined conventional warfare with urban guerrilla operations.
The Birth of “Ispanec”
It was in the occupied city of Horlivka that Stanislav Orlov acquired the alias that would eventually give its name to an entire brigade. Integrated into a unit commanded by Igor Bezler, alias “Bes”—a former GRU colonel who had become one of the most notorious separatist commanders—Orlov quickly distinguished himself through his ability to coordinate foreign fighters⁶.
According to the official version put forth by the brigade itself, the nickname “Ispanec” (Spanish) arose from a combination of factors: Orlov’s knowledge of foreign languages, including Spanish, and his role as coordinator of international mercenaries, which supposedly included a Spanish fighter⁷. However, open source analysis suggests that adopting this alias also responded to broader tactical considerations, creating an operational identity that provided some distance from his Russian origins.
“The choice of foreign aliases by Russian commanders in Donbas was not random,” explains a 2024 report on military nomenclature. “These names served multiple purposes: confusing Ukrainian intelligence, creating an image of revolutionary internationalism, and allowing for a degree of plausible deniability on Moscow’s part.”
“Skull and Bones”: The Prototype
Orlov’s true innovation in the Donbas theater was the creation of the reconnaissance unit “Skull and Bones” (Cherep i Kosti), an experiment in the direct militarization of football hooligan networks. The unit, which included members of the Spartak Moscow ultra firm—a traditional rival of Orlov’s CSKA—represented one of the first systematic attempts to channel football rivalries toward shared military objectives.
“Skull and Bones” operations near Horlivka and during the 2015 Battle of Debaltseve provided valuable lessons on the military effectiveness of fighters from organized violent subcultures. According to leaked documents from the time, the unit specialized in deep reconnaissance and sabotage operations, leveraging its members’ familiarity with group coordination and tactical violence learned in stadium settings.
“What we saw in ‘Skull and Bones’ was essentially a prototype,” notes a later military analysis. “Orlov had discovered how to transform the tribal loyalty of hooliganism into effective military cohesion, while maintaining the operational flexibility characteristic of irregular formations.”
Temporary Exile and the Montenegrin Experiment
The period between 2015 and 2022 marks a crucial but little-documented phase in Orlov’s evolution. After leaving the Donbas front in 2015, he joined the “Union of Donbas Volunteers,” an organization coordinated by Vladislav Surkov, then an influential advisor to Putin, and led by Alexander Khodakovsky. This transition suggests that Orlov’s demonstrated capabilities had attracted attention in higher circles of Moscow’s power structure.
The most intriguing incident from this period occurred in September 2016, when Orlov was reported in Montenegro, allegedly involved in the failed coup attempt against Prime Minister Milo Đukanović. While the details remain classified, Orlov’s presence in this operation indicates that his skills had been recognized for more sophisticated international destabilization missions.
Following the failure of the Montenegrin coup, Orlov effectively disappeared from public view, a period that close sources describe as one of “strategic recalibration.” This temporary exile allowed him to observe from a distance the developments in the landscape of hybrid conflicts, including the rise of formations such as the Wagner Group and the evolution of Ukrainian military capabilities.
2022: The Resurgence and Genesis of Española
On February 24, 2022, when Vladimir Putin announced the start of the “special military operation” in Ukraine, Stanislav Orlov emerged from relative obscurity with an ambitious proposal: to create a new formation that would combine the lessons learned in Donbas with the military capabilities developed during his exile.
The decision to gather former “Skull and Bones” comrades to form what would eventually become Española was not merely nostalgic. Analyses of the unit’s initial composition reveal a careful selection of individuals with experience both in organized hooliganism and military operations, creating a unique recruitment base within the Russian irregular formations landscape.
Initially operating under the umbrella of the Vostok battalion of the self-proclaimed Donetsk People’s Republic, Orlov’s new formation immediately set itself apart by its distinctive ethos and combat effectiveness. The choice of the name “Española”—a direct extension of its commander’s alias—not only provided narrative continuity, but also established a distinctive brand in the crowded Russian paramilitary market.
The Counterforce to Azov
From its earliest public statements, Española explicitly positioned itself as a counterforce to the Ukrainian Azov Regiment, a narrative that resonated deeply both with official Russian propaganda and with the personal motivations of its fighters. This constructed rivalry was no accident: both formations shared origins in football hooliganism and had evolved into significant military capabilities, creating a parallel that Española exploited for both tactical and propagandistic purposes.
“Española’s obsession with Azov goes beyond simple military rivalry,” observes a 2023 analysis. “It represents a fundamental competition over which model of militarizing extremist subcultures will prove most effective in the context of the Russian-Ukrainian conflict.”
This constructed rivalry also served broader strategic objectives for the Kremlin. By presenting Española as the “legitimate” answer to Azov’s “Nazism,” Russian authorities could simultaneously utilize a formation with clear neo-Nazi elements while maintaining the official narrative of Ukraine’s “denazification.”
The Legacy of the Foundations
Looking back at Stanislav Orlov’s trajectory from CSKA ultra to commander of Española, patterns emerge that transcend individual history to reveal broader trends in the evolution of contemporary warfare. His ability to transform football loyalties into military cohesion, combine conventional experience with irregular operations, and navigate the complex networks of power connecting oligarchs, intelligence services, and state structures, represents a model that has been replicated in multiple contexts.
The foundations established by “Ispanec” in his formative years—from the Luzhniki stands to the battlefields of Chechnya and Donbas—not only shaped Española, but also provided a template for the militarization of marginal subcultures that continues to influence contemporary conflicts.
In the next chapter, we will examine how these personal foundations were translated into organizational structures, as Española evolved from a volunteer unit into a complex private military company with both official and unofficial ties to the Russian state apparatus.
