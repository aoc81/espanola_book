Chapter 8 — Who Paid for the Brigade?

No formation like Espanola survives on enthusiasm alone. Combat footage, pirate insignia, and fraternity rhetoric may attract attention, but they do not buy drones, communications equipment, transport, salaries, or the public machinery needed to keep a brand alive. The brigade's financial architecture is therefore one of the most revealing parts of the story. It is also one of the areas where the prose must move carefully, because much of what matters most is reconstructed through investigative reporting rather than official disclosure.

At the broadest level, the brigade appears to have operated through a layered economy. There was grassroots or small-donor support, often routed through humanitarian language and public-facing campaigns. There were supply managers and intermediaries who helped translate material needs into organized collection. And there was a higher level of patronage that outside reporting connected to politically powerful actors, most notably Viktor Shendrik and, through him, figures in the Rotenberg orbit. The key point is not to pretend this chain is judicially settled. It is to recognize the pattern across independent reporting: Espanola looks less like a self-sustaining militia than like a formation embedded in a plausible ecosystem of patronage.

Investigations cited in Meduza and echoed in later analytical pieces described Shendrik, head of security at Russian Railways, as an important intermediary around the brigade. Those same reports linked him to Arkady and Boris Rotenberg, oligarchs with deep proximity to the Kremlin. One of the strongest and most repeated allegations in the file set is that Espanola may have served as the basis for a kind of private army project under elite sponsorship. That claim must remain attributed. But even at the level of attribution, it is analytically explosive. It suggests that the brigade's value extended beyond battlefield utility into the political economy of privatized force among Russia's connected elites.

The lower tiers of support were no less important. Donations, logistics drives, and the social intimacy of the humanitarian wing made the brigade legible to ordinary supporters. A small donor funding equipment through a public campaign and an elite intermediary facilitating larger flows through administrative networks belonged to different rungs of the same ladder. Both helped transform the brigade from a fighting unit into a durable enterprise.

This is why the finances should not be separated too sharply into "grassroots" and "oligarchic" spheres. The two reinforced one another. Popular support gave the formation moral texture and public plausibility. Elite patronage, if the reporting is broadly correct, supplied insulation, access, and a scale of support ordinary donations could not match. The resulting structure was hybrid in the same way the brigade itself was hybrid: intimate enough to feel authentic, connected enough to survive.

The allegations around Russian Railways matter especially because they show how force, infrastructure, and elite networks can meet without a clean institutional line between them. A brigade that moved through military proxies like Redut while drawing alleged patronage from the orbit of a major state-linked corporation was neither neatly private nor neatly state. It lived in the zone where contemporary Russian war organization often prefers to operate: deniable where useful, legible where necessary.

There is a temptation to turn the funding chapter into a detective board of proper nouns. That would miss the larger point. The real importance of financing lies in what kind of organization it made possible. A brigade that can pay, recruit, advertise, equip specialists, sustain a humanitarian wing, and maintain a distinctive media presence is not just being kept alive. It is being built as an asset.

This, in turn, deepened the autonomy problem that would later become central to the book. A formation with its own donors, its own alleged patrons, and its own public-facing legitimacy acquires more than money. It acquires political weight. That is one reason the brigade's propaganda mattered so much. It was not only selling combat. It was converting visibility into resources.
