Chapter 7 — Inside the State’s Shadow Chain of Command
Redut, oversight, and the limits of autonomy
For much of its public life, Española worked hard to project a specific illusion. It was disciplined but not bureaucratic, militant but not faceless, technically adaptive but not swallowed by the machinery of the Russian state. In its own telling, and in the sympathetic ecosystems that amplified it, the brigade appeared as something close to an exception: a volunteer formation that had preserved initiative, identity, and internal coherence in a war otherwise associated with rigidity, incompetence, and waste. That image was not entirely fabricated. The brigade does seem to have enjoyed a degree of internal distinctiveness. But it was also misleading in a deeper sense. However branded, however self-conscious, however culturally separate it wanted to appear, Española did not stand outside the command architecture of the Russian war. It existed within it—through contracts, proxies, elite patrons, intelligence-linked relationships, and the larger system through which Moscow learned to use semi-autonomous fighters without fully surrendering control. Multiple source types in this record—independent media, analytical reporting, sanctions data, and even the brigade’s own public claims—point to the same central fact: the unit’s autonomy was always partial.
The most concrete piece of that architecture is Redut.
Several sources in the file set describe Española as part of, or formally subordinate to, Redut, the opaque pseudo-private military structure widely understood as serving as a recruitment and contracting proxy for the Russian Ministry of Defense. Meduza’s English retelling of the iStories reporting says this directly: like most Russian volunteer formations, Española was formally part of Redut, which meant that its fighters signed contracts not with a wholly independent company of their own, but with Redut itself. Ukrainska Pravda, summarizing reporting from Vazhnye Istorii, makes the same point in a more transactional register: women being recruited into Española’s structure were told that their contracts would be signed with Redut, and that this arrangement allowed the formation to avoid certain Ministry of Defense rules applicable elsewhere. Jamestown’s March 2024 profile similarly notes that female recruits signed with Redut rather than Española. Taken together, these accounts establish something fundamental. Whatever the brigade called itself in public, its legal-administrative bloodstream ran through a state-adjacent proxy.
That fact sharply clarifies what Redut actually was in this context. It was not simply a label or umbrella. It was a mechanism by which the state could absorb irregular formations into its war effort without fully regularizing them. Meduza describes Redut as a mercenary recruitment proxy for the Defense Ministry. The September 2024 Meduza feature on the brigade’s public image adds contextual detail in a footnote-style insert, describing Redut’s earlier existence, its reported base near the 45th Special Forces Brigade in Kubinka, and its earlier role guarding Syrian assets linked to the Timchenko orbit. These details do not by themselves prove direct tactical micromanagement of Española from above. They do something more important: they place the brigade inside a pre-existing infrastructure through which Russian state and elite interests had already learned to manage deniable or semi-deniable force.
The brigade’s own claims about its autonomy become more interesting in light of that structure. Jamestown, drawing on Orlov’s statements to Russian media, reports that Española considered itself an “independent PMC” by spring 2023, after its arrangement with the Vostok Brigade expired. Orlov reportedly argued that this transition allowed the unit to decide where to fight and to control its internal discipline, bureaucracy, and training—especially around anti-drone warfare—more fully than before. This should not be dismissed simply because it is self-serving. It likely reflects a real change in the brigade’s internal freedom of action. But the key word is internal. Redut’s role suggests that whatever operational flexibility Española enjoyed, it did so within a framework still anchored to state needs, contracting mechanisms, and higher-level patronage. It could choose some things. It could not choose the system in which it existed.
That distinction—between freedom inside the unit and dependence above the unit—is one of the core structural tensions of the Russian irregular war system. It helps explain why formations like Española could feel genuine to their members while still serving the interests of the state. Fighters could experience themselves as part of a brotherhood, recruited through personal networks, trained in a distinctive way, led by men they knew or admired, and yet remain contractually and politically embedded in a chain of force that pointed upward toward the Defense Ministry, military intelligence, and elite intermediaries. This is what made the system effective. It allowed the state to borrow the emotional energy of informal militancy without having to produce that energy bureaucratically.
The question, then, is who sat further up that chain.
One recurring name in the source base is Vladimir Alekseev, First Deputy Chief of Russia’s General Staff and a figure repeatedly linked in reporting to the Redut ecosystem. Molfar’s analytical file describes Alekseev as one of the key overseers of Redut and, by extension, of formations the Ministry of Defense referred to as “volunteer” units. That account also states that contracts were signed specifically through Redut, not through some fully sovereign Española entity, and it situates the brigade within a system of military intelligence oversight. Because Molfar is an adversarial analytical source, this must remain attributed. But the value of the file is not only in the claim itself; it is in the pattern of corroboration. The suggestion that Redut-linked formations answered, directly or indirectly, to military intelligence structures is consistent with how independent Russian reporting and later analysis have described the broader pseudo-PMC environment.
Molfar adds a second, more tactile indicator of this relationship: it notes that Alekseev had personally awarded medals to Española fighters, including Mikhail Turkanov’s motorcycle unit, and that the brigade itself had announced in September 2024 the launch of a secure Telegram bot “together with specialized services of Russian military intelligence.” Neither point alone proves a formal GRU command chain in the strict military sense. But together they suggest what matters most for this chapter: the brigade was not merely tolerated. It was recognized, interacted with, and at times publicly elevated by structures associated with Russian military intelligence. That is a very different condition from true private autonomy. It means the brigade was not only operating in the state’s shadow. It was moving within its line of sight.
The brigade’s formal-political legibility also surfaces in sanctions and classification data. The OpenSanctions entry for PMC ESPANIOLA identifies the group as an organization under multiple name variants, including “Espanola Brigade” and “PMC ESPANIOLA,” and flags it as a sanctioned entity. Sanctions databases do not, of course, resolve questions of operational control. But they do show how the organization came to be recognized externally: not as an informal club of volunteers, but as a distinct armed body legible enough to be tracked, named, and categorized within a wider international enforcement architecture. That external codification matters because it reflects the brigade’s evolution from subculture into institution-like object. The state may have preferred ambiguity; the outside world increasingly did not.
At the same time, internal Russian narratives continued to insist on a special status that was neither fully state nor fully private. One supportive line of argument portrayed Española as “experimental,” a brigade not built according to ordinary army staffing tables but around whatever tasks the formation itself prioritized. Another presented it as “closed,” in the sense that commanders could not simply be imposed from outside and that the brigade recruited and trained its own people. These descriptions came from explicitly sympathetic environments and should therefore be treated as self-mythologizing or promotional. But they reveal something important about how the brigade’s internal sovereignty was imagined. Even as contracts ran through Redut and even as outside analysts linked the formation to higher state structures, Española still needed to tell itself—and others—that it remained somehow uncolonized by the dead hand of the army. That myth of selective autonomy was part of its attraction.
The same duality appears in its claims to be a PMC. Meduza’s March 2024 retelling notes that in February 2024 Española publicly declared itself a private military company, listing its specialist units and supply management. Yet the same report immediately undercuts the idea of full privateness by explaining that the fighters sign contracts with Redut and that the brigade belongs to the broader ecosystem of Russian volunteer formations. In other words, the PMC label functioned less as a precise legal description than as a political-aesthetic identity. It said: we are not just another battalion. We are a recognizable war corporation, a force with its own structure and name. The state could tolerate such rhetoric because the decisive mechanisms of control lay elsewhere.
Here the role of elite intermediaries becomes impossible to ignore. The Meduza/iStories reporting alleging that Viktor Shendrik and, behind him, the Rotenberg orbit used or planned to use Española as the basis for a private army complicates the brigade’s position still further. If that reporting is broadly correct, then the chain of command above Española was not a simple vertical line from unit to ministry. It was a more hybrid arrangement: military intelligence and Defense Ministry proxies above, patrons and elite sponsors adjacent, local supply managers and public-facing organizers below. This is precisely the kind of structure that modern authoritarian wars generate when the state wants force that is deniable, denser in ideological or subcultural cohesion than regular army formations, and still sufficiently contained to remain useful.
Such structures also help explain why Española could seem, at moments, both unusually independent and unusually protected. A brigade whose commander could speak openly in the media, whose fighters could be publicly decorated by senior military-intelligence figures, whose propaganda operation could claim to cooperate with specialized services, and whose public image was boosted across parts of Russian media was not operating from the margins in any ordinary sense. Nor was it fully sovereign. It occupied a zone of licensed distinctiveness. That distinction is subtle, but essential. The Russian state did not need to reduce every useful irregular formation to a faceless unit number. Sometimes it was more advantageous to let a formation keep its name, myth, and founder aura—so long as the deeper channels of money, contracting, and political protection remained above it.
This arrangement had operational benefits. It allowed the state to draw on a motivated pool of fighters tied to football-hooligan and far-right milieus without having to mass-mobilize politically risky constituencies directly. It allowed new technical and tactical experiments to happen in semi-detached formations. It also distributed the burdens of war in ways that were psychologically and politically useful: men joined a brotherhood, not a bureaucracy; patrons backed a brigade, not the entire war machine; public audiences followed charismatic units rather than abstract military districts. But the arrangement also had a limit.
That limit was loyalty.
A formation like Española derived part of its power from horizontal bonds—to its own commander, its own symbols, its own martyr narratives, its own media world. States can use such bonds. They do not love them. The same structure that makes a brigade effective in murky wartime conditions can make it dangerous if its internal loyalty begins to rival vertical obedience. Wagner had shown how destabilizing that could become. Smaller formations like Española were therefore valuable precisely so long as they remained intense without becoming truly autonomous, visible without becoming sovereign, admired without becoming alternative centers of power. Redut, elite patrons, military-intelligence visibility, and contractual dependency all helped police that boundary.
Seen this way, the brigade’s organizational history becomes less mysterious. Española was not outside the Russian state, nor simply inside it. It was one of the devices through which the state extended itself into zones where official hierarchy alone was insufficient: the hooligan scene, the ultranationalist milieu, the volunteer economy, the media spectacle of patriotic war. Its chain of command was not a single chain at all, but a braided one. That is why it could look paradoxical from the outside—part football firm, part drone brigade, part humanitarian foundation, part PMC, part GRU-adjacent asset. The paradox dissolves once one sees the system that produced it.
In the next chapter, that system begins to tighten. We move from managed autonomy to managed consolidation: how the Russian state, especially after Wagner’s revolt, began to squeeze semi-autonomous volunteer and PMC structures more aggressively—and why formations like Española, useful as they were, were never likely to be left entirely alone forever.
