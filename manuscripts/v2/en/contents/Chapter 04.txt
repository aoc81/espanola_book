Chapter 4 — The War as Performance
How Española learned to sell itself
Most armed formations seek recognition. Few understand as quickly as Española did that recognition is not something war simply grants. It has to be built, edited, repeated, and staged. On the battlefield, a unit may earn a reputation among those who serve beside it. But outside the battlefield—in the hinterland, in the donor class, in fan culture, in official media, in the eyes of potential recruits—reputation is manufactured through narrative. That was one of Española’s decisive advantages. It did not wait to be described. It described itself first.
By the time the brigade had begun to consolidate as a recognizable formation, it was already building a public image far more elaborate than the average volunteer detachment. Analysts and investigative outlets repeatedly noted the strength of its online propaganda apparatus, especially on Telegram, where official and affiliated channels pushed video, iconography, statements from commanders, and highly stylized representations of the unit’s battlefield identity. Jamestown described Española as using a “robust online propaganda apparatus” alongside real-world domestic networks and humanitarian activity to boost its profile and grow its ranks. Molfar, reading the same pattern from the opposite side of the war, argued that an informational campaign was helping promote the “Española” brand across Russian media and television, perhaps even as part of a broader effort to elevate it within the wartime symbolic landscape. Those interpretations differ in tone, but not in substance. They point to the same reality: Española was not just fighting the war. It was producing a version of the war in which it occupied a central, legible place.
The word brand is not ornamental here. It is analytical. War brands are made from visual consistency, emotional tone, a cast of recognizable figures, and a claim to exceptionalism. Española had all four. It had a name derived from the alias of its founder, a pirate-inflected skull-and-crossbones visual vocabulary, a social-media ecosystem linking official channels to personalities like “Pitbull,” and a consistent narrative about what kind of formation it was: not anonymous infantry, not state bureaucracy, not expendable cannon fodder, but an elite brotherhood of men with history, conviction, and style. Even Telegram’s own mirrored presentation of the channel makes plain how much the unit leaned into this identity, describing itself as a union of veteran hooligans and prominently linking its official community to affiliated personalities and outreach contacts. That alone does not prove effectiveness, but it reveals intention. The brigade wanted to be encountered as a total world, not a mere unit.
This mattered because propaganda, in Española’s case, was not simply persuasion aimed outward. It was also an internal technology of cohesion. Violent subcultures already understand the power of symbols, nicknames, banners, coded language, and enemy images. War gave those familiar tools a larger stage. What had once been terrace theater became military theater. The chants were replaced by statements, the banners by insignia, the ritual confrontation by curated battlefield clips, but the underlying grammar remained recognizable. The unit’s media did not only inform. It intensified. It transformed belonging into spectacle.
At the center of that spectacle stood people, not abstractions. And among them, few mattered more than Mikhail “Pitbull” Turkanov. Jamestown’s profile of Turkanov captures the logic of his role more clearly than any neutral military description could. Turkanov was not only a commander. He was a propaganda figure: a former MMA fighter, a Zenit hooligan, a man with visible neo-Nazi-associated tattoos and a cultivated aura of combat credibility. Jamestown argues that figures like him could appeal to highly nationalistic football hooligans who might otherwise remain skeptical of Moscow, thereby offering the Kremlin a route into ultra milieus it did not fully control. Whether one accepts that explanation in full, the broader point stands. Turkanov functioned as a translator between worlds. He was legible to the hooligan scene, to militarized patriots, and to audiences who needed a hero-face for the brigade. His online presence, his combat wounds, and his decoration all helped turn him into an instrument of recruitment and myth.
This was one of Española’s clearest departures from older paramilitary anonymity. Traditional clandestine or semi-deniable formations often obscured individual identities where possible. Española, by contrast, seemed to understand that public war in the age of Telegram rewards selective visibility. Not every fighter needed to be known. But enough did. The organization required faces with biographies, injuries, callsigns, and emotional charge. A faceless unit can be feared. A unit with recognizable protagonists can be followed. That distinction matters in the political economy of war because followers become donors, imitators, sympathizers, and recruits.
The recruitment dimension is especially revealing. Molfar’s intelligence-style file states that unlike Wagner, which used a broader and more centralized recruitment apparatus, Española recruited heavily through Telegram while also maintaining presences on VK and YouTube. Potential applicants reportedly underwent interviews through these channels before being sent for training. Even if every procedural detail cannot be independently verified here, the pattern fits what other reporting suggests: recruitment was not hidden in the shadows. It was integrated into the media ecosystem. The channel was not only a bulletin board. It was a funnel. The same platform that distributed image and myth also processed entry into the organization. That is an important shift. It means propaganda and human resources were not separate functions. They were fused.
That fusion helps explain why the brigade’s public messaging could feel so layered. One register was martial and technical: reconnaissance, drones, electronic warfare, battlefield competence, specialist roles. Another was emotional and tribal: brotherhood, sacrifice, fan solidarity, loyalty to fallen comrades. A third was civic or quasi-humanitarian: aid drives, sports outreach, social support, public initiatives in occupied territory. A fourth was ideological: nationalist rhetoric, anti-Azov positioning, the claim to embody an authentic patriotic counterforce against the enemy. All four registers appeared, in different combinations, across the source base. They were not accidental. They allowed Española to address multiple audiences without changing its essential identity. The donor could see duty. The recruit could see adventure and belonging. The media consumer could see heroism. The state could see usefulness.
The children’s outreach material is especially important here, precisely because it shows how far the brigade’s narrative ambition extended beyond the trench. Meduza’s summary of Cherta’s reporting noted that Española’s humanitarian wing—particularly Ilya Khanin and Alexey Trifonov—helped create a boys’ football team in Horlivka named after the brigade and modeled around its pirate mascot and iconography. That episode is easy to dismiss as grotesque local color. It is more than that. It shows that the brigade was not only trying to persuade adults. It was trying to naturalize itself inside everyday life. A skull-and-crossbones brand that began in hooligan violence and matured in war was being repackaged as youth culture and community symbolism. This was not just propaganda in the narrow informational sense. It was social implantation.
What makes this particularly striking is that Española’s self-mythologizing did not rely on denial of its origins. Quite the opposite. The football-hooligan roots were often part of the pitch. Jamestown’s March 2024 profile explicitly discusses how soccer was central to the group’s identity and how sports culture could be used as a recruiting reservoir. Molfar likewise describes the formation as increasingly visible in Russian media precisely as a brigade of football ultras, some with neo-Nazi views. Instead of hiding this past, the brigade weaponized it—selectively. It did not foreground the ugliest aspects in every context, of course. But the ultra pedigree itself became proof of authenticity. These were not polished state propagandists pretending to be rough men. They were rough men whose roughness had been made into propaganda.
That authenticity, however, was carefully managed. The brigade’s public image was raw, but not random. It was militant, but not wholly incoherent. The pirate emblem, for example, carried useful ambiguity. It suggested rebellion, brotherhood, danger, and masculine freedom without being reducible to the formal symbols of the Russian state. That made it flexible. The unit could remain culturally distinct while still serving a state war effort. In effect, the emblem did the same kind of work the organization as a whole was doing: it offered deniable distance without real independence.
There is a temptation to treat such branding as superficial, a kind of fascistic cosplay layered over more serious military realities. That would be a mistake. In formations like Española, style is part of military function. Style attracts men. Style creates retention. Style distinguishes one armed group from another in a crowded ecosystem of volunteer battalions, PMCs, and state-adjacent units. Style even affects access. A formation that captures attention can win coverage, influence, and patronage that a more anonymous one cannot. This is why Molfar’s suggestion that the promotion of the “Española” brand may have served to counterbalance Akhmat or even fill some of Wagner’s old media space is worth taking seriously as an analytical possibility, even if it cannot be conclusively proved. In wartime Russia, visibility is not an accident. It is allocated, negotiated, or seized. Española managed to obtain a share of it.
The media ecology around the brigade also had a second-order effect: it made conflict itself portable. A man did not need to join Española to enter its universe. He could follow it, donate to it, repost it, identify with it, learn its callsigns, echo its grievances, and treat its fortunes as part of his own political-emotional life. That is what war brands do when they succeed. They expand the conflict beyond the battlefield into a dispersed spectator-participant community. In that sense, the Telegram channel was not simply a record of the brigade. It was one of the places where the brigade existed most fully.
And like all successful wartime narratives, Española’s propaganda depended on contrast. It contrasted itself with bureaucratic lethargy, with unmotivated regular troops, with the facelessness of the state, with the moral ambiguity of mercenaryism, even with rival patriotic formations. It contrasted itself, too, with Azov—not only in military terms but in symbolic terms, as if the war were also a struggle between rival models of subcultural militarization. Jamestown’s profile of Turkanov underscores this broader role of Española within the Russian far-right military ecosystem: distinct from Rusich and the Russian Imperial Movement, yet supportive of that world and useful in mobilizing ultra-nationalist communities. That positioning gave the brigade strategic flexibility. It could be extreme enough to attract militants, but still absorbable enough to remain useful to the state.
The more the brand grew, the more it became a political object. Once a brigade is visible across official Russian media, discussed on Telegram, featured through wounded heroes, framed as technologically adept, and embedded in domestic outreach and symbolic life, it stops being merely a tactical unit. It becomes a claim: about who counts as patriotic, about which forms of masculinity are useful to the war, about how the state can metabolize radical subcultures without fully sanitizing them. This is why propaganda matters so much in the Española story. It is not secondary to the brigade’s history. It is one of the main ways the brigade became historically significant at all.
But performance has a cost. The more a unit turns itself into a brand, the more it becomes legible—not only to admirers, but to rivals, investigators, and the state itself. Visibility brings recruits and patrons, but it also creates expectations, enemies, and vulnerability. A media-savvy formation cannot remain just a brotherhood in the shadows. It becomes a public actor, and public actors eventually collide with other centers of power.
That collision is where the story goes next. In the following chapter, we move from image to battlefield use: how Española’s propaganda claims about competence, technological innovation, and combat effectiveness intersected with the actual military role the brigade played inside Russia’s war, and how battlefield reputation helped sustain everything built around it.
