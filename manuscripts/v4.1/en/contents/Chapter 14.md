# Chapter 14 — A Private Army, but Not Quite

The phrase "private army" traveled far in Russian wartime discourse, and it traveled fast. After the Wagner Group demonstrated that a formation with its own name, its own leader, its own symbols, and its own political ambitions could be simultaneously useful and terrifying to the state that had enabled it, every formation in the wider Russian irregular ecosystem acquired a new frame of reference. Española was described as a private army — by Ukrainian intelligence, which called it United Russia's own private militia; by international media, which reached for the label partly because Wagner had made it legible; and occasionally by its own supporters, who used it as a badge of distinction rather than a legal description.[^ch14-001] [^ch14-007]

Each of those descriptions captured something real and missed something equally real. The miss matters, because the label "private army" implies a degree of operational sovereignty that the available evidence does not support for Española. A truly private army commands its own logistics, controls its own legal status, determines its own deployments, and maintains a chain of authority that does not ultimately answer to a state structure. Wagner, at its peak under Yevgeny Prigozhin, came close to that model — close enough that the challenge it posed to Putin in June 2023 was treated as a genuine threat. Española was not that. According to the reporting available, it was not even close to that.[^ch14-003]

What the available record does support is something more interesting than either the "private army" claim or the flat denial of it. Española was a hybrid formation: genuine in its internal culture, real in its lateral loyalties, authentic in its symbols and its founder mythology, but dependent in its administrative infrastructure, its contracting, its legal cover, and, if investigative reporting is correct, its higher-level patronage. The fighters could plausibly feel they were inside a chosen world. The machinery above them ran through structures they did not control. Those two things coexisted because they were designed to.[^ch14-005]

The coexistence had a strategic logic. A formation that is experienced by its members as chosen, horizontal, and self-directed generates a kind of loyalty and intensity that can't be produced by decree. Men who have opted into a fraternity feel differently about it than men who have been posted to a unit. The emotional charge of subcultural belonging — the sense that these men chose each other, that the symbols mean something, that the founder is a real person you could, in principle, drink with — produces cohesion that standard military structures must work hard to simulate. Española did not need to simulate it. It had it naturally, as an extension of the terrace culture that had built its membership.

That emotional charge was precisely the resource that Redut-linked contracting, higher-level patronage, and administrative embedding were designed to borrow and make useful. The state — or the interests above the brigade that sat adjacent to the state — did not need to manufacture loyalty. It needed to capture loyalty that already existed and route it toward ends it could use. The arrangement worked because each side was getting something real. The brigade got the freedom to be itself: rough, coded, subcultural, founder-centered. The structures above it got recruitable men, persuadable donors, and a propaganda object with genuine emotional intensity.

Ukrainian intelligence's characterization of Española as United Russia's private militia, while overstated as a precise description, was not entirely wrong as a characterization of political function. A formation with that level of visibility, that kind of donor base, and that density of nationalist and hooligan-adjacent membership was useful to the Russian political economy of war in ways that exceeded its military contribution. It demonstrated that the Russian nationalist milieu was fighting, not merely talking. It generated media content that official outlets could amplify. It created a community of supporters with emotional investment in the war's continuation. Whatever the exact patronage relationship, that function was political as much as military.[^ch14-007]

The comparison with Wagner clarifies the limits as much as the similarities. Wagner accumulated, over years, a genuine institutional sovereignty: its own financing, its own recruitment apparatus independent of the state, its own political relationships, its own foreign deployments. Española had none of that at the same scale. It had a strong brand and a loyal following, but its fighters signed contracts through Redut, its patronage was linked to state-adjacent networks, and its administrative label shifted over time in ways that showed increasing rather than decreasing dependence. Its branded autonomy was real as an experience and as a marketing claim. It was not real as an operational fact.[^ch14-004]

After June 2023, that distinction began to matter in a new way. Wagner's mutiny had shown what happened when a branded irregular formation became too autonomous in reality rather than merely in presentation. The consequences of that episode narrowed the tolerance for similar ambiguity across the entire ecosystem. Española, which had been thriving in the space between genuine independence and administrative dependence, found that space beginning to contract. The licenses that made it useful were being scrutinized. The very traits that had made it recruitable and memorable — the horizontal loyalty, the founder mythology, the sense that it belonged partly to itself — were acquiring, in the changed political environment, a different valence. They were beginning to look less like assets and more like risks.
