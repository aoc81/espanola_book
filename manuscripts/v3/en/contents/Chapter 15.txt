Chapter 15 — After Wagner: Why the Kremlin Needed Units Like This

The mutiny by Yevgeny Prigozhin changed the meaning of formations like Espanola even if they had no intention of repeating Wagner's path. Before June 2023, the Russian state could tolerate a wide band of ambiguity among PMCs, volunteer formations, and branded semi-regular units. After Wagner marched on Moscow, ambiguity itself became a problem to manage more carefully. Yet the state did not stop needing irregular force. It still needed manpower, initiative, and identities strong enough to recruit men the bureaucracy could not easily inspire.

That is the paradox of the post-Wagner landscape. The Kremlin had learned that charismatic and semi-autonomous war brands were dangerous. It had also learned how useful they could be. Smaller formations like Espanola therefore became attractive precisely because they seemed to offer some of Wagner's advantages without, at first glance, posing the same scale of threat. They had recognizable faces, a devoted following, and enough subcultural authenticity to mobilize hard-edged constituencies. But through Redut and adjacent structures, they also appeared more governable.

This helps explain why later analysis so often places Espanola inside a broader effort to reorganize and domesticate the irregular field after Wagner. The state no longer wanted force centered purely on a warlord's personal empire. It wanted formations that could retain zeal and style while remaining braided into military intelligence, ministry proxies, and elite patronage networks. In that sense, Espanola's value increased at the very moment the tolerance for true autonomy narrowed.

The comparison with Akhmat is useful here, though only up to a point. Akhmat occupied one mode of wartime branding: politically protected, regionally rooted, and heavily mediatised. Espanola occupied another: football-derived, urban, rougher at the edges, and appealing to parts of the nationalist and hooligan milieu that the state could not simply conjure administratively. Multiple brands served the center well because they divided symbolic capital and reduced the chance that one formation alone would monopolize militant prestige.

The post-Wagner moment also widened the political value of governable radicalism. Men shaped by supporter violence, extremist adjacency, and digital war culture could be useful if their energy ran upward into the state project rather than sideways into rebellion. Formations like Espanola became ways to capture that energy. They offered a route for mobilizing violent subcultures while packaging them through contracts, propaganda, and patronage into something more legible than a raw street movement.

This does not mean the Kremlin designed every detail from above. The evidence is too messy for a theory of total orchestration. But it does support a more modest and still important claim: after Wagner, the Russian state had strong incentives to preserve irregular capability while shrinking the room for uncontrolled personal sovereignty. Espanola fit that demand unusually well. It was branded, intense, and publicly alive, yet still tied to structures the state could influence.

That was why the formation remained useful. It was also why its later visibility became riskier. The more clearly a brigade demonstrated that it could recruit, fight, fundraise, and narrate itself as a world of its own, the more the post-Wagner state had reason to ask whether utility was beginning to slide into autonomy.

The next stage of the story begins when visibility stops being only an asset. A formation that turns itself into a public object becomes legible not only to admirers and donors, but to rivals, investigators, and the state apparatus that once profited from its blur.
