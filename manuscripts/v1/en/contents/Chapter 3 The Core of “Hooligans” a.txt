Chapter 3: The Core of “Hooligans” and “Ultras”
The social alchemy that transforms football rivalries into military cohesion

Within the ecosystem of irregular military formations that have proliferated during the Russian-Ukrainian conflict, few exhibit a composition as specific and culturally distinctive as Española. Its recruitment base—hooligans and ultras from Russian football clubs—represents a fascinating sociological experiment: the conversion of violent tribal identities, traditionally focused on sports rivalries, into unified military objectives.
The World of Russian Hooliganism
To understand the uniqueness of Española’s composition, it is essential to first examine the context of Russian football hooliganism, a phenomenon that has evolved considerably since its origins in the 1990s. According to a 2020 study by the Higher School of Economics, Russian football fans can be classified into four distinct categories: okolofutbolshchiki (quasi-footballers), ultras, true hooligans, and ordinary fans (kuzmichi).
It is from the first three groups—and particularly from the hooligans and ultras—that Española has drawn its most effective personnel. These individuals come to the formation with pre-existing skills that are highly transferable to the military context: group coordination under pressure, familiarity with organized violence, intense tribal loyalty, and experience in complex logistical operations needed to mobilize large groups of people.
Russian hooligan culture, which began to develop a far-right political inclination from the mid-1990s, had been the subject of multiple state co-optation attempts. From Vladimir Zhirinovsky’s efforts in the late 90s, to the use of hooligans as security guards by the Nashi youth movement in the 2000s, to the alleged creation of the All-Russian Football Fans’ Association by order of the FSB, Russian authorities have long recognized the political potential of these organized subcultures.
The Great Unification: Surpassing Centuries-Old Rivalries
Perhaps Stanislav Orlov’s most extraordinary achievement in building Española has been his ability to unify hooligans from traditionally antagonistic clubs under a common cause. The brigade’s composition reads like a who’s-who of Russian football hooliganism, including followers from:
●	CSKA Moscow: Orlov’s home club, providing the leadership core
●	Spartak Moscow: CSKA’s eternal rival, whose integration represented a significant organizational coup
●	Torpedo Moscow: Contributing urban operations experience
●	Zenit Saint Petersburg: Providing resources and connections in Russia’s second largest city
●	Lokomotiv Moscow: Adding specialized logistical capabilities
●	Dinamo Moscow: Providing links to state security structures
●	Regional clubs: Including Orel, Ural (Yekaterinburg), Shinnik (Yaroslavl), Rostov, Nizhny Novgorod, Kuban (Krasnodar), Saturn (Ramensky District)
●	Clubs from occupied territories: Such as Shakhtar Donetsk, Tavriya (Simferopol), and Illichivets (Mariupol)
This unification transcends mere numbers. Rivalries among these hooligan factions had resulted in decades of organized violence, with clashes occasionally escalating to levels requiring massive police intervention. The December 2010 riots in Manezhnaya Square, where hooligans from multiple clubs participated in coordinated xenophobic violence, demonstrated both the destructive potential and organizational capacity of these groups.
Orlov’s ability to channel these previously antagonistic energies toward a common goal represents an innovation in organizational management that goes beyond purely military considerations. As one analyst noted in 2023, “what we are seeing is essentially the application of nation-building principles at a micro level, where competitive local identities are subordinated to a broader imperial narrative.”
Fighter Profiles: The Faces of Española
Mikhail “Pitbull” Turkanov: The Controversial Icon
No figure better embodies the inherent contradictions of Española than Mikhail “Pitbull” Turkanov, the former professional mixed martial arts fighter who serves as the formation’s anti-aircraft battery commander. Turkanov represents both the assets and liabilities of the brigade’s recruitment strategy.
On the one hand, Turkanov brings genuine combat credentials, experience in leadership under pressure, and an imposing physical presence that helps maintain discipline among fighters from inherently violent cultures. His prominence in the brigade’s promotional material—frequently appearing in training videos and awards ceremonies—suggests that military authorities value these contributions.
On the other hand, Turkanov is also the most visible example of the extremist ideological tendencies that permeate the formation. His swastika tattoos, widely documented on social networks, exemplify the neonazi imagery that complicates the official Russian narrative of “denazification” in Ukraine. However, far from being marginalized for these associations, Turkanov has been decorated for his combat actions, indicating that operational effectiveness outweighs ideological considerations in the brigade’s priorities.
Andrey Solomatin: The Bridge to Legitimacy
The incorporation of Andrey Solomatin into Española in August 2023 marked a crucial moment in the evolution of the formation’s public image. As a former player for the Russian national team who had played for prestigious clubs such as CSKA Moscow and Lokomotiv, Solomatin brought a level of sporting credibility unmatched by any other member.
Solomatin’s decision to adopt the military nickname “Soloma” and join operations near Bakhmut was not merely symbolic. His presence provided the brigade with a recognizable face that could appeal to audiences broader than the traditional extremist subcultures. In terms of public relations, Solomatin represented the possibility that “normal” citizens could identify with the brigade’s cause.
More significantly, Solomatin’s integration illustrated the brigade’s capacity to attract individuals from diverse backgrounds, expanding its recruitment base beyond hardcore hooligans into more mainstream elements of Russian sports.
Vyacheslav Subbotin: The Price of Leadership
The figure of Vyacheslav Subbotin, who served as head of the Reconnaissance Division until his death in combat in March 2024, exemplifies both the capabilities and vulnerabilities of Española’s leadership structure. With the military nickname “Vykhodnoy,” Subbotin represented the type of mid-level commander that allowed the brigade to maintain operational effectiveness as it grew in size and complexity.
Subbotin’s death attracted extra media attention precisely because he embodied the formation’s ideological contradictions. As a self-declared neo-Nazi, his leadership in reconnaissance operations underscored the centrality of extremist elements in the brigade’s operational capabilities. His loss represented not only a tactical blow, but also the elimination of a crucial link between the formation’s base extremist culture and its professional military aspirations.
The Psychology of Conversion
Transforming football hooligans into effective military fighters requires more than simply redirecting preexisting aggression. Individuals joining Española must adapt skills developed for short-term urban clashes to sustained military operations that can last weeks or months.
This adaptation is facilitated by several structural factors inherent to hooligan culture. First, experience in complex logistical planning—necessary to coordinate the movements of large groups to distant stadiums while evading police surveillance—translates directly to reconnaissance and tactical movement capabilities.
Second, familiarity with informal hierarchies based on respect earned through demonstrations of physical courage provides leadership frameworks that work effectively in combat environments where formal military structures may be inadequate.
Third, experience in maintaining group cohesion in the face of external threats—whether police, rival hooligans, or sports authorities—develops exactly the kind of “us against the world” mentality that is crucial for combat effectiveness.
Internal Dynamics: Rivalry and Cooperation
Despite the apparent unification under Española banner, the brigade’s internal dynamics reflect residual tensions between previously antagonistic factions. Reports from internal sources suggest that the informal organization retains some segregation based on original club affiliations, with units tending to group themselves by football origins.
This segregation does not necessarily compromise operational effectiveness. In fact, internal competition between groups can be channeled toward productive military objectives, with different factions competing for recognition based on combat performance rather than interfactional violence.
However, these tensions also create potential vulnerabilities. Changes in leadership, unequal distribution of resources, or operational failures could reactivate latent antagonisms, threatening the organizational cohesion that constitutes one of the brigade’s main strengths.
The Evolution of Recruitment
Española’s initial composition, dominated by Donbas conflict veterans with hooligan backgrounds, has gradually evolved into a more diverse profile that reflects both changing operational needs and expanded recruitment opportunities.
The decision to begin recruiting women in November 2023 represented a significant departure from the traditionally overwhelmingly masculine norms of football hooliganism. The incorporation of women into roles that include snipers, drone operators, electronic warfare specialists, and even assault units, indicates an organizational sophistication that transcends purely cultural considerations.
This evolution also reflects the practical realities of prolonged conflict. As the brigade’s operations have become more technically complex—particularly in areas such as electronic warfare and drone operations—the need for personnel with specialized skills has outweighed concerns about cultural purity or conformity to the traditional norms of hooliganism.
The Future of Hooligan Identity
The fundamental question facing Española is whether it can maintain its distinctive cultural identity while evolving into a more conventional military formation. Integration into the Ministry of Defense’s Volunteer Corps, with its requirements for standardization and conformity to formal military procedures, threatens to dilute precisely those characteristics that make the formation effective.
The tension between cultural authenticity and military effectiveness represents a challenge that extends beyond Española to other formations seeking to militarize distinctive subcultures. The resolution of this tension will determine not only the brigade’s future, but also the viability of the broader model of mobilizing marginal subcultures for state geopolitical objectives.
