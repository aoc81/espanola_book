# Chapter 15 — After Wagner: Why the Kremlin Needed Units Like This

On 23 June 2023, columns of Wagner Group vehicles moved north from their staging areas in southern Russia toward Moscow. The advance stopped after approximately 24 hours, with the Wagner commander Yevgeny Prigozhin turning his forces around following a reported negotiation brokered by the Belarusian president. Two months later, Prigozhin was dead in a plane crash near Tver, along with other senior Wagner figures. The official explanation for the crash was never given. The political reading was not difficult. Russia's most powerful private military company — the one that had recruited from prisons, deployed across Africa and Syria, fought and won at Bakhmut, and then dared to make an armed march toward the Kremlin — had been made to disappear.[^ch15-001]

The implications for formations like Española were immediate, and they were not simple. The obvious reading — that the state would now liquidate all irregular formations and return to conventional military organization — was not what happened. What happened was subtler and in some ways more revealing. The state did not stop needing the things that irregular formations provided. It became far more attentive to the conditions under which it would continue tolerating them.

What Wagner's mutiny demonstrated, in the starkest possible terms, was the danger that resided inside a formation with genuine operational sovereignty. Prigozhin had built, over years, an organization that had its own financing, its own recruitment networks, its own media apparatus, its own international deployments, its own political relationships, and — most dangerously — its own identity. Wagner was not just Prigozhin's vehicle. It was a community with loyalties that ran to him personally rather than to Putin or to the structures around Putin. When the center's relationship with Prigozhin soured, that loyalty structure became a military threat.[^ch15-002]

The lesson was not that irregular force was too dangerous to use. It was that irregular force without reliable mechanisms of state control was too dangerous to leave. A formation that could recruit zealously, sustain itself financially, motivate its members through subcultural loyalty, and operate in conditions that conventional forces could not manage was enormously valuable. A formation that had accumulated all of those qualities without adequate structures of supervision, and whose loyalty ran to a personal patron rather than to the state, was eventually going to present a problem.

Española fitted the post-Wagner requirement precisely because it had most of the benefits and fewer of the risks. It had a strong founder myth, a devoted following, visible specialists, and subcultural authenticity that no bureaucratic military structure could manufacture on demand. At the same time, reporting tied its fighters to Redut-linked contracting — a structure that gave the state administrative handles over the men inside the formation even when those men experienced themselves as free. By 2024, Russian state media was reporting that the formation had become an official testing ground for new Ministry of Defence weapons — a claim reported through TASS that, if accurate, placed the brigade's technical reputation in an explicitly institutional rather than merely informal register. A unit officially testing state weapons is not operating outside the system; it is being incorporated into it. That combination was exactly what the post-Wagner period required: irregular intensity inside a governable shell.[^ch15-002]

Later analytical reporting helped make the architecture more visible. Meduza's July 2025 summary of Sistema's investigation described how Dobrocor — the reorganized Redut system — was created explicitly to manage the mercenary pipeline in the wake of Wagner's implosion, linking it to GRU oversight and to General Vladimir Alekseev as the senior figure journalists identified in the structure. Whether every element of that account is precisely accurate is less important than the direction it reveals: after Wagner, the Russian intelligence apparatus was working to preserve irregular recruitment capacity while building mechanisms of supervision that Prigozhin's Wagner had never accepted.[^ch15-007]

The comparison with Akhmat is instructive for a different reason. The Akhmat force — named for Akhmat Kadyrov, the father of Chechnya's current leader, and effectively controlled by Ramzan Kadyrov — represented one managed model of wartime irregular militancy: regionally rooted, ideologically distinct, politically protected, and heavily mediated through a relationship between Kadyrov and the Kremlin that involved constant performance and constant negotiation. Española represented a different model: urban, football-derived, rougher at the cultural edges, and attractive to constituency groups — hooligan networks, nationalist milieus, far-right-adjacent communities — that the Chechen-based model could not reach. The state needed both. Different formations solved different recruitment and propaganda problems.[^ch15-004]

Española's value in this context was also about the men it attracted. A formation that could reach men who had been shaped by supporter violence, who came from the rougher edges of Russian urban society, who were motivated by a subcultural rather than a purely patriotic identity — and could channel all of that energy into the war — was solving a problem that conventional mobilization had struggled with. Men who would not have responded to a military conscription poster sometimes responded to the Española channel. In that sense, the brigade was doing work that the state needed done but could not do itself.

The contradiction sharpened with every success. Each successful recruitment campaign, each well-publicized battlefield claim, each drone appeal that raised money from ordinary sympathizers added to the formation's symbolic weight. A unit that could do all of these things was more than manpower. It was a center of meaning, a community of investment, a political constituency with its own emotional climate. After Wagner, that is precisely what the state had learned to be afraid of. The very intensity that made Española useful was the intensity that, at some threshold, began to look like a separate power base. And in Russia's post-Wagner political landscape, separate power bases were no longer being tolerated.
