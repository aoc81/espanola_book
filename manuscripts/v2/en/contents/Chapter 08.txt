Chapter 8 — The Reassertion of Control
After Wagner, the state moves to close the circle
The system that produced formations like Española was always double-edged. On one side, it gave the Russian war effort something the regular state often struggles to generate on its own: zeal, identity, initiative, deniable aggression, and access to violent subcultures that do not naturally emerge from military bureaucracy. On the other, it created armed entities with their own symbols, their own loyalties, their own patronage networks, and above all their own charismatic centers of gravity. For a while, that contradiction could be managed. Then Wagner marched on Moscow.
After Prigozhin’s mutiny in June 2023, the entire political meaning of semi-autonomous Russian war formations changed. The question was no longer only whether such groups were useful. The question was whether they were governable. Later analytical writing on both Redut and Española treats this moment as the decisive turn: the point at which the Kremlin and the security apparatus intensified efforts to pull volunteer units, PMCs, and mercenary brands into tighter lines of control. Jamestown’s January 2026 essay on the elimination of PMCs and semi-autonomous volunteer units frames Española’s later disbandment and the killing of Orlov as part of exactly that post-mutiny process. A second Jamestown piece from March 2026 sharpens the same argument, describing how Vladimir Alekseev helped place groups like Redut, Storm Z, and formations such as Española under GRU coordination after Wagner’s liquidation. Even adversarial analytical material like Molfar, working from a different angle, reaches a similar conclusion: the state’s linkages to Redut, GRU oversight, and oligarchic financing made formations like Española valuable precisely because they could be controlled before they became another Wagner.
This is the chapter where the illusion of stable hybridity starts to break.
Before Wagner’s revolt, the Russian state could tolerate a certain amount of ambiguity. A formation could call itself a PMC, cultivate its own mythology, and recruit through subcultural networks so long as it still fed manpower and battlefield value into the war. After June 2023, ambiguity itself became suspect. A unit centered on a leader, tied together by horizontal loyalty, reinforced by media and patronage, and partially insulated from regular military culture no longer looked merely useful. It looked like a potential problem waiting for a moment of instability. That is why the post-Wagner story is not simply one of repression. It is one of administrative recapture: the effort to preserve the battlefield utility of these units while stripping them of the conditions that could turn them into alternative centers of power.
The evidence for this tightening appears first in the broader handling of Redut and adjacent formations. Jamestown’s March 2026 piece states that after Wagner’s liquidation, Alekseev played a key role in placing Redut and numerous mercenary and volunteer formations under GRU control, a move that caused tension with other law-enforcement agencies. The article goes on to say that one of Alekseev’s “projects” was Española itself. That wording is revealing. It treats the brigade not as an organically sovereign unit that happened to overlap with state structures, but as one node inside a managed portfolio of force. Once you see it this way, the larger logic becomes harder to ignore. The war was creating charismatic, branded, semi-detached armed bodies. The security state’s answer was not to abolish them immediately, but to map them, subordinate them, and prepare to absorb or dissolve them if necessary.
The process can also be traced through smaller administrative clues. The continued use of Redut contracts for recruitment—even for women entering Española’s assault and technical roles—shows that the brigade’s growth remained tethered to a higher proxy structure rather than to its own sovereign contracting base. Ukrainska Pravda’s November 2023 report, citing Vazhnye Istorii, makes clear that the contract was signed with Redut and that this arrangement created a special legal space outside normal Ministry of Defense rules about indefinite contract duration. That is a telling contradiction: the same mechanism that gave units like Española attractive flexibility also made them easier to channel. Flexibility below was enabled by architecture above. What looked like freedom could be switched, narrowed, or withdrawn from elsewhere.
At first, this may have seemed like an advantage for the brigade. It could continue to market itself as distinct while remaining sheltered inside a system capable of protecting and feeding it. But that kind of arrangement only works as long as the state perceives the brand as useful and loyal. The more visible Española became—through battlefield reputation, media operations, humanitarian and donor networks, and the growing public importance of Orlov himself—the more it accumulated the very attributes the Kremlin had reason to fear after Wagner: a recognizable leader, a closed internal ethic, a loyal constituency, and a degree of symbolic autonomy.
Jamestown’s January 2026 essay makes the parallel explicit. It argues that the disbandment of Española and the killing of Orlov represent a logical continuation of the process that began after Prigozhin’s mutiny, and stresses that both Prigozhin and Orlov were not just commanders but the primary brands of their formations. Without them, the structures became far easier to dissolve. This is a sharper observation than it first appears. It suggests that the Kremlin’s problem with formations like Wagner and Española was not merely that they possessed fighters. Many forces did. The problem was that they possessed narrative coherence around a man. A brigade with its own mythology and its own charismatic nucleus can become something more than an instrument. It can become a political actor.
That helps explain why Española’s later disbandment, as described in Jamestown and echoed elsewhere, should not be read as an isolated collapse or as the accidental death spiral of one brigade. According to the March 2026 Jamestown article, Española was “forced to self-dissolve under pressure from above” in the autumn of 2025. The wording matters: not disintegrated on its own, not naturally exhausted, but pushed toward dissolution. A few months later, according to the same article and the January 2026 Jamestown piece, Orlov was killed during his arrest. Current Time and ASTRA reporting from December 2025 sit behind these later analytical claims, with Novaya Gazeta Europe also framing Orlov’s death as the end of the line not only for the man but for the already-dismantled formation he had once led.
The exact mechanics of this end remain murky, and that murkiness is part of the story. Novaya Gazeta Europe’s December 2025 piece describes Orlov’s death as surrounded by dispute and rumor, including allegations tied to an illicit weapons trade. ASTRA, relayed by Current Time, reported that he was likely killed during an attempted arrest. Wikipedia’s late 2025 article text, drawing on Telegram reporting, reflects that same confusion: detention, possible shooting, uncertainty. Jamestown’s January 2026 analysis emphasizes the “high level of informational uncertainty” around both Prigozhin’s death and Orlov’s. This is not merely a matter of imperfect reporting. Informational ambiguity is one of the ways the Russian state and the ecosystems around it metabolize the fall of such figures. The message is plain enough—this man is gone, this brand is over—even if the official narrative remains blurred at the edges.
The role of Alekseev in this period makes the politics even more intricate. Jamestown’s March 2026 piece suggests that many pro-war circles viewed the liquidation of Española as a direct blow to Alekseev’s network, especially because the brigade had been one of his favored units and its members were reportedly personally loyal to him. If that reading is correct, then the dismantling of Española was not only part of a general anti-autonomy campaign. It was also entangled with internal power struggles among Russian силовики and military structures. In that interpretation, the crackdown on semi-autonomous units was not purely ideological or administrative. It was factional. Units were not only tools of war. They were assets in networks of influence. To force a brigade like Española to dissolve was also to weaken the men who had cultivated it.
This is where the war starts to look less like a military campaign and more like a contest over ownership of violence. Who gets to raise men? Who gets to brand them? Who gets to decide whether their symbolic capital belongs to the unit, to its commander, to its patrons, or to the state? In peacetime, these questions are easier to suppress. In wartime—especially after a mutiny—they become urgent. The Russian answer, by late 2025, appears to have been centralization. Jamestown’s January 2026 essay says so directly: the Kremlin had decided to dismantle and absorb military structures centered on charismatic leaders, seeking complete centralization and leaving no room for independent field commanders or units with their own ideologies that could pose a threat during internal instability. Even if one treats Jamestown’s analysis as interpretive rather than definitive, the logic is hard to dismiss. Everything in the arc of Wagner and Española points in that direction.
And yet the irony is unavoidable. The state was trying to abolish precisely the features it had once exploited. It had benefited from the intensity of hooligan and far-right militancy, from the charisma of men like Orlov, from the social-media power of brigades that felt alive in ways regular units often do not. It had allowed these formations to recruit through identity rather than mere payroll, through myth rather than discipline alone. It had let them become brands. Then, once those brands grew too legible, too networked, too centered on individual men, it moved to erase or absorb them. This is not a contradiction in Russian wartime governance. It is its rhythm: outsource violence, instrumentalize identity, then close the space once the outsourced force starts to resemble an actor.
The pattern is visible even in how Española announced itself and how later sources remembered it. The brigade’s own channels and public-facing material had worked hard to present a complete world—symbols, ethos, humanitarian wing, technical prowess, battlefield record, leader cult. By the time later analysts and sanctions trackers described the brigade, it had become something hard to classify neatly: part football movement, part Redut structure, part PMC claim, part oligarchic project, part GRU-adjacent volunteer unit. That classificatory confusion was never a bug. It was the very form of the thing. But it also meant that when the state decided to reclaim the monopoly, there was no clean institutional shell to preserve. There was the brand—and the man who embodied it. Once those were removed, the rest could be redistributed.
This helps explain why the chronology of Wagner and Española is so unsettlingly parallel. Jamestown points out the sequence: Wagner was dissolved in July 2023, and Prigozhin died a month later; Española announced its disbandment in early October 2025, and Orlov was dead by early December. The comparison is not exact—Prigozhin challenged the Kremlin openly, Orlov did not—but the structural lesson is similar. Once a brand-centered formation has crossed from utility into potential risk, the safest route is not reform. It is decapitation followed by absorption.
That does not mean the ecosystem vanished. Jamestown’s January 2026 essay notes that Akhmat remained one of the few structures with substantial autonomy. Redut itself did not disappear. Nor did the larger machinery of volunteer and mercenary formations. What changed was the tolerance for units organized too visibly around their own men and myths. The Kremlin was not eliminating irregular force as such. It was eliminating those versions of irregular force that retained too much narrative and personal sovereignty.
For Española, this was the end of the bargain. The state had tolerated its hooligan pedigree, its far-right adjacency, its donor ecosystem, its humanitarian mask, its Telegram mythology, and even its claims of internal independence so long as those features amplified the war and remained containable. After Wagner, the tolerance narrowed. By late 2025, what had once been a useful experiment in controlled militancy was treated as unfinished business.
That is the significance of this chapter. The story of Española is not only the story of how a violent subculture was militarized. It is also the story of how the state that used it eventually tried to take it back whole.
