# Chapter 8 — Who Paid for the Brigade?

Start with the material question. A wartime formation of the scale and ambition Española achieved does not run on enthusiasm. Drones cost money. Communications gear costs money. Transport, modified vehicles, specialist equipment, ammunition, and body armor cost money. So does the media operation: the cameras, the editing, the bandwidth, the moderation of channels with tens of thousands of followers. So does the humanitarian wing: the supplies, the logistics, the salaries of organizers. And behind all of that, somewhere, are the fighters' wages, the compensation for the injured, the payments to the families of the dead. The financial question is not a detail. It is the question that explains what kind of organization Española actually was.[^ch08-001]

At the lower level, the answer was visible in public. The brigade's Telegram channels and affiliated platforms ran fundraising appeals that were targeted, specific, and morally framed. They did not ask for contributions to a general war effort. They asked for a particular drone for particular men, for a particular vehicle to supply a particular unit, for medicine needed at a specific position. The granularity was intentional. It converted the act of donating into a personal relationship between the sympathizer and the formation: you did not give money to the Russian war machine, you helped Española, these men, this cause, this named brotherhood.[^ch08-002]

That personalization was one of the brigade's most effective financial innovations. The supporter milieu had long understood the power of group identity to create financial obligations. Men from the terrace donated to their firm, covered bail for arrested comrades, chipped in for medical bills and lawyers. The same logic translated directly to wartime fundraising, with the important addition that the stakes were higher and the emotional charge correspondingly greater. Dedicated channels, named fundraising accounts, and the visible gratitude of men at the front made the system work at a scale the terrace had never required.[^ch08-002]

The harder question sits above that level. Independent investigative reporting from iStories, published in March 2024 and summarized by Meduza, identified a patronage environment that extended well beyond Telegram donations. According to that reporting, the brigade's higher-level support was connected to Viktor Shendrik — the head of security at Russian Railways, described in the investigation as a former Vympel officer and a protégé of the Rotenberg network — and through Shendrik to the orbit of Arkady and Boris Rotenberg, brothers whose business interests, political relationships, and proximity to Vladimir Putin have made them central figures in reporting on the financial architecture of Putinist Russia.[^ch08-003]

That chain of connection requires careful attribution at every step. The available source base does not justify a flat claim that the Rotenberg network directed, controlled, or decisively financed Española. What the investigative reporting does justify is the narrower but still significant statement that journalists linked the brigade's higher patronage environment to networks operating at that level of the Russian political economy — and that the formation was one instance of a broader wartime tendency in which powerful Russian actors sought influence, proximity, and leverage through semi-private armed structures. That tendency, documented across multiple investigative outlets in this period, was one of the defining features of the Russian war economy after 2022.[^ch08-003] [^ch08-004]

Ukrainian military intelligence offered a different version of the patronage claim. A statement from Ukraine's Defence Intelligence described Española as connected to Putin's United Russia party — effectively, in this reading, a party militia. That framing matters as evidence of how a hostile state understood the formation's political function. It should not, however, be merged uncritically with the Meduza/iStories patronage chain. The Ukrainian intelligence assessment reflects a different evidentiary basis and a different analytical interest. It is relevant as an attributed claim about political sponsorship, not as independent corroboration of the specific connections identified by Russian investigative journalists.[^ch08-009]

What the two accounts share — even where they diverge on specifics — is the picture of a formation that was not merely receiving public donations. It had higher-level sponsors who saw value in its existence beyond battlefield utility: political value, propagandistic value, the value of having a recognizable armed brand aligned with your interests in a war where visibility was itself a form of power.

That picture has structural consequences for understanding the whole organization. A brigade with public donors and private patrons of the kind described operates in a financial architecture that is more layered and more sophisticated than either a state-funded unit or a grassroots volunteer formation. The public donations give it moral texture: the appearance of popular support, the intimacy of a personally backed cause. The private patronage gives it scale, insulation, and access to resources that public fundraising alone could not sustain. Together, the two tiers create a hybrid economy that looks different from whichever angle you approach it — which is part of its durability and part of its utility to the actors involved.[^ch08-005]

In the language of the Russian war economy after 2022, that kind of arrangement was not an exception. It was a model. Multiple formations in this period drew on some version of the same logic: public loyalty as social cover, private patronage as operational foundation, the combination presented to outsiders as proof of grassroots support for the war. Española was perhaps better at it than most. Its brand was more coherent. Its founder mythology was more compelling. Its media operation was more sophisticated. But it was playing a game that others were playing too, and the rules of that game were determined not by the brigades but by the state that needed them and the patrons who invested in them.[^ch08-006] [^ch08-007]

The financial question, answered as fully as the available evidence permits, therefore points toward something the formation's public language was designed to obscure. Española was supported as an enterprise. Someone at a level above the Telegram channel thought it was worth sustaining. That calculation had consequences that would only become fully visible later, when the value of the enterprise began to look, from above, like a liability.
