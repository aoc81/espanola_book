# Chapter 2 — Stanislav Orlov and the Making of "The Spaniard"

A name becomes a brand only when the story behind it can be retold. Stanislav Orlov's story was told and retold across sympathetic channels, hostile investigations, and the space between them, and what is striking about those accounts is not how different they are but how consistently they agree on the broad architecture: a CSKA supporter who became a soldier, a soldier who went to Donbas, a Donbas fighter who built something that outlasted the first war and became the foundation of a much larger enterprise in the second. The details vary. The myth-making is obvious. But the scaffolding of the biography holds up across sources that had no reason to agree.[^ch02-001]

Begin with the football. Multiple profiles and investigative reports place Orlov inside the CSKA Moscow supporter world and specifically within or adjacent to the Red-Blue Warriors environment — one of the better-organized and more durable firms in the Russian stadium landscape. The significance of that placement is not merely atmospheric. In the supporter world, status was not granted; it was accumulated through presence, confrontation, and the kind of loyalty that survives bad seasons and police pressure. A man who came up through that environment had already spent years inside a social system that rewarded the same qualities — disciplined belonging, physical credibility, willingness to accept hierarchy while maintaining a sense of personal distinction — that a wartime formation would later need to project.[^ch02-002]

From the supporter world, accounts push Orlov toward military service. Russian media profiles — carrying biographical details that should be read as reported rather than independently verified — describe him as a graduate of the Moscow State Technical University, a veteran of the 106th Airborne Division, and a man who served in the North Caucasus during the Second Chechen War. Those details appear in multiple reporting chains, both sympathetic and hostile, and they do consistent work in the biography's architecture: they give the callsign bearer an educational credential alongside a martial one, a degree alongside a combat record, in a milieu where neither alone would have been enough. Open sources are uneven on the specifics, and the accounts originate partly in environments invested in the legend. What is more important than any individual detail is the pattern the accounts are constructing: Orlov was being given a biography that joined terrace authenticity to soldierly legitimacy. The man who later asked fighters to follow him had, in the story told about him, been both a supporter and a soldier — two forms of masculine credentialing that were distinct enough to be additive.[^ch02-003]

The firmer ground arrives in 2014. A cluster of reporting places Orlov in Horlivka, a city in the Donetsk region that became one of the early centers of the armed conflict in Donbas, operating under Igor Bezler — a figure known to journalists and analysts covering the first phase of the war as a local strongman whose authority sat uneasily between Russian state structures, criminal networks, and separatist institutions. Inside that environment, reporting and later analytical writing describe Orlov as associated with a small reconnaissance unit that drew on men from football and nationalist milieus. The unit, referred to in various accounts as Skull and Bones, has accumulated a certain mythology of its own. The admiring versions describe it as an early demonstration that hooligan cohesion could survive translation into battlefield function. The skeptical versions note that many of the strongest claims about its effectiveness come from sources invested in the legend. Both observations can be true at once.[^ch02-004] [^ch02-005]

The callsign Ispanec — the Spaniard — attaches to Orlov in this period. Several accounts suggest it arose from his ability to speak some Spanish, related in some versions to time in Spain or contact with Spanish-speaking environments, which also helped explain a later role connecting him to foreign volunteers. Other accounts offer it more loosely, as the kind of distinctive nickname a memorable man in a small world accumulates without any single origin. The precise etymology is unclear. What is clear is that the alias worked: it was short, portable, personal, and unusual enough to stick. In a military environment where callsigns often distinguish individuals from bureaucratic anonymity, a good one is not a trivial possession.[^ch02-004]

What comes next is less dramatic in surface appearance but more consequential in structural terms. Several accounts place Orlov inside the Union of Donbas Volunteers after the fighting around Debaltseve, during the period when the first active phase of the Donbas war was giving way to a frozen-conflict dynamic. That organization served as a kind of administrative and political linking structure for veterans, wartime managers, and those whose careers had been shaped by the conflict. Moving through it was a transition upward — not in military rank, necessarily, but in the kind of legibility that mattered for anyone hoping to operate at a larger scale. He was becoming visible to people and structures above the level of a field detachment.[^ch02-006]

When the full-scale invasion began in February 2022, that network was already in place. The earliest phase of what would become Española, according to multiple independent accounts, was organized under the Vostok Battalion — one of the Donetsk People's Republic's formal armed formations — before the unit's identity consolidated under the name associated with Orlov's callsign. The Vostok phase mattered not as a detail of organizational genealogy but as evidence of how Orlov navigated the 2022 mobilization: not as an outsider making himself available, but as someone already legible to the structures that needed to absorb him.[^ch02-006b]

One allegation trails him further still. Some analytical and intelligence-adjacent sources have connected Orlov to the attempted coup in Montenegro in 2016, in which a group of figures associated with Russian political networks were alleged to have planned to disrupt the country's accession to NATO. The documentation available in the public record does not support treating this as established biographical fact. It functions here as something narrower: evidence of how Orlov was being read by people who tracked these networks. By the late 2010s, he was already understood — at least by some observers — as someone who was plausible in the zone between irregular warfare, covert action, and state utility. That is a particular kind of reputation, and it did not hurt him when the larger war came.[^ch02-007]

He arrived in 2022 with a biography that had already been narrated, a callsign that had already been circulated, a Donbas lineage that could be advertised, and a network of men who had already, in some form, followed him. That accumulation of prior meaning was what made a brigade named after his callsign plausible rather than merely unusual. He did not merely found a unit. He provided the story that a unit needed to become more than a formation of fighters. He gave it a founder's myth — and the myth, once established, would prove more durable than any single battlefield association.[^ch02-008]
