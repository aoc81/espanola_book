Chapter 1 — Before the War: The Stadium as a Political Incubator

Long before Espanola appeared in combat footage, before the pirate emblem and the Telegram channels and the claims of modern battlefield competence, there was the stadium. Not football in the romantic sense, not sport as leisure, but the social world that formed around Russian supporter tribes in the post-Soviet decades: hierarchy, masculinity, ritualized confrontation, coded loyalty, and a readiness for organized violence. The terrace did not produce a paramilitary formation by itself. It produced a culture in which paramilitary adaptation was imaginable.

Russian hooliganism in the 1990s and 2000s was never one thing. There were ordinary supporters, ultras, harder hooligan cores, and surrounding milieus whose members moved in and out of violence, politics, and petty criminality. But for the purposes of this story, the important feature is not taxonomy. It is structure. These were environments in which group identity mattered intensely, where status was won through discipline and aggression, where confrontation with rivals could be planned in advance, and where loyalty to the group was expected to override comfort, legality, and often common sense.

That culture also developed inside a state that alternated between suppression, observation, and use. Russian authorities spent years trying to discipline football violence, especially when it spilled into broader public disorder. At the same time, multiple reports and retrospective analyses suggest that hooligan and ultra networks were never seen only as a policing problem. They were also understood as reservoirs of mobilizable young men: physically hardened, organizationally literate, and politically volatile. The state did not invent these milieus, but it learned to read their utility.

The far-right overlap belonged to this ecology even before the war in Ukraine made it internationally salient. Xenophobic slogans, racist iconography, and white-supremacist codes circulated through parts of the supporter scene. That did not mean every fan or even every organized ultra group shared the same politics. It did mean that extreme nationalism, violent masculinity, and group identity were often close neighbors. By the time the Kremlin began justifying war through the language of national emergency and imperial restoration, some of the emotional grammar it needed already existed in the stands.

The terrace also trained men in a useful kind of ambiguity. Hooligan violence was collective without being fully institutional. It depended on planning, but not on formal hierarchy alone. It prized local identities, yet could be redirected when broader symbols became compelling enough. This matters because later accounts of Espanola often talk as if the transformation from fans to fighters was startling. In one sense it was. In another, the foundations were visible much earlier. A social world built around enmity, prestige, choreography, and physical risk does not need to be remade from scratch in order to become useful to war.

Episodes like the Manezhnaya Square unrest in 2010 exposed both the scale of the problem and the latent political charge inside these networks. The official answer was greater control: more pressure on supporter movements, more efforts to monitor them, more attempts to depoliticize or tame the roughest elements. But pressure can disperse a subculture without dissolving it. It can also prepare its members to seek new arenas. When stadiums become harder to use as spaces of confrontation, the habits learned there do not simply vanish. They migrate.

What the stadium incubated, then, was not a straight line to the front. It incubated a repertoire: disciplined group movement, ritual loyalty, symbolic aggression, and a readiness to interpret violence as meaningful rather than merely criminal. War would later supply the larger frame. Men who had learned to perform brotherhood in one arena could be asked to perform it in another. Men who had already defined themselves against rivals could be offered a national enemy instead of a club one. Men used to codes, scars, hierarchy, and spectacle could be told that history had finally found the right use for them.

That promise was powerful because it did not require total conversion. It required reframing. The stadium did not disappear when Espanola emerged. It traveled with the men who joined it. Its chants became slogans, its banners became insignia, its rivalries were partially suspended in favor of a larger fraternity, and its violence was given a state-useful mission. To understand that later transformation, the reader has to begin here: in a social environment that already knew how to make belonging feel like combat.

The founder who would turn those materials into a recognisable military identity did not come from outside this world. He came from deep inside it.
