Chapter 1 — The Foundations of “Ispanec”
From the CSKA stands to the battlefields of Donbas
Long before Stanislav Orlov became a wartime brand, he belonged to another theater of organized passion and ritualized violence. In Moscow, among the red-and-blue symbols of CSKA, the stadium terraces offered more than spectacle. They offered hierarchy, belonging, discipline, and enmity. Later source accounts would describe Orlov as a member of the Red-Blue Warriors, one of the better-known CSKA fan groups, and would trace his path from those circles into the armed world that eventually produced Española. Those early affiliations matter not because football fandom automatically leads to militancy—it does not—but because in post-Soviet Russia, some ultra milieus became places where masculinity, group loyalty, and violence were not merely tolerated. They were cultivated.
That is the first thing to understand about Orlov. He did not emerge from nowhere, and he did not step into war as a blank figure. He came from a social world that had already taught him how to move inside tightly bonded male formations, how to perform loyalty, how to read status, and how to convert rivalry into identity. By the time he was drafted into the Russian armed forces in 1999, according to biographical accounts later circulated in both hostile and sympathetic treatments, he had already been shaped by that environment. One source states that he served in the Airborne Troops and claimed to have taken part in the Second Chechen campaign and “many hot spots” thereafter; another sympathetic profile repeats the same broad outline, presenting Chechnya as his first real experience of war. Those are not identical kinds of sources, and they do not deserve identical weight, but together they establish the broad arc: the terrace militant became a conscript soldier, and the soldier carried that experience forward into later conflicts.
It would be too neat, and probably too flattering, to describe this as a straight line from fan to commander. Human lives are rarely so geometric. But in Orlov’s case the continuities are hard to miss. The hooligan milieu had familiarized him with a code of brotherhood rooted in danger and confrontation. Military service gave that code a different vocabulary: reconnaissance, discipline, command, fieldcraft. When later admirers wrote about him, they would present him as one of those instinctive men of war—an untutored strategist, a natural commander, the kind of figure war supposedly reveals from beneath the dead weight of bureaucracy. That language is revealing not because we should accept it at face value, but because it shows the mythology that would later accumulate around him. Even before Española became a recognized formation, the idea of Orlov was already being built: not as a bureaucratic officer, but as a man of action who had risen through ordeal rather than institution.
The next decisive shift came in 2014, when eastern Ukraine became a proving ground for a new kind of war and a new generation of irregular actors. Source accounts from independent investigations and later analytical pieces agree on the essentials: Orlov went to Horlivka in occupied Donbas that year with a group of ultras and joined the forces of Igor Bezler, the separatist commander known as “Bes.” According to these accounts, Orlov’s knowledge of foreign languages helped place him in a coordinating role with foreign fighters or mercenaries, and it was in this setting that he acquired the alias “Ispanec”—“the Spaniard”—that would later outgrow the man and become the name of a military formation. Some later sympathetic or movement-adjacent texts explain the alias through his command of Spanish. Other hostile or analytical accounts emphasize the role he reportedly played among foreign volunteers. These versions are not identical, but they converge on the same point: the name was born in Donbas and stuck because it served a function.
That alias mattered. In wars such as this one, names are never just names. They are shields, signatures, and instruments of self-invention. “Ispanec” sounded distinctive, memorable, slightly theatrical. It provided distance from the ordinary Russian biography of Stanislav Orlov while making him easier to circulate inside separatist military culture. It also hinted at a quality that would become central to his later success: an ability to inhabit a persona rather than simply a rank. In hindsight, the emergence of the alias looks like a small rehearsal for something larger. The man learned to operate not only as a fighter, but as a figure.
In Donbas, he also built what appears to have been the first real prototype of the organization he would later create. Multiple sources state that after serving under Bezler he created or led a reconnaissance unit called “Skull and Bones,” which fought near Horlivka and Debaltseve. That unit matters for more than biographical completeness. It was here, in miniature, that the central innovation later associated with Española began to take shape: the translation of football-firm loyalties into military cohesion. The point was not that hooligans magically became soldiers. The point was that men already accustomed to operating in tight, aggressive, status-conscious groups could be reorganized for irregular warfare faster than more atomized recruits. Several later accounts describe “Skull and Bones” as a reconnaissance company or separate reconnaissance squad that included ultras and proved itself in the fighting around Horlivka and the Battle of Debaltseve.
This was the real foundation—not merely Orlov’s personal combat record, but his discovery that subcultural cohesion could be repurposed for war. In the stadium world, loyalty had been horizontal, tribal, emotional, and often irrational. In Donbas, it was made functional. Rival affiliations that would have mattered intensely in peacetime could be subordinated to a larger frame: Russia, Donbas, war, the enemy. This was not an ideological miracle. It was a practical one. Orlov appears to have understood before many others that the hooligan world already possessed the raw materials for paramilitary organization: recruitment networks, masculine prestige, ritualized violence, and a language of sacrifice that could be transferred from club to nation with surprising ease. Later writing on Española would return to this theme again and again, often admiringly, presenting it as proof that organized fan culture could be transformed into a coherent fighting force.
In 2015, according to multiple accounts, Orlov left the immediate front and joined the Union of Donbas Volunteers, the structure associated with Vladislav Surkov and led by Alexander Khodakovsky. Here, the record becomes especially revealing. The move suggests that Orlov’s trajectory was not confined to the battlefield or to private initiative. He was entering a broader ecosystem in which militants, political operators, and state-adjacent organizers overlapped. The Union of Donbas Volunteers was not simply a veterans’ club. It sat inside a wider architecture through which Russia managed, laundered, and politically organized the personnel of its first war in Ukraine. To move into that orbit was to become legible to power. It was also, perhaps, to be tested for future use.
Then comes one of the murkier episodes in Orlov’s biography: Montenegro. Several later analytical and intelligence-style sources state that he was allegedly involved in, or suspected in connection with, the failed 2016 coup attempt against Prime Minister Milo Đukanović. This claim should be handled carefully. It appears in later analytical reporting and in hostile-source profiles, but it remains a reported allegation rather than a court-established biographical fact in the sources available here. Still, its recurrence across multiple accounts is significant. At minimum, it shows how Orlov had come to be understood: no longer merely a Donbas militant, but a figure whose name could plausibly surface in the gray zone where irregular warfare, covert action, and state interests intersect. Even the allegation tells us something, if not necessarily everything, about his place in that world.
After that, Orlov seems to recede for a time. One analytical account describes him as disappearing from public view after 2016, only to re-emerge after Russia’s full-scale invasion in 2022. Whether this was strategic retreat, quiet work inside less visible networks, or simply the normal opacity of such milieus is difficult to say with confidence. But when he returned, he did so with a project far larger than a reconnaissance detachment. By 2022, he was no longer merely “Ispanec,” the Donbas militant with a useful alias. He was becoming the founder of a formation built around his name, his biography, and the organizational lessons he had extracted from the previous war.
The timing mattered. Russia’s full-scale invasion created demand for manpower, initiative, and new narratives of patriotic mobilization. Orlov’s answer was to gather fighters from fan circles and former comrades into a new volunteer formation that would become Española. Multiple accounts describe the unit as founded by Orlov in 2022, with roots in football fan and hooligan networks across multiple clubs and cities. Some of these accounts explicitly state that the organization was conceived as a counterforce to Ukraine’s Azov movement, which also drew from football and extremist subcultures. Whether that rivalry was strategic, symbolic, or propagandistic—it was probably all three—the comparison was useful. It gave Orlov’s project a ready-made enemy, a mirror, and a justification. Russia could claim to be fighting extremism while simultaneously cultivating a formation that drew deeply from ultra and far-right milieus of its own.
The unit’s name completed the transformation. “Española,” derived from Orlov’s alias, was not just a label. It was a brand. That matters because branding is not secondary in modern irregular war; it is often part of the weapon system. A memorable name helps recruitment, narrative differentiation, fundraising, and internal cohesion. It tells potential fighters that this is not an anonymous battalion swallowed by bureaucracy but a formation with style, mystique, and purpose. In that sense, the move from “Ispanec” to “Española” marks more than an organizational birth. It marks the moment a personal war biography was converted into a transferable identity.
By then, the essential pattern was already in place. The stadium had taught Orlov the grammar of belonging. The Russian military had given him institutional contact with war. Donbas had offered both a battlefield and a laboratory, allowing him to test whether hooligan tribalism could be converted into paramilitary utility. The Surkov-linked volunteer world had brought him closer to a broader state-managed ecosystem. And the full-scale invasion of 2022 gave him the opportunity to assemble all of that into a single formation. Española did not appear from thin air in the spring of that year. It was the product of a much longer apprenticeship in violence, identity, and informal power.
Looking back, the important thing is not whether Stanislav Orlov was a visionary in the grand sense. Such words flatter too easily. The important thing is that he recognized earlier than many others that Russia’s hooligan world could be militarized not in spite of its disorder, but through it. He understood that men shaped by the codes of the terrace could be taught to think of war not as rupture but as continuity: another arena, another enemy, another chance to turn loyalty into action. That insight, more than any single battle or personal myth, was the real foundation of “Ispanec.”
In the next chapter, those personal foundations begin to harden into structure: recruitment, patronage, command, media, and the transformation of a founder’s aura into an organization capable of moving through the blurred space between volunteer brigade, paramilitary enterprise, and state-useful force.
