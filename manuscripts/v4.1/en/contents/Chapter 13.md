# Chapter 13 — Battlefield Reputation: Mariupol, Bakhmut, Avdiivka

The geography of Española's war was wider than the three battles that came to define its public reputation. Before Mariupol became the formation's foundational credential, it was active in theaters less amenable to single-name narration. Independent and self-reported accounts place Española on the Kherson and Zaporizhzhia directions in 2022 — the southern and southeastern fronts where Russian forces attempted to consolidate the corridor between occupied Crimea and the Donbas. The formation also appears in reporting on the fighting around Vuhledar, a strongly fortified Ukrainian position south of Donetsk that saw some of the war's most intensive armored engagements. These earlier or parallel theaters do not feature in the brigade's own retrospective narration with the same emphasis as Mariupol, Bakhmut, and Avdiivka. The public record on them is thinner, and the claims less fully corroborated. But they belong in the geographic account because they indicate that the formation's operational footprint was broader than its brand story suggested — and that the brand story, as constructed, selected for the most symbolically legible engagements rather than attempting to describe the full range of reported activity.[^ch13-001b]

Mariupol was the war's first great siege, and it produced a legend that neither side could leave alone. For Ukraine and its international supporters, the defenders of Azovstal — primarily the Azov regiment, holding a shattered steel plant against an encircling Russian force for eighty-six days before surrender in May 2022 — became a symbol of resistance whose emotional power outlasted the military outcome. For the Russian state, Mariupol was something else: proof of the denazification mission's urgency, evidence that Azov's fighters were exactly as dangerous and as ideologically committed as official propaganda had described. The capture of the city and the surrender of Azovstal gave Russian war media the imagery it needed to sustain that narrative through months of difficult fighting elsewhere.[^ch13-002]

For a formation like Española, whose self-presentation was built in part around the anti-Azov mirror — the claim to be a Russian answer to Ukraine's most symbolically charged formation — association with Mariupol was close to ideal. Multiple outside accounts place the brigade in the Mariupol theater in 2022. The scale and distinctiveness of its role are harder to establish from the available public record: the claims about what Española specifically did at Mariupol rely heavily on the brigade's own channels and on commentary produced by environments that had reasons to amplify the story. But presence itself was already a narrative resource. Mariupol had weight — enormous, symbolic, undeniable weight — and any formation that could honestly say it had been there shared in that weight by association.[^ch13-002]

The Daily Sabah feature published in July 2023 made the civic dimension of that presence visible. Reporting on Española fighters in and around a damaged Mariupol stadium — Russian club symbols replacing Ukrainian markers, men associated with the formation posed in the space — showed that the brigade's relationship to Mariupol had extended beyond the siege phase into the occupation period. The formation was not only claiming a combat association with the city. It was physically present in it, inscribing its own symbols in its public spaces, participating in the reordering of an occupied Ukrainian city under Russian administration. The feature quoted a claimed brigade strength of roughly 600 members and described training near the Azov Sea. Those numerical and capability claims should remain attributed rather than treated as audited fact.[^ch13-009]

Bakhmut supplied a different kind of credential. If Mariupol provided the founding association, Bakhmut — where fighting continued from the summer of 2022 through May 2023, producing casualties on a scale that made it one of the most devastating engagements of the war — provided duration. Any formation that could claim a presence in or around Bakhmut over an extended period could borrow from a theater already saturated with sacrifice, exhaustion, and comparison to the grinding attrition of earlier wars. The Wagner private military company had made Bakhmut its most publicized battle; any unit associated with the same theater, however different its scale and role, was placed by proximity in a more serious league than formations that had fought only in quieter sectors.[^ch13-003]

The open source record supports Española's association with the Bakhmut theater more readily than it supports precise claims about the formation's tactical role there. The important distinction, again, is between presence and legend. Presence was well enough documented. Legend was what the brigade made of it — what the channel posts, the commemorations, and the commander messages turned a period of fighting in a terrible theater into for the purposes of external narration.[^ch13-005]

Avdiivka, where Russian forces broke through Ukrainian defenses in February 2024 after months of fighting, completed the sequence. By the time Española was associated with Avdiivka, it no longer needed a single foundational battle. It had a list: Mariupol, Bakhmut, Avdiivka, and in the brigade's own accounts various other engagements besides. Lists of that kind do specific work in wartime public culture. They tell audiences that a formation belongs to the war's recognizable geography rather than to its decorative margins. Each name in the sequence adds weight to the others. The accumulation makes individual claims about any single battle less important than the overall pattern of association.[^ch13-004]

The record here is not neutral, and neither are the sources. Much of the strongest battlefield language originated with the brigade itself. Some of the most dramatic characterizations came from adversarial environments — Ukrainian analysts and officials — who had their own reasons to make the unit appear particularly significant or dangerous. The reliable middle ground is narrower: multiple outside accounts do place Española in or near these theaters, and the brigade consistently used those associations to build a story of seriousness, sacrifice, and tactical relevance that the available evidence does not entirely contradict. Whether that story is fully accurate in its details is, as a general matter, not resolvable from open sources.[^ch13-005]

Named battles perform work that operational ledgers cannot. They condense complex, partial, and often chaotic participation into a story a listener can follow and remember. A formation that acquires a list of recognizable battle names — that can say Mariupol, Bakhmut, Avdiivka in a row and have those names register — becomes harder to dismiss as pure theater. Not because the names prove every claim, but because the repeated coincidence of the brigade's presence with the war's most contested and most consequential places accumulates its own form of plausibility. Española understood this. The channels tracked it carefully. The names were chosen, curated, and circulated because they worked — because, in a media-saturated war where narrative was itself a resource, a formation with a good list was a formation with a future.
