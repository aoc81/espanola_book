Chapter 4: Extremist Ideology and Its Controversial Symbols
Between neo-Nazi codes and imperial narratives: the complex ideological matrix of Española

Few military formations in the 21st century have exhibited such a flagrant contradiction between the official narrative of their sponsoring state and their real ideological identity as Española. While the Kremlin justifies its invasion of Ukraine under the banner of “denazification,” one of its most active units operates under an explicitly neo-Nazi designation—the “88th Brigade”—and is populated by individuals who openly display Third Reich symbolism. This paradox is not merely cosmetic, but reveals fundamental tensions in the ideological construction of the contemporary Russian imperial project.
The “88” Code: An Explicit Statement
The decision to formally adopt the designation “88th Reconnaissance and Sabotage Brigade” is perhaps the clearest manifestation of the formation’s ideological leanings. In international neo-Nazi symbology, the number “88” constitutes a widely recognized code in which the eighth letter of the alphabet (H) repeated twice means “Heil Hitler.” This reference is not some cryptic subtlety accessible only to insiders, but a public statement that any observer familiar with extremist iconography can immediately decode.
The adoption of this specific numerology cannot be attributed to coincidence or ignorance. The founders and leaders of Española, including Stanislav Orlov, possess the cultural sophistication to understand the implications of this choice. Its maintenance, even after the brigade’s integration into formal state structures, indicates that this ideological identity is not considered problematic by the supervising Russian authorities.
The prominence of “88” in promotional materials, uniforms, and official brigade communications serves as a signal both to potential recruits and to external observers. For individuals with far-right inclinations, it represents confirmation that they can find an ideological home within the formation. For international analysts, it constitutes irrefutable evidence of the nature of the political project the brigade represents.
The Baklanov Flag: Nostalgic Imperialism
One of the most prominent symbols used by Española is the so-called “Baklanov Flag,” featuring a skull and crossbones accompanied by a religious inscription. This iconography, associated with Imperial Russian Army Lieutenant General Yakov Petrovich Baklanov, operates on multiple symbolic levels that reveal the formation’s complex ideological layers.
Baklanov, who served during the 19th century, was particularly known for his role in the repression of ethnic minorities in the Caucasus, activities which made him a controversial figure even in his own time. His adoption as a symbol by Española is no accident: it represents a direct connection to Russian imperial traditions that emphasized the violent subordination of non-Russian populations.
The flag functions as an ideological bridge between traditional Russian nationalism and more extreme elements. For mainstream audiences, it can be interpreted as legitimate historical patriotism. For more radical elements, it represents an explicit celebration of repressive methods that resonate with contemporary agendas of ethnic cleansing.
The prominence of religious symbols in the flag also reveals attempts by the brigade to appropriate narratives of religious Orthodoxy, presenting their activities as a sacred crusade rather than merely secular military operations. This fusion of extreme nationalism with religious iconography has been characteristic of both historical and contemporary fascist movements.
Tattoos and Bodily Markings: Inscribed Ideology
Perhaps no aspect of Española’s symbolic culture is as revealing as the tattoos displayed by its prominent members. Photographic documentation available through their social media channels shows a notable prevalence of explicitly neo-Nazi iconography, including swastikas, kolovrats (Slavic pagan symbols adopted by neo-Nazi groups), and numerical references such as “14/88.”
The most documented case is that of Mikhail “Pitbull” Turkanov, whose swastika tattoos have been widely photographed and distributed through official brigade channels. Far from being hidden or downplayed, these ideological marks are displayed as legitimate credentials, suggesting that the organizational culture not only tolerates but celebrates these affiliations.
The “14/88” combination found on several members deserves special mention. “14” refers to the “Fourteen Words,” a white supremacist slogan advocating for the preservation of the Aryan race, while “88” maintains its reference to “Heil Hitler.” The presence of this specific combination indicates familiarity with international codes of white supremacy that transcend specific national contexts.
Institutional tolerance for these ideological markers reveals the centrality of extremist elements in the brigade’s organizational identity. This is not a case of isolated individuals whose personal views are tolerated out of operational necessity, but of an organizational culture in which these ideologies are defining characteristics.
The Corporate Logo: Appropriated Soviet Militarism
The official logo of Española combines the word “Española” in cursive with the image of a Soviet Tokarev pistol, creating a visual juxtaposition that encapsulates the broader ideological contradictions of the formation. The choice of the Tokarev, an iconic weapon from the Soviet arsenal, alongside stylization evoking mid-20th-century graphic design, represents an attempt to appropriate historical military symbols of power.
This appropriation of Soviet iconography by a formation with explicitly neo-Nazi leanings illustrates the syncretic nature of contemporary Russian extremism. Rather than adhering to coherent ideologies, these movements combine elements from different authoritarian traditions—Tsarist imperialism, Soviet power, European fascism—to create amalgams that serve contemporary political objectives.
The use of cursive lettering for the word “Española” also deserves consideration. This typographical choice evokes graphic design traditions associated with political movements of the 20th century, providing an aesthetic that simultaneously suggests professionalism and radicalism.
Rituals and Ceremonies: Building Collective Identity
Española’s symbolic culture extends beyond static iconography into rituals and ceremonies that reinforce ideological identity through collective performance. Award ceremonies, in which fighters receive decorations from Russian military authorities, are particularly revealing of how extremist symbolism is integrated into official contexts.
The presence of Lieutenant General Vladimir Stepanovich Alekseyev, Deputy Chief of the GRU, at ceremonies where fighters with neo-Nazi tattoos receive state decorations illustrates the level of institutional acceptance of these ideologies. These ceremonies are not private or unofficial events, but formal activities involving representatives of the highest echelons of the Russian security apparatus.
Photographs of these ceremonies, widely distributed through the brigade’s official channels, function as implicit endorsements of the represented ideologies. The juxtaposition of respectable state authorities with fighters displaying extremist symbols normalizes these associations and presents them as compatible with legitimate Russian patriotism.
The Feminization of Extremist Iconography
Española’s decision to begin recruiting women in November 2023 has introduced additional dimensions into its symbolic culture. The integration of women into combat roles, including assault units, represents a significant departure from masculinist traditions that have historically characterized both football hooliganism and neo-Nazi movements.
This evolution suggests a pragmatization of extremist ideology wherein operational considerations outweigh doctrinal purity. The military effectiveness required for the Ukrainian conflict has forced adaptations that contradict traditional elements of neo-Nazi ideology, particularly its concepts of appropriate gender roles.
However, the integration of women has also required adaptations in the brigade’s symbolic culture. Promotional materials now include images of female fighters displaying the same extremist symbols as their male counterparts, extending the normalization of these ideologies to audiences that traditionally would have been excluded.
Contradictions and Ideological Tensions
The ideological matrix of Española contains inherent tensions that reflect broader contradictions in the contemporary Russian political project. The simultaneous celebration of Soviet iconography and tsarist imperialism, combined with European neo-Nazism, creates a syncretism that is more effective as a tool of mobilization than as a coherent belief system.
These contradictions are particularly evident in the context of the official narrative of Ukraine’s “denazification.” The prominence of explicit neo-Nazi symbology in a formation supposedly fighting Nazism exposes the instrumental rather than genuine nature of the ideological justifications offered by the Kremlin.
Official tolerance of these contradictions suggests that Russian authorities prioritize operational effectiveness over ideological consistency. Española’s ability to mobilize extremist elements for state objectives is valued more highly than the coherence of the broader political narrative.
International Impact and Perceptions
The open adoption of neo-Nazi symbolism by Española has significantly complicated Russian diplomatic efforts to portray the Ukrainian conflict as a fight against fascism. International observers have highlighted these contradictions as evidence of the hypocritical nature of the justifications offered for the invasion.
Sanctions imposed by the United Kingdom in November 2024 specifically mentioned the extremist nature of the formation, indicating that these ideological characterizations have tangible diplomatic consequences. The international visibility of the symbols used by the brigade has made its iconographic choices a diplomatic liability for the Russian state.
However, these same characteristics that complicate international relations also facilitate domestic recruitment among populations with extremist inclinations. The brigade functions as a honeypot for individuals who might otherwise be problematic for internal stability, channeling their energies toward state geopolitical objectives.
The Future of Extremist Identity
The long-term sustainability of Española’s symbolic culture faces significant challenges as the formation becomes more fully integrated into conventional military structures. The requirements for professionalization and standardization associated with integration into the Ministry of Defense’s Volunteer Corps could gradually erode the distinctive ideological characteristics that have defined the brigade’s organizational identity.
Alternatively, the successful normalization of extremist symbolism within official military contexts could represent a broader mainstreaming of these ideologies in Russian society. The precedent set by Española could facilitate the acceptance of similar elements in other formations and institutional contexts.
The resolution of this tension between extremist identity and institutional integration will determine not only the future of Española, but also the broader trajectories of political extremism in contemporary Russia.
In the next chapter, we will examine how these complex and controversial ideological identities are translated into concrete strategies for recruitment and the formation of new members, exploring the mechanisms by which the brigade perpetuates and expands its distinctive organizational culture.
