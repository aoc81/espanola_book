# A Note on Names and Terms

Several naming decisions in this book require brief explanation, because the terms involved are neither standardized nor neutral.

**The formation's name.** This manuscript uses *Española* as the primary rendering throughout. Sources, quotations, and archived materials may use the variant *Espanola* (without the accent), the Russian-language form *Эспаньола*, or occasionally other transliterations. These variants are preserved in direct quotations and source citations but normalized to *Española* in the body of the text. The name is Spanish, and the accent belongs.

**The founder's name and callsign.** Stanislav Orlov is introduced by full name on first reference and thereafter referred to by his callsign *Ispanec* — the Spaniard — which is the form most widely used in both sympathetic and investigative Russian-language sources. The Russian-language form *Испанец* appears in source material and quotations. The English translation *the Spaniard* is used where the prose requires it for clarity.

**Place names.** Place names follow the most common English-language forms used in coverage of the war. *Horlivka* is the primary rendering of the city in Donetsk region where Orlov operated in 2014; the variant *Gorlovka* appears in source material and is preserved in quotations. *Chasiv Yar* is used as the primary form; the variant *Chasov Yar* appears in some sources.

**Organizational labels.** This is the most consequential naming question in the book, because the labels applied to the formation changed over time and were not always used consistently even by the sources that applied them. *Battalion*, *brigade*, *PMC*, *volunteer formation*, and *unit* are not synonymous, and the book does not treat them as interchangeable.

The label *88th brigade* — or *88th volunteer brigade* or *88th reconnaissance-sabotage brigade* — appears in Russian state media and the formation's own post-2024 messaging and refers specifically to the formation's later organizational designation within the Volunteer Corps. It is used in this book where it applies to that period; it is not used as a general synonym for Española across the full span of its history.

The variant *Hispaniola* appears occasionally in secondary transliterations and reference-style material. It is not the dominant spelling in either the formation's own Russian-language self-description or in the primary English-language analytical and journalistic corpus reviewed for this project. This book uses *Española* throughout and treats *Hispaniola* as a secondary transliteration of lower evidentiary weight.

Earlier in the story, the formation operated in a looser wartime ecosystem in which its precise legal and command status was undefined or deliberately blurred. During the middle phase, fighters were reported to sign through Redut-linked contracting arrangements, which gave the formation a more formal administrative shell while preserving considerable cultural autonomy. In the later phase, outside reporting and the formation's own public statements placed it within the 88th reconnaissance and sabotage brigade, the Volunteer Corps, and the Dobrokor environment.

Where the exact legal or command relationship is unclear in the available record, the prose uses deliberately narrower formulations: *Redut-linked*, *state-adjacent*, or *linked in outside reporting to military intelligence structures*. These phrasings signal attributed interpretation rather than established organizational fact.

Readers who encounter apparent inconsistency in these labels should read them as chronological markers rather than errors. What changed over time was not only the label but the underlying reality it was trying to describe.
