# Chapter 6 — The Humanitarian Wing

In the occupied city of Horlivka — the same Donetsk-region city where Stanislav Orlov had first tested his model a decade earlier — a boys' football team played under the visual identity of Española. Reporting and archived material describe the project as bearing the same pirate-coded insignia that marked the brigade's wartime channels and patches. Boys in a city under occupation, organized into a sport by a formation that had gone to war partly because of the world that same sport had once given its founder.[^ch06-003]

The scene is worth pausing on, because it captures something that the purely military account of Española misses entirely. By 2023, the formation was not operating only on the front. It was embedded in occupied territory in ways that exceeded its military function. The Horlivka football project was the most visible instance of something more general: a brigade that had learned to live behind the lines as well as at the front, and that understood — whether or not it articulated the understanding in those terms — that durability required a social presence that went beyond combat.

The word "humanitarian" that Española and its affiliated channels used to describe their rear-area activities requires careful handling. In the context of the Russian war effort, and specifically in the vocabulary of formations like this one, "humanitarian" did not mean what it means in the standard language of international relief organizations. It was not about neutrality. It was not about impartiality between combatants. It referred, instead, to the support infrastructure around an armed formation: the collection and distribution of equipment, supplies, and money; the care of fighters' families; the provision of aid to civilians in occupied territory; and the cultivation of the public-facing image that gave the formation a life beyond the battlefield.[^ch06-004] [^ch06-005]

Two men named repeatedly in reporting as central to this infrastructure were Ilya Khanin and Alexey Trifonov. Their roles, as described in Cherta Media and Meduza reporting, were those of brokers and organizers: men who moved goods, coordinated appeals, and presented the brigade's support needs in terms that invited civic participation rather than purely military transaction. The language of their appeals was intimate and personal in a way that bureaucratic fundraising rarely achieves. A potential donor was not being asked to contribute to a military budget line. They were being asked to help specific men, to keep a particular named formation supplied, to participate in the life of a brotherhood they could follow from their phone.[^ch06-002]

A third figure in this support architecture operated at a different register. Alexey Zhivov — a pro-war blogger identified on the Ukrainian War and Sanctions portal as a promoter and material supporter of the formation — was active in UAV and electronic-warfare fundraising and connected to the broader pro-war media ecosystem that amplified Española's appeals to wider audiences. Where Khanin and Trifonov were brokers in goods and logistics, Zhivov was a broker in attention: a person whose presence in pro-war media circles widened the formation's reach beyond the hooligan and nationalist milieus that were its organic base. His involvement points to something the chapter on the media operation will develop further: the formation's support infrastructure was not limited to fighters and their immediate networks. It had built connections into the professional information apparatus of the Russian war effort.[^ch06-002b]

By 2024, the financial architecture had also acquired a more formal institutional expression. A registered charity structure — a Fund bearing the Española name — appeared in Russian corporate-registry aggregators and in the formation's own public communications. This was not a covert financial vehicle but an overt branding institution: a legally registered body through which the formation could collect and disburse funds in ways that a Telegram channel could not. The fund was the administrative formalization of what the humanitarian appeals had been building informally for two years — a civic presence with a legal address.[^ch06-002c]

That intimacy was engineered, and it worked. The fundraising appeals that passed through affiliated channels asked for vehicles, drones, medical supplies, communications equipment, and support for fighters' families. These were not hidden. They were presented in the same moral register as charitable giving — in some cases indistinguishable in tone from the appeals of explicitly civilian aid organizations — while the needs being met were, in practice, those of an active combat formation. The category was hybrid from the start: humanitarian in presentation, military in application, civic in aspiration.[^ch06-004]

That hybridity was not accidental or confused. It was functional. It gave sympathizers who were unwilling or unable to enlist a meaningful role in the brigade's project. It made fundraising respectable by attaching it to the language of care rather than the language of war. It allowed the formation to reach into social milieus — families, women, older supporters, people with money but without the inclination to fight — that pure military recruitment could not access. And in occupied territory, it gave the brigade a relationship with the civilian population that was more durable than anything that could be achieved through military presence alone.

The Horlivka football project concentrated these functions into a single image. Here was Española not as a fighting unit but as a community institution: organizing children, continuing the game that had built its founder's social world, planting its symbol in a Ukrainian city now under Russian administration. The line between outreach, normalization, propaganda, and long-term recruitment theater was, in this project, essentially invisible. All four functions were present simultaneously. That is precisely what made it such a useful piece of the formation's civic portfolio.

The sports world offered a different kind of event for a different audience. On 1 September 2023, a CSKA Moscow hockey evening was organized in connection with the formation, an occasion documented by the Ukrainian War and Sanctions tracking portal and involving Russian ice hockey player Ivan Fedotov alongside other sports-world figures. The event is a concrete instance of what remained otherwise easy to describe only in general terms: the formation's capacity to move between military, subcultural, and mainstream sporting registers without losing coherence. A hockey evening with professional athletes is not a fundraiser in the sense that a Telegram drone appeal is a fundraiser. It is a visibility event — the kind that says, to an audience that might never donate to a military formation, that serious people from the recognized sporting world have found the brigade worth their presence.[^ch06-002d]

A formation with only a military face is limited. It depends on the war to sustain it, and it disappears when the war ends or when the state withdraws its support. A formation that has embedded itself into sport, family care, local public life, and the emotional rhythms of the communities around it is harder to remove. It has built constituencies that extend beyond its fighting membership. That is what the humanitarian wing accomplished for Española: it converted military presence into social depth. The boys playing football in Horlivka under a pirate-coded banner were not merely the beneficiaries of a charity project. They were the evidence of a formation that intended to persist.
