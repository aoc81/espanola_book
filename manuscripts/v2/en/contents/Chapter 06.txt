Chapter 6 — Mirrors, Rivals, and Useful Extremists
Española inside the war’s crowded militant ecosystem
No armed formation defines itself in isolation. Even the most self-mythologizing brigade needs contrasts, enemies, peers, and rivals against which to sharpen its own identity. By the time Española had become visible on the battlefield and in Russian wartime media, it was no longer just a standalone unit with a founder, a donor network, and a cultivated style. It was becoming legible inside a much larger ecosystem of irregular force: football ultras, neo-Nazi and far-right militants, volunteer battalions, Redut-linked formations, remnants of Wagner’s prestige economy, and competing patriotic brands such as Akhmat. To understand what Española became, one has to understand not only how it fought, but where it placed itself inside that crowded and unstable hierarchy. Several analytical and investigative sources describe the brigade as occupying a distinct niche within Russia’s wider far-right and paramilitary environment: close enough to extremist circles to draw recruits and symbolic capital from them, yet structured enough—and sufficiently tied to Redut and the broader state-managed war system—to remain useful to power.
Its most important symbolic mirror was Azov.
From the beginning, outside analysts and several source accounts described Española as a Russian counterforce to the Ukrainian Azov formation. Jamestown’s March 2024 overview states this explicitly, arguing that Española was reportedly created as a counterforce to Azov, which, like it, had roots in football hooliganism. Evocation’s hostile profile of Orlov makes a similar point, though it adds a useful complication: Orlov himself reportedly denied that this was the original reason, instead framing the decision to go to the front as a response to how Azov fighters treated prisoners. Whether that denial reflected genuine chronology or later rhetorical adjustment matters less than the basic structure of the comparison. Azov offered España an ideal enemy-image precisely because it was so similar in form: a movement forged in radical subculture, steeped in militant masculinity, and transformed by war into something much larger than its origins. In that sense, Azov was not only an adversary. It was a mirror.
This symmetry mattered enormously to Russian propaganda. The Kremlin’s official justification for the invasion leaned heavily on the language of “denazification,” with Azov serving as its most convenient emblem. Yet as independent observers and analysts repeatedly noted, Russia was fielding its own formations drawn from violent ultra-nationalist and neo-Nazi milieus, including Rusich, elements around the Russian Imperial Movement (RIM), and the football-hooligan militia Española. Jamestown’s Turkanov profile captures the contradiction directly, noting that international observers quickly pointed out Russia’s own far-right radicals as soon as the Kremlin made Azov central to its rhetorical case for war. But contradiction is not the same thing as dysfunction. For the Russian state, units like Española could be useful precisely because they turned the logic of extremist mobilization against Ukraine while remaining rhetorically subordinated to a state anti-fascist narrative. The hypocrisy was not a bug in the system. It was part of how the system worked.
In practice, this made Española valuable in at least two ways. First, it provided a Russian analogue to Azov that could be marketed to domestic audiences as a kind of patriotic answer to Ukrainian nationalist militancy. Second, it offered a route for mobilizing sectors of Russian ultra culture and far-right masculinity that the state could neither fully generate on its own nor fully trust if left unsupervised. Jamestown argues that figures like Mikhail “Pitbull” Turkanov helped attract highly nationalistic soccer hooligans who might otherwise be skeptical of Moscow. Molfar, from a Ukrainian intelligence-analysis perspective, makes a similar point in harsher terms, describing Española as useful because it could attract a specific part of Russia’s ultraright militaristic ecosystem while remaining connected to Redut, the Ministry of Defense, and oligarchic or GRU-linked structures. In other words, the brigade’s political value lay in its ability to mobilize radicals without becoming completely uncontrollable.
That is where Rusich and RIM enter the story.
Jamestown’s Turkanov profile describes Española as “distinct from, yet supportive of,” both the neo-Nazi Rusich group and the hard-line Orthodox Russian Imperial Movement. That phrasing is more important than it may first appear. It suggests that Española’s function inside the Russian far-right military ecology was not to represent the most openly ideological or most brutally infamous current—that role was already occupied elsewhere—but to provide an adjacent, more adaptable vessel. Rusich had notoriety. RIM had its own ideological and networked history. Española had something different: broader cultural reach through football fandom, a stronger social-media brand, and a way of blending subcultural authenticity with an image of operational usefulness. It could therefore occupy a mediating position between hardcore extremist milieus and the wider patriotic war audience.
That mediating function may also explain why individual figures associated with more notorious extremist currents appear around the brigade. Molfar’s file, for example, states that the “Melody” information and analytical center was led by Yevgeny Rasskazov (“Topaz”), whom it describes as a neo-Nazi linked to Rusich and as one of the more notorious separatist extremists from Donbas. That source is adversarial and must be handled carefully, but it is still analytically significant. If accurate, it would suggest that Española did not merely coexist alongside other far-right militant formations; it could also absorb or work with personnel whose ideological or operational lineage connected them to even more radical units. Such permeability is one of the defining features of this ecosystem. It is not a landscape of clean organizational boundaries. It is a landscape of overlapping men, symbols, and loyalties.
The comparison with Wagner is different, because Wagner was not simply another far-right formation. It was, for a time, the dominant prestige brand in Russia’s irregular war world.
By the time Española was consolidating its public identity, Wagner had already shown what a successful Russian paramilitary brand could become: feared on the battlefield, culturally legible at home, media-savvy, and politically dangerous. Several sources in this material suggest that Española’s rise was inseparable from the post-Wagner moment. Molfar argues that the promotion of the “Española” brand may have been part of an attempt to replace Wagner in the media sphere and to provide a new symbolic counterweight within the wartime landscape. Jamestown’s March 2024 overview similarly situates increased attention to groups like Española in the aftermath of Prigozhin’s mutiny, when Russia’s mercenary and volunteer environment came under much greater scrutiny and pressure. These are not identical claims, but they converge on a plausible interpretation: Wagner’s rise created a model; Wagner’s revolt created a vacuum. Smaller, more governable brands suddenly became more attractive.
Yet Española’s relationship to Wagner was not one of simple succession. Jamestown’s Turkanov profile records that despite the brigade’s prominence, Española continued to lionize Yevgeny Prigozhin after his death. It also notes that when the Wagner mutiny occurred, Rusich, Española, and Turkanov himself criticized the Russian Ministry of Defense, especially in connection with the state’s attempt to force irregular and volunteer units into contract arrangements that would tighten control. Molfar, in turn, states that after Prigozhin’s rebellion, some Wagner fighters moved to Española, and that the brigade invited Wagner men to join it while not openly supporting the mutiny itself. This is a revealing combination. It shows that Española admired Wagner’s charisma and prestige, drew strength from the disillusionment Wagner had articulated toward the Ministry of Defense, and may even have absorbed some of its personnel—but it did so without crossing the line into outright rebellion. That was precisely the sort of balance the Kremlin could tolerate.
If Wagner represented the danger of an irregular force becoming too powerful, too personal, and too autonomous, Española represented a more careful experiment: a branded volunteer-paramilitary formation with subcultural roots and charismatic figures, but embedded through Redut into a structure more directly useful to the Ministry of Defense. Jamestown notes that even as the brigade advertised its distinctiveness, its fighters’ contracts were still handled through Redut, which some observers viewed as a replacement mechanism for Wagner after Prigozhin. Molfar makes the same broader point more explicitly, arguing that Española’s connection to the GRU/Redut system and financing from the Ministry of Defense and oligarchs close to Putin made it a way for the authorities to control ultraright militarized networks while reducing the risk of another “Prigozhin revolt.” That claim should remain attributed, not presented as judicially settled fact. But as an analytical proposition, it is strong. España’s appeal to radicals was an asset only if the state could channel it without losing command.
The comparison with Akhmat reveals a different kind of rivalry: not symbolic mirroring, but direct competition within the pro-war camp.
Molfar reports that Española had a conflict with Akhmat, the heavily mediatised Chechen formation associated with Ramzan Kadyrov. According to that account, a column of Española fighters moving for rotation was blocked and attacked by men later identified as Akhmat members. In response, Española reportedly appealed to Alexander Bastrykin, head of the Russian Investigative Committee, with whom, Molfar claims, the brigade had established a special relationship. The conflict allegedly led to the exposure of the identities of Española fighters in private channels. Because this comes through an adversarial analytical source, it must remain clearly attributed and treated with caution. Still, the episode matters because it points to something structurally important: pro-Russian irregular formations were not simply united under one patriotic banner. They were competitors, jealous of prestige, media space, resources, and symbolic primacy.
That competition helps explain why some analysts read the promotion of the Española brand as a counterweight to Akhmat. Akhmat already occupied one pole of Russia’s wartime media environment: heavily branded, politically protected, and associated with a particular national-regional and ideological style of combat masculinity. Wagner had occupied another: hyper-charismatic, brutally effective in public myth, and eventually too autonomous. Española offered a third model—urban, football-derived, digitally fluent, nationalist but not formally Chechen, rough-edged yet capable of broader mainstreaming. In a war that was also a contest among internal brands of loyalty, that distinctiveness had value. A state does not always want one armed mythology to dominate. Sometimes it wants several, each balancing the others.
There is also a broader geopolitical dimension to this ecosystem. Jamestown’s March 2024 essay emphasizes that Española’s connection to football could help forge ties not only across Russia but also with extremists and fans in the Balkans, Central Europe, and beyond. Molfar likewise notes the presence of foreign fighters from Serbia, Cuba, the United States, Spain, and possibly elsewhere in the brigade’s orbit. These reports vary in specificity and quality, and foreign-fighter claims always deserve caution. But the principle is clear. Football provided something older and more transnational than the war itself: a set of subcultural pathways through which militant identification could travel across borders more easily than bureaucratic ideology usually does. That made Española more than a Russian brigade with a domestic recruiting base. It made it a potential node in a broader network of militant fandom and extremist affinity.
What emerges from all of this is a more precise picture of Española’s niche.
It was not the most ideologically explicit formation in Russia’s militant far-right world. Others were more nakedly so. It was not the most institutionally important formation either. Wagner and Akhmat carried more national weight at their peaks. Nor was it the most bureaucratically anonymous. That was exactly what made it useful. Española occupied the space between extremes: radical enough to attract ultras and far-right militants, branded enough to build a following, culturally distinct enough to feel authentic, and structurally embedded enough—through Redut and elite patronage allegations—to remain within the state’s zone of utility.
This is why the chapter is not really about ideology alone. It is about management. Modern authoritarian war does not merely crush extremism, nor does it simply unleash it. It curates it. It selects which militant currents are too unruly, which are too marginal, which are too useful to waste, and which can be repackaged into patriotic force. Española was one such package: a formation that could draw on the energies of football hooliganism and the symbolic capital of the far-right ecosystem without being reduced to either. It did not stand outside the war’s architecture of power. It helped show how that architecture functioned.
But ecosystems built on controlled rivalry and partial autonomy are unstable by nature. The more such formations gain battlefield reputation, media presence, and their own constituencies, the more they test the boundaries of what the state is willing to tolerate. In the next chapter, we follow that tension into the machinery above Española: Redut, the Ministry of Defense, intelligence-linked oversight, and the larger effort to fold semi-autonomous war brands back under central command before they become problems of their own.
