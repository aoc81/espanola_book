Chapter 3 — From Terrace to Trench

The social problem Espanola had to solve was not recruitment in the narrow sense. Russia had no shortage of men willing to fight for money, ideology, adventure, or simple drift. The harder task was converting rival supporter identities into a single militant fraternity without draining them of the emotional force that had made those identities useful in the first place. In peacetime, the hooligan world was built on opposition. A CSKA core did not become Spartak simply by joining the same front. The remarkable part of Espanola's early social engineering was that it found a way to suspend club rivalry without pretending it had never existed.

This required a new scale of belonging. Club loyalty had to be subordinated, not erased, by a larger frame: Russia, Donbas, war, anti-Azov symbolism, the brotherhood of men already shaped by violence. In practice this meant that the traits learned in supporter culture could be retained while their object changed. Group discipline still mattered. Nicknames still mattered. Memory, grievance, and masculine performance still mattered. What changed was the declared enemy and the prestige attached to confronting him.

There was nothing inevitable about that process. Rivalries among Russian supporter groups were not decorative. They had produced years of organized violence and durable tribal memory. Yet war offered something the stadium could not: a chance to reclassify those rivalries as secondary, even childish, before a supposedly higher historical mission. The move was persuasive not because it dissolved old identities, but because it allowed men to keep the emotional intensity of those identities while claiming they had matured into something larger.

Sport supplied the grammar for this conversion. Supporter culture already knew how to talk about loyalty, sacrifice, betrayal, symbolic territory, and the dignity of the group. It already knew how to turn style into belonging and belonging into action. War did not have to teach these men collective feeling. It had to reroute it. This is why later descriptions of Espanola often sound less like the creation of a standard military unit and more like the expansion of an ultra scene into a new arena. The habits of recognition, the value placed on scars and courage, even the aesthetic of banners and signs, all translated more easily than they would have in a less prepared social world.

Recruitment took advantage of that familiarity. Men did not have to imagine entering a faceless institution. They were entering a brotherhood that claimed to understand them already. Existing social ties mattered, but so did narrative plausibility. A recruit from one supporter tribe could believe he was not betraying his own world by joining men from another if the war was presented as the proper place where old rivalries were superseded by national purpose. The point was not reconciliation. It was hierarchy. The larger mission now sat above the smaller feud.

This is also where the language of authenticity began to matter. A conventional unit can promise salary, equipment, and command. Espanola promised recognition. It told potential recruits that the qualities which had made them difficult to assimilate in civilian life — aggressiveness, loyalty to the group, contempt for passivity, fluency in confrontation — were not defects to be sanded down. They were assets waiting for the right use. That promise was both socially powerful and politically dangerous. It did not civilize violent subculture. It gave it a patriotic frame.

At the same time, the conversion was never complete. Drafts and reporting alike suggest that club origins still mattered inside the formation, even if publicly subordinated. Residual segregation, internal competition, and status rivalry did not disappear. They were managed. That matters because it keeps the chapter from slipping into romantic language about perfect unity. Espanola did not abolish the logic of hooligan identity. It repurposed it and profited from the intensity that remained.

Seen this way, "from terrace to trench" is not a metaphor of personal growth. It is a description of social transport. The trench inherited a culture first rehearsed in the stands: men who prized the group, accepted risk as proof of value, and understood style as part of power. Once those men had been shown that war could offer a broader fraternity than football, the formation they entered was no longer just a military unit. It was a supra-club identity built for violence.

The first place that identity was tested was not the full-scale invasion of 2022. It was Donbas, where the networks, practices, and roles that later defined the brigade had already begun to take shape.
