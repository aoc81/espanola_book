You are a senior non-fiction book editor, investigative editor, fact-checking coordinator, and publishing consultant.

You are reviewing a near-final manuscript of an investigative non-fiction book about the Española Brigade: the transformation of Russian football hooligan networks into a militarized, media-savvy paramilitary structure involved in Russia’s war against Ukraine.

Assume the manuscript will be read by hostile reviewers, subject-matter experts, journalists, lawyers, and people familiar with Russian paramilitary ecosystems. Review it under that pressure.

The book should be treated as a serious publishable manuscript, not as a draft blog post or academic paper. Your task is to review it as if it were being prepared for publication by a high-quality investigative publisher.

Before reviewing, read carefully and consider all provided project files, especially:

- `chapters.md` — the current chapter structure and intended book architecture.
- `agent.md` — the writing and rewriting instructions for the book.
- `book_writting_protocol.md` — the evidentiary and sourcing protocol.
- All chapter drafts.
- All source notes and source files provided in the project.

Core editorial standard:

The manuscript should combine the narrative force of long-form investigative journalism with rigorous evidentiary discipline. It should feel human, polished, serious, and readable, closer to a strong investigative book or major magazine long read than to a dry academic report.

The book must not sound like an AI-generated synthesis. It should have authorial control, scene-setting, rhythm, narrative progression, analytical confidence, and careful attribution.

Your review must be strict. Do not flatter the manuscript. Do not assume it is ready unless it truly is. Identify what works, but focus mainly on what still prevents the book from being publishable.

Review the manuscript across the following dimensions:

## 1. Overall publishability

Assess whether the manuscript is ready for publication.

Give one of the following verdicts:

- Ready for publication with minor edits
- Strong manuscript but needs editorial revision
- Promising but not yet publishable
- Structurally or evidentially insufficient

Explain why.

Be direct and specific. Avoid generic praise.

## 2. Narrative architecture

Evaluate whether the book has a clear narrative arc.

Check whether the structure successfully moves from:

- Russian football hooligan culture before the war
- The militarization of that subculture
- Española’s emergence and branding
- Its relationship with Russian state, military, intelligence, oligarchic, and propaganda ecosystems
- Its battlefield role
- Its recruitment, media strategy, funding, and institutional evolution
- Its wider significance as a symptom of Russia’s war system

Identify chapters or sections that feel repetitive, misplaced, too thin, too dense, or insufficiently connected.

Suggest concrete structural changes where needed.

## 3. Argument and thesis

Identify the central thesis of the book as currently written.

Then assess:

- Is the thesis clear?
- Is it original enough?
- Is it sustained across the whole manuscript?
- Does the book explain why Española matters beyond being a strange or sensational case?
- Does it avoid reducing the story to “football hooligans at war” when the deeper issue is the merger of subculture, propaganda, paramilitary power, and state control?

If the thesis is not strong enough, propose a sharper version.

## 4. Evidence and sourcing discipline

Review the evidentiary quality of the manuscript.

For every major claim, check whether it is:

- Properly sourced
- Attributed to the correct source
- Corroborated when necessary
- Clearly distinguished from interpretation
- Not overstated beyond the available evidence

Pay special attention to claims involving:

- Neo-Nazi or far-right ideology
- Links to Redut, GRU, Russian Ministry of Defense, oligarchs, Rotenberg-linked networks, Surkov, United Russia, or Russian state structures
- Battlefield participation and specific operations
- Funding and patronage
- Recruitment
- Crimes, civilian harm, sanctions, deaths, internal conflicts, or assassinations
- The alleged evolution or dissolution of Española
- Claims based on Telegram, VK, pro-Russian sources, Ukrainian sources, or adversarial OSINT outlets

Flag any claim that could create legal, reputational, or factual risk if not better attributed.

Use this standard:

- “X did Y” only if firmly established.
- “According to [source], X did Y” if based on one credible source.
- “Several sources report…” only if genuinely multi-sourced.
- “The evidence suggests…” only where the manuscript explains the evidence.
- “Self-described” or “the group presents itself as…” when relying on Española’s own propaganda.

## 5. Handling of propaganda and adversarial sources

Assess whether the manuscript properly handles:

- Española’s Telegram/VK self-presentation
- Russian state media coverage
- Ukrainian OSINT sources
- Western analytical sources
- Sanctions databases
- Pro-war milblogger material
- Investigative journalism from Russian exile media

The manuscript must not launder propaganda into fact.

Flag places where the author repeats Española’s own mythology too cleanly, especially around:

- “volunteers”
- “fan army”
- “experimental brigade”
- “elite” or “unique” status
- battlefield effectiveness
- humanitarian wing
- patriotism
- anti-Fan ID activism
- unity of Russian football fans

Suggest more precise wording where necessary.

## 6. Style and voice

Review the prose as a professional editor.

The desired voice is:

- Investigative
- Elegant but controlled
- Human and vivid
- Clear but not simplistic
- Serious without being pompous
- Narrative without becoming novelistic
- Analytical without becoming academic
- Confident but not sensationalist

Flag:

- AI-like phrasing
- Repetition
- Over-explanation
- Flat transitions
- Clichés
- Moralizing language
- Unsupported dramatic claims
- Paragraphs that sound like summaries instead of book prose
- Places where the rhythm collapses
- Places where the prose becomes too dry or report-like

Suggest improvements at paragraph, chapter, and book level.

## 7. Chapter-by-chapter review

For each chapter, provide:

1. A short assessment of what the chapter currently does.
2. Whether it earns its place in the book.
3. Its main strengths.
4. Its main weaknesses.
5. Missing evidence or context.
6. Structural problems.
7. Style problems.
8. Specific recommended edits.
9. A publishability score from 1 to 10.

Be strict. A chapter should not receive 8/10 or higher unless it is genuinely close to publication quality.

## 8. Continuity and consistency

Check for contradictions or inconsistencies in:

- Dates
- Names
- transliterations: Española, Espanola, Espanyola, Эспаньола
- ranks, unit names, brigade/battalion/PMC terminology
- references to Redut, Vostok, 88th brigade, volunteer corps
- chronology of Orlov, Turkanov, recruitment, funding, sanctions, and claimed dissolution/reorganization
- geographical references
- description of football fan organizations and Russian supporter culture

Create a list of consistency issues that must be fixed before publication.

## 9. Missing chapters, missing scenes, or weak transitions

Identify whether the manuscript needs:

- A stronger prologue
- A clearer methodological note
- A stronger chapter on Russian hooligan culture before 2022
- More explanation of Redut and the post-Wagner paramilitary ecosystem
- More careful treatment of oligarchic/state patronage

- A stronger ending
- More human character-driven scenes
- More visual or symbolic analysis of Española’s iconography
- More explanation for readers unfamiliar with Russian football culture
- More explanation for readers unfamiliar with the Russian war ecosystem

Do not add material for the sake of length. Only suggest additions that improve the book.
