Chapter 13: Chronology of Relevant Events
From Russian stadiums to the Ukrainian front: the sequence of a paramilitary mutation

The history of Española is, in many ways, the story of the militarization of a violent and marginal subculture, of the recycling of social capital from sports and extremism for the hybrid wars of the 21st century. This chapter traces the most relevant milestones in the evolution of the brigade, highlighting how historical conjuncture, political opportunities, and the vagaries of leadership have come to define the final profile of this controversial formation.

Detailed Timeline

1990s
●	Mid-90s: Hooliganism and the ultra movement in Russian football radicalize and begin to openly flirt with the far right.
●	Late 90s: The Kremlin tries to co-opt this phenomenon, using it as a shock force for social control; figures like Vladimir Zhirinovsky promote its political instrumentalization.
1999
●	Stanislav Orlov is drafted into the Russian Airborne Forces and fights in the Second Chechen War, where he gains experience in asymmetric warfare.
2000s
●	The Nashi youth movement uses hooligans as security personnel.
●	The FSB, according to reports, fosters the creation of the All-Russian Supporters Association to channel and monitor fan groups.
2010
●	December: After riots in Moscow’s Manezhnaya Square, the state intensifies the campaign to “tame” or redirect football hooliganism.
2014-2015
●	February: After Maidan and the participation of Ukrainian ultras—especially from the nascent Azov Regiment—the Kremlin perceives the military utility of Russian hooligans.
●	Orlov travels to the Donbas and joins Igor Bezler (“Bes”), standing out for his language abilities and coordination of foreign mercenaries.
●	He founds the “Skull and Bones” reconnaissance unit, integrating rival Spartak Moscow hooligans.
●	Participates in urban combat near Horlivka and Debaltseve.
●	The “Shield and Sword” Association is registered as a legal entity and begins to operate as an umbrella for patriotic veterans and athletes.
2015
●	Orlov joins the “Union of Donbas Volunteers” under the Kremlin umbrella, led by Alexander Khodakovsky.
2016
●	September: Orlov and others appear implicated in the failed Montenegrin coup attempt against Milo Đukanović, after which he temporarily disappears from the public eye.
●	The Fan ID ticket system is introduced for stadiums: many hooligans are excluded and seek new forms to channel their violence.
2017
●	“Shield and Sword” launches social initiatives and receives public thanks from Vladimir Putin on Unity Day.
2014-2021
●	Four major supporter tribes take shape: hooligans, ultras, okolofutbolshchiki, and kuzmichi.
●	“Shield and Sword” organizes patriotic forums and leagues, consolidating links between sport, politics, and paramilitarism.
2022
●	February 24: The Russian invasion begins; Orlov gathers former comrades and founds Española under the Vostok Battalion of the self-proclaimed DPR.
●	They take part in the brutal Mariupol siege and fighting at Azovstal. Amidst the chaos, they appropriate lion cubs as symbolic “trophies.”
●	Briefly integrate into the 114th Independent Motorized Rifle Brigade.
●	“Shield and Sword” promotes the creation of the “Uranus” training center.
2023
●	February: Española officially declares itself a PMC, claiming autonomy and national reach.
●	March: Formally splits into military and humanitarian branches; Vyacheslav Subbotin (reconnaissance) is killed.
●	Spring: Begins operating under Redut (a PMC linked to the Russian Ministry of Defense).
●	July: Runs technical recruitment campaigns—seeking engineers and ICT specialists—and events in Moscow.
●	August: Andrey Solomatin (“Soloma”), former Russian national footballer, joins.
●	September: CSKA Moscow hockey teams display Spanish Brigade symbols on their uniforms.
●	October-November: Redut reveals recruitment drives for women, expanding combatant profiles.
●	December: Top-level football coaches visit brigade positions on the front.
2024
●	First half: Notable participation in the battle for Avdiivka.
●	March: Media confirm economic ties with the Rotenberg oligarchs—via Viktor Shendrik, head of security at Russian Railways.
●	March: Vyacheslav Subbotin, chief of intelligence, is killed.
●	October: Formal constitution as the 88th Reconnaissance and Assault Brigade of the “Volunteer Corps.”
●	November 5: Pavel “Joker” Apalkov is killed in fighting towards Chasiv Yar.
●	November 7: British sanctions are imposed against the PMC Española and Stanislav Orlov.
●	November: Various international reports detail the neo-Nazi character and public/private financing of the formation.
●	June 23–24 (possibly 2025, according to some sources): Ukrainian Neptune missiles reportedly hit command facilities in Crimea, though the brigade denies casualties.
2025
●	May–July: Western intelligence reports and OSINT platforms focus on Española as a potential replacement for PMC Wagner after the depletion of Wagner Group.
●	July: Internal and external disputes over the unit’s narrative and documentation reach open platforms like Wikipedia.


Final Commentary: Legacy and Perspectives
The chronology of Española illustrates how the Russian state has been able to instrumentalize—and at the same time become hostage to—the forces and symbols that arise from the social margins, such as football hooliganism and identity extremism. The journey of the brigade, from the stands to the war front, shows the plasticity of Russian power to turn “useful” violence into an armed wing and, simultaneously, the difficulty of breaking free from its own ideological monsters.
What began as a semi-clandestine experiment in organized violence ended with an internationally sanctioned PMC, granted mixed funding (public, oligarchic, and “community”), legitimized at sporting events and—paradoxically—presented as the “denazifying” arm of a regime that has made propagandistic flexibility its main resource.
The phenomenon of Española raises fundamental questions for the future of the Russian-Ukrainian conflict and for the global cycle of militarizing extremist subcultures. How far can the state tolerate, absorb, or even promote groups whose ideological convictions contradict the official discourse? Is this model exportable or are we witnessing an anomaly of the post-Soviet Russian crucible?
The evolution of Española is, in the end, a warning: in modern war, the boundaries between state, crime, politics, and sport can dissolve until they disappear. The “hooligans” at the front are only the most visible sign of a society where organized violence has found new legitimacies.
