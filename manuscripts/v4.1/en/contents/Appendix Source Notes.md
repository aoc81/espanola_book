# Appendix — Source Notes

These notes identify the principal source chains behind the book's major factual claims, attributed allegations, chronological anchors, and key interpretive passages. They are designed as a proportional source apparatus for an open-source analytical book rather than as exhaustive academic annotation. Readers seeking to verify or extend a specific claim should follow the footnotes embedded in each chapter first, then use this appendix to locate the broader reporting and analytical context that informs the chapter's framing.

One methodological rule governs all source use in this project and applies across all sections below. Telegram and VK material preserved in the project archive are used to establish self-presentation, rhetoric, recruitment language, memorial culture, fundraising posture, and the timing of public announcements. They are not treated on their own as decisive proof of battlefield performance, internal command relationships, patronage structures, or criminal allegations. Where those matters appear in the chapters, they are sourced to independent reporting, registry material, or investigative journalism, not to the formation's own channels.

---

## Prologue

The Prologue draws on the cluster of reporting that appeared in December 2025 around Orlov's death and the subsequent funeral. The most important sources are The Guardian, El País, ASTRA/Current Time (via Radio Svoboda), Novaya Gazeta Europe's "Командир-200," and RTVI's insider account. These sources do not agree on the cause of death, and the Prologue does not resolve the disagreement — it presents the absence of resolution as itself significant. The material about the formation's public identity, Redut-linked administration, and general wartime structure is sourced to the fuller reporting chains described in the chapter-level notes below.

## Chapter 1 — Before the War: The Stadium as a Political Incubator

The account of Russian football supporter culture, hooligan organization, and the nationalist-subculture overlap draws primarily on Cherta Media's profile of the formation ("Кто такие ЧВК «Эспаньола»? Фанаты на войне"), BBC News Russian's investigation "Армия на полставки," and the Dialnet academic analysis of hooligan culture preserved in the project archive ("La Amenaza Hooligan," local PDF). The Manezhnaya Square riot of December 2010 is widely documented in Russian and international press; the Cherta and BBC materials provide the framing that connects it to the later wartime mobilization of similar milieus.

References to Fan ID legislation and its effect on supporter subculture are analytical inferences built on those reporting strands and the broader archive of press coverage of Russian stadium-control policy. They should be read as interpreted context rather than as directly sourced fact.

## Chapters 2 and 4 — Orlov's Biography and Donbas as Laboratory

Orlov's trajectory through the CSKA milieu, into military service, to Donbas under Bezler-linked structures, and then through the Union of Donbas Volunteers relies on a cluster of overlapping sources: Novaya Gazeta Europe's August 2023 profile ("Появились «футболисты» в окопах под огнем"), the Jamestown Foundation's analytical work on Española and on Orlov specifically, and supporting archive material.

The claim about the Skull and Bones unit — its role, composition, and battlefield significance — is sourced to the same cluster and is subject to the caveat stated in both chapters: the most detailed accounts of its effectiveness come from sources with an interest in Orlov's legend. The Montenegro allegation appears in Jamestown and intelligence-adjacent archive material and is treated throughout as an unresolved allegation rather than established biographical fact.

The Horlivka setting draws on BBC News Russian and Novaya Gazeta Europe. Igor Bezler is a documented figure in journalistic coverage of the early Donbas conflict; his role is described in terms consistent with that coverage without overstating the precision of the available account.

## Chapter 3 — From Terrace to Trench

Claims about cross-club composition and the suspension of rival identities under a wartime frame are based on the formation's own self-presentation, its recruitment rhetoric, and later reporting that described the brigade as drawing from multiple supporter communities. The Mariupol stadium scene — the core visual anchor of this chapter — is sourced entirely to the Daily Sabah / AFP-style feature of July 2023 ("Battle on pitch: Russian football ultras take arms in Mariupol"), preserved in the local archive. Numerical claims in that article (approximately 600 members; training near the Azov Sea) are treated as reported figures, not verified strength counts.

The claim that the public record is too thin to map internal social mechanics is not a limitation introduced by the chapter — it is the honest state of the evidence.

## Chapters 5 and 9 — Branding, Iconography, and the Media Operation

The visual and symbolic analysis of Española's brand — pirate motifs, skull imagery, founder-derived naming, anti-Azov framing — draws on the brigade's own Telegram channels (t.me/spainrus and affiliated channels), the GNET analysis of the formation's online activities ("Española: Russia's Far-Right Hooligan Militia and Its Online Activities"), the Regnum photo essay, and Meduza/Cherta reporting. Statements about the propaganda function of specific post types are interpretive syntheses of those materials rather than quotes from any single source. All interpretive claims are framed accordingly in the prose.

## Chapter 6 — The Humanitarian Wing

The military/humanitarian distinction, the named intermediaries (Khanin, Trifonov), and the Horlivka football project are sourced primarily to Cherta Media's profile and related Meduza archive material. The Horlivka youth-football example is described as bearing the formation's visual identity on the basis of that reporting; if more specific photographic documentation becomes available, the chapter's account should be updated to cite it directly.

The claim that "humanitarian" in this context covered military logistics, legitimacy, and social expansion is an interpretive synthesis from the available material. It is stated as an analytical observation rather than as a quote from any source.

## Chapter 7 — Contracts, Brokers, and the Redut Interface

Redut-linked contract and recruitment descriptions draw on the BBC News Russian investigation ("Армия на полставки"), Jamestown Foundation reporting, and Ukrainska Pravda's account of women's recruitment into Redut-linked formations ("Russian Redut Private Military Company recruits women into assault units for war"). The broader account of Redut as a GRU-linked recruitment proxy draws on Meduza's October 2023 summary of the Sistema/Skhemy investigation ("Why does GRU need a PMC?"). The Dobrocor reorganization is described on the basis of Meduza's July 2025 reporting ("Russian military intelligence develops new mercenary deployment scheme").

The exact chain of command above Redut remains opaque in public sources and is described conservatively throughout. The chapter does not claim a fully reconstructed command pyramid.

## Chapter 8 — Who Paid for the Brigade?

Funding and patronage claims at the public level draw on archived Telegram channel material, MEMRI's archive capture of fundraising appeals, and Cherta/Meduza reporting on the formation's financial self-presentation.

The higher-level patronage claim — linking the brigade's support environment to Viktor Shendrik, Russian Railways, and the Rotenberg orbit — relies on Meduza's summary of iStories reporting ("Española's patron revealed"). This claim is attributed to that reporting throughout and is not presented as judicially established fact. The Ukrainian Defence Intelligence statement about United Russia and Española is treated as a hostile-state attribution — relevant evidence of how the formation's political function was read by an adversarial government — rather than as independent corroboration of the Meduza/iStories patronage chain.

## Chapter 10 — Faces of the Formation

Turkanov, Solomatin, and other visible personalities are described on the basis of Jamestown Foundation analysis, media profiles, Regnum photographic material, and Telegram channel content. The OpenSanctions entries for Turkanov and for PMC Española are used as documentary evidence of formal exposure and sanctions status; they are not treated as biographical narrative beyond the identifiers they record.

The identification of far-right-adjacent symbolism in Turkanov's public image is sourced to GNET and Jamestown reporting; it is stated as those outlets' finding rather than as direct authorial assessment.

## Chapter 11 — The Far Right in Uniform

The ideological analysis draws on visual evidence, GNET's systematic analysis of the formation's online imagery and symbolism, Jamestown reporting, and Meduza/Cherta material. Searchlight's September 2024 piece ("Mixed fortunes for Russia's far right assets") is used for comparative ecological framing — placing Española within the broader landscape of Russian far-right assets after 2022 — and is noted as anti-fascist monitoring journalism rather than neutral registry material.

The chapter does not treat every member of the formation as ideologically identical, and that restraint should be preserved in any future revision.

## Chapters 12 and 13 — The Military Experiment and Battlefield Reputation

Technical adaptation and specialist recruitment claims are supported unevenly across self-presentation, sympathetic commentary, and outside analytical reporting. The chapter emphasizes reputation and self-presentation because the underlying capability cannot be fully verified from open sources; that is a methodological choice, not an evasion.

Women's recruitment is placed in wider context using Novaya Gazeta Europe's "Women at war" (August 2023) and Ukrainska Pravda's Redut recruitment reporting; these are not treated as specific evidence about Española's internal composition.

Battlefield associations — Mariupol, Bakhmut, Avdiivka — are established through a combination of outside reporting and the brigade's own channel material. The chapter is careful to distinguish between documented presence in or near these theaters and the stronger claims about tactical significance that the formation's own channels made. Numerical and capability claims from the Daily Sabah Mariupol piece remain attributed to that source.

## Chapters 15, 16, and 17 — Post-Wagner, Exposure, and State Recapture

The post-Wagner political context draws on BBC News Russian, Jamestown Foundation series on the elimination of semi-autonomous volunteer formations, and Meduza/Sistema reporting on Dobrocor. The role attributed to General Alekseev is sourced to the Jamestown Foundation's reporting on the assassination attempt against him and to Meduza's summary of the Sistema investigation; it is treated as reported and attributed throughout.

UK sanctions references are cross-read with OpenSanctions registry entries (PMC Española; Mikhail Victorovich Turkanov) and UNITED24 Media's November 2024 summary. These are used as exposure evidence — proof that the formation had crossed into formal administrative databases — rather than as investigative findings.

Inter-formation frictions (Akhmat and others) are treated throughout as unevenly sourced and analytically useful for pattern-reading rather than as settled accounts of specific episodes.

## Chapter 18 — Disbandment, Death, and the Uses of Uncertainty

The dissolution of Española in October 2025 is documented through The Insider's reporting, Kyiv Post's coverage, and the brigade's own public statements on 6 October 2025. The political reaction is described using Meduza's October 6, 2025 account ("'It's clear the system doesn't need smart people'"). The chapter notes that on the same day, a Volunteer Corps political officer publicly insisted the brigade was still performing tasks — a simultaneous claim of dissolution and continuity treated as evidence of Russian wartime organizational opacity rather than contradiction requiring resolution. The naval detachment continuation and Melodiya's spin-out as a separate structure are sourced to the brigade's own channel messaging and to GNET's prior description of Melodiya's function.

The post-dissolution brand ecosystem — veterans union, Fund "Española," MotoGuard security and training structures, civilian-military schools in Moscow — is sourced to official Española channel activity in early 2026, Fund "Española" corporate-registry presence, and MotoGuard's description on the official channel as a civilian-military security enterprise. These structures are presented as evidence of brand survival, not as evidence that the old 88th brigade continues in any operational form.

Orlov's death in December 2025 is documented through a four-date sequence: December 9 (initial claims via Tsargrad and pro-war channels), December 19 (official channel confirmation), December 22 (funeral at Cathedral of Christ the Saviour). Sources are ASTRA/Current Time reporting, RTVI's insider accounts, Novaya Gazeta Europe's "Командир-200," The Guardian's December 2025 feature, El País's coverage, and The Moscow Times. These sources offer partially incompatible accounts of the circumstances. The chapter does not resolve the incompatibility; it treats the unresolved state of the record as itself analytically significant. Sevastopol is named as the reported location of Orlov's final weeks and death, consistent with the naval detachment's Crimea connections.

Criminal allegations — weapons trafficking and related charges — are described as allegations reported by specific outlets and are not stated as established facts in the author's own voice. That framing must be maintained in all future editions unless new reporting substantially changes the evidentiary base.

## Epilogue

The synthesis argument — that Española exemplified a method of outsourcing irregular force to cultural communities the state did not build, then reclaiming administrative control when the balance of interests changed — is authorial synthesis from the source chains listed above. It is not attributed to any single outlet because no single outlet made it; it is the analytical conclusion that emerges from treating the full record in combination.

The epilogue's updated claim — that the method survives its practitioner not only as precedent but in active institutional form — is grounded in the early-2026 brand ecosystem described in Chapter 18 source notes above. The persistence of Fund "Española," MotoGuard, veterans union activity, and civilian-military training operates as concrete evidence that organizational survival is underway, not only analytical inference.
