# A Note on Method and Its Limits

The source environment around Española is not clean. It could not be. The formation operated through deliberate ambiguity, cultivated a propaganda apparatus that made its public record inseparable from its self-promotion, and existed inside a wartime information ecosystem in which every actor — Russian state media, Ukrainian intelligence, international journalists, pro-war Telegram channels, opposition outlets, anti-fascist monitors, and sanctions databases — had interests that shaped the material they produced. None of those interests are identical. None of the sources they generated are equally reliable. The method of this book depends on knowing that.

The source base spans Russian-, Ukrainian-, and English-language materials. It includes investigative journalism, mainstream and specialist reporting, open-source analytical writing, sanctions and registry documents, archived webpages, platform captures, and Telegram and VK content preserved in the project archive. The archive was built iteratively over the period of research described in the Author's Note, with sources checked against each other and claims followed across multiple reporting chains before they were accepted as usable.

Claims in this book fall into four distinct categories, and the prose is designed to make clear which category a given claim belongs to.

The first category is facts that can be stated directly — claims supported across multiple credible and independent sources, confirmed by documentary evidence, or established in the public record beyond reasonable dispute.

The second category is source-dependent claims — findings that rest on a specific reporting chain, analytical source, or investigative outlet, and that are therefore attributed explicitly in the text and in the endnotes. If the underlying source is wrong, the claim is wrong with it. Readers should follow the attributions and evaluate the sources independently.

The third category is contested or weakly corroborated claims — matters that appear in some reporting but lack sufficient independent corroboration, that are disputed between sources, or that sit near the edge of what the available evidence can establish. These are described in the prose using language such as alleged, reported, disputed, or unresolved. They are included where relevant but marked clearly.

The fourth category is self-description and propaganda — statements made by Española itself, by affiliated channels, by sympathetic commentary, or by the broader pro-war information ecosystem. These are treated as evidence of rhetoric, self-presentation, timing, and the formation's own identity claims. They are not accepted as stand-alone proof of disputed events, battlefield performance, or contested institutional relationships.

Telegram and VK material are used throughout this project for a specific and limited purpose: to establish the formation's public language, symbolic vocabulary, recruitment posture, fundraising rhetoric, and memorial culture, and to document the timing of public announcements and claims. They are not treated, on their own, as conclusive proof of battlefield performance, internal command relationships, patronage networks, or criminal conduct.

This is not a book that claims total verification. It is a book that aims for proportionate confidence: saying what can be said firmly, attributing what cannot be settled, and marking the limits of the record when those limits matter to the argument. Where uncertainty is preserved in the prose, that preservation is intentional.
