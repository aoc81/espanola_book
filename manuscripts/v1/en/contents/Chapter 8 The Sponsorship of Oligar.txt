Chapter 8: The Sponsorship of Oligarchs and the Russian State
The hidden networks of power and finance sustaining irregular warfare

The operational sustainability of Española transcends the self-financing capacities of any irregular formation, no matter how effective it is at recruitment and combat operations. Its ability to maintain sophisticated equipment, extensive training programs, and competitive compensation reflects the existence of complex financing networks that connect directly to the highest circles of economic and political power in the Russian Federation. These links reveal both the integration of the brigade into the Russian imperial project and the inherent contradictions of a system that must balance plausible deniability with effective support.
The Rotenberg Brothers: Oligarchs in the Shadows
In March 2024, investigations by independent media, including reports from Meduza, identified the oligarchs Arkady and Boris Rotenberg as the likely primary sponsors of Española. This revelation was not surprising for observers familiar with the financing patterns of Russian irregular formations but provided concrete confirmation of connections that had previously been the subject of speculation.
The Rotenberg brothers, close friends of Vladimir Putin since their youth in St. Petersburg, represent the archetype of the Putinist oligarch: individuals whose wealth derives directly from proximity to political power and whose investments serve geostrategic objectives as much as they do financial returns. Their interest in Española cannot be understood as mere military philanthropy, but as part of a broader strategy to develop private power capabilities that complement but retain relative independence from the formal state apparatus.
The Rotenbergs’ motivation for funding the brigade, according to sources cited in the reports, included the desire “to have their own private army.” This ambition reflects broader trends among the Russian oligarchic elite, where access to organized violence capabilities has become an important component of personal security and political power projection.
However, the Rotenbergs’ sponsorship also creates potential vulnerabilities for the brigade. Dependence on private financing, while providing operational flexibility, also exposes the formation to changes in priorities or the financial capacities of its sponsors. Additionally, these private connections could generate tensions with state authorities who prefer direct control over significant military formations.
Viktor Shendrik: The Crucial Intermediary
The practical mechanics of Rotenberg financing operate through Viktor Shendrik, head of the security department of Russian Railways (RZD), who acts as an intermediary between the oligarchs and the brigade. This intermediary structure reflects typical patterns of Russian oligarchic operations, where direct links are avoided in favor of chains of responsibility that provide plausible deniability.
Shendrik brings unique credentials to this role. His prior experience in security, including work in the secret “Vympel” unit of the FSB, gives him familiarity with clandestine operations and an understanding of operational security requirements. His current position at Russian Railways, one of Russia’s most important state-owned companies, grants access to logistical and financial resources that would be difficult to replicate via purely private channels.
Using Russian Railways as a funding vehicle also provides important institutional camouflage. As a state company involved in many aspects of national infrastructure, RZD can justify various expenses that would be more difficult to explain if issued directly from private entities. This structure allows for financial flows that maintain the appearance of legitimate business while serving clandestine military objectives.
However, Shendrik’s centrality in these operations also creates single point of failure vulnerabilities. His removal—whether through political change, legal investigation, or personal misfortune—could significantly disrupt the brigade’s financing mechanisms.
Redut: The State Interface
In parallel with oligarchic financing, Española operates within state structures through its subordination to Redut, the “phantom” PMC linked to Russia’s Ministry of Defense and the GRU. This dual relationship enables the brigade to simultaneously access both private and state resources, creating redundancy in financing that increases operational sustainability.
Redut essentially functions as a management company handling administrative, logistical, and financial functions for multiple irregular formations. For Española, this structure means fighters can sign formal contracts, receive regular payments from the state budget, and access military-grade equipment—all while maintaining the legal fiction of independence.
Redut’s structure also facilitates coordination among different funding sources. State resources can complement private funds for specific capabilities, while oligarchic funding can support activities that would be politically sensitive if financed directly by the state. This financial flexibility allows optimization of resources across different operational requirements.
However, operating through Redut also imposes certain constraints on the brigade’s autonomy. Contractual relationships with state entities require compliance with bureaucratic procedures that can limit operational flexibility, while oversight requirements may compromise some of the independence that makes irregular formations effective.
Lieutenant General Alekseyev: The GRU Connection
The presence of Lieutenant General Vladimir Stepanovich Alekseyev, Deputy Chief of the GRU, at ceremonies where Spanish Brigade members receive state decorations, provides clear visual evidence of connections between the brigade and the highest levels of Russian military intelligence. These ceremonies are not private or informal events but official activities involving representatives of the upper echelons of the security apparatus.
Alekseyev’s participation in such ceremonies signals institutional approval at the highest levels of the military intelligence hierarchy. As Deputy Chief of the GRU, Alekseyev would not attend such events without clear authorization from senior leadership, indicating that support for the brigade extends beyond individual commanders and into institutional policy.
This connection also suggests that the brigade provides intelligence value justifying high-level attention. Irregular formations like Española can access information and conduct operations that would be difficult for conventional military units, providing intelligence capabilities that complement formal GRU operations.
However, such visible connections also create potential complications for Russian deniability strategies. Photographic evidence of senior GRU officials decorating members of formations with explicit neo-Nazi affiliations complicates narratives about “denazification” goals in Ukraine.
Broader Corporate Sponsorship: The PMC Ecosystem
Española’s financing exists within a broader ecosystem where major Russian corporations and state agencies provide support for various irregular formations. While Española operates through Redut and receives backing from the Rotenbergs, other formations have developed different sponsorship relationships that showcase broader patterns.
Roscosmos, the Russian space agency, has been identified as the funder of the Uran battalion, while Sberbank and Rosatom reportedly support the Soyuz unit. These connections suggest a systematic approach to corporate sponsorship of irregular formations, going beyond individual oligarch preferences and toward institutional policy.
This distribution of sponsorship across different corporate entities also provides resilience for the overall system of irregular formations. Disruption of any single sponsorship relationship does not compromise the entire ecosystem, while multiple funding sources create redundancy that increases sustainability.
However, these corporate connections also create potential vulnerabilities to sanctions regimes. International sanctions targeting specific corporations or individuals can disrupt funding flows, while sanctions compliance by international partners of Russian corporations may limit access to certain types of equipment or services.
Mechanisms of Financial Transfer
The practical mechanics of transferring funds from various sponsors to the brigade involve complex networks designed to maintain plausible deniability while ensuring reliable resource flows. These mechanisms typically involve multiple intermediary entities, shell corporations, and legitimate business activities that provide cover for military financing.
Financial transfers may utilize combinations of consulting contracts, security services agreements, equipment procurement deals, and charitable donations that create legitimate business justifications for resource flows. These structures require sophisticated legal and financial expertise to establish and maintain, indicating institutional support that goes beyond individual initiative.
Cash flows may also be supplemented with in-kind support including equipment, training, logistical services, and access to facilities that provide value without requiring direct financial transfers. This diversification in support types increases flexibility while reducing traceable financial connections.
However, complexity in financial mechanisms also creates potential vulnerabilities. Sophisticated transfer systems require multiple participants who may become security risks, while complex structures may be more susceptible to disruption if key components are compromised.
Sustainability and Vulnerabilities
Española’s current financing structure demonstrates remarkable resilience through the combination of multiple funding sources, but it also contains potential vulnerabilities that could affect long-term sustainability. Dependence on oligarch financing creates exposure to changes in political relationships, economic conditions, or individual priorities that could affect resource availability.
State funding through Redut provides more stability but also imposes constraints on operational independence that may reduce some of the advantages associated with irregular formations. The balance between sustainability and autonomy represents an ongoing challenge for formations that attempt to maintain distinctive capabilities while accessing state resources.
International sanctions regimes present increasing threats to financing networks as Western governments develop a more sophisticated understanding of Russian irregular formation structures. Targeted sanctions against specific individuals, corporations, or financial mechanisms could significantly disrupt current arrangements.
The brigade’s future sustainability, therefore, likely depends on the continued evolution of financing mechanisms that can adapt to changing political, economic, and security environments while maintaining the resource flows necessary for operational effectiveness.
In the next chapter, we will examine how these financial resources are channeled through the brigade’s “humanitarian wing” toward specific operational capabilities, and how fundraising activities create additional connections between the formation and broader Russian society.
