# A Note on AI Assistance

AI tools were used throughout the preparation of this manuscript. Their role was extensive in some areas and limited in others, and being precise about the distinction matters.

On the extensive side: AI assisted with organizing and cross-referencing large source sets, with translation support across Russian- and Ukrainian-language material, with structural testing of the manuscript's logic and sequence, and with editorial refinement of the prose. It helped identify repetition, tighten transitions, and improve the readability of passages that had grown unwieldy through multiple drafts. In the final stages of revision, it contributed substantially to the literary rewriting process that produced the different drafts without altering the underlying factual claims or their evidentiary basis.

A fuller account of the tools may be useful to readers interested in the workflow. For source organisation and the production of study guides, briefings, and mind maps, I used NotebookLM (Google). For independent fact-checking and research expansion, I used ChatGPT Deep Research (OpenAI), Gemini Deep Research and Perplexity Deep Research, cross-referencing between the three. For prose drafting, grammar correction, and AI-assisted paragraph generation from notes and prompt directions, Claude (Anthropic) was my primary working tool throughout. Cover imagery was produced using Claude Design and ChatGPT Images 2.0 (OpenAI). The manuscript itself was written in Markdown format inside VS Code — a code editor that, as it turned out, offered a more controllable environment for a long-form analytical project than a conventional word processor. From there, own scripts and the support of coding agents handled web searches for licence-free images, document assembly, automated indexing, citation formatting, and final export to Word format. I also created the guiding documents for those agents, including a Source Usage and Narrative Integration Protocol (agent.md), a set of writing-behaviour rules (book-writing-protocol.md), and a dedicated editorial-review instruction set assigning one agent the role of editor-in-chief, with responsibility for evaluating narrative force, evidentiary discipline, and overall publishability. All project files, including those documents, are available in a public repository at https://github.com/aoc81/espanola_book

On the limited side: AI was not an authority on facts, and no claim in this book appears because a language model asserted it. The underlying source research — the collection and archiving of materials, the comparative evaluation of sources, the decisions about what was reliable and what was not, the judgments about what could be stated firmly and what had to be attributed or marked uncertain — remained human responsibilities throughout. Where a model produced confident-sounding language that exceeded what the source record supported, the correction was human. Where the record was genuinely ambiguous, the instruction to the model was to preserve that ambiguity rather than paper over it with fluency.

The book is transparent about this because transparency is appropriate. AI assistance is now a normal part of many analytical and writing workflows. The question is not whether it was used but how, and whether the human judgment it was meant to assist remained genuinely in control of the claims the book makes. In this project, it did. Readers are invited to test that claim against the sources.
