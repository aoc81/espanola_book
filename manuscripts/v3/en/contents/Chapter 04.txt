Chapter 4 — Donbas as Laboratory

The temptation in books about wartime formations is to begin where public attention begins. For Espanola, that would mean 2022: the full-scale invasion, the flood of propaganda, the rise of the brand. But that chronology is too neat. The more revealing story begins in the first Donbas war, where irregular fighting, covert state involvement, and militant subcultures created conditions in which something like Espanola could be tested before it was fully named.

In 2014 and 2015, Donbas was not only a battlefield. It was a sorting mechanism. Men arrived there for different reasons: ideology, adventure, loyalty to Russian imperial narratives, access to violence, or the simple gravitational pull of armed networks that had suddenly opened a path out of civilian marginality. The region also provided cover. State interests, separatist structures, veteran networks, and semi-formal volunteer formations overlapped in ways that blurred responsibility while creating room for experimentation.

Orlov's reported service under Igor Bezler belongs to that phase. So does the formation known as Skull and Bones, which multiple accounts link to him. Whatever the exact internal composition of that unit, its significance lies in the type of work it represented: reconnaissance, small-group discipline, local legitimacy earned through combat, and the translation of supporter-world cohesion into battlefield function. It is here that one sees the first durable pattern. Hooligan culture did not merely supply manpower. It supplied men already accustomed to operating in tight, status-conscious, aggressive groups.

Donbas also taught a harder lesson about institutions. Improvised militancy alone was not enough. A unit that wanted to survive needed relationships upward: commanders, supply channels, patrons, administrative shelter, and eventually access to structures that were not fully official but not fully private either. This is one reason Orlov's later movement into the Union of Donbas Volunteers matters so much. The organization sat in the space where volunteer legitimacy, Kremlin management, and veteran identity could be joined. It was not just a holding zone for old fighters. It was part of the machinery through which irregular force became politically useful.

This is what makes Donbas a laboratory rather than just a prehistory. It produced personnel, tested tactics, and created the middle layer between the street and the state. It showed how men from football and nationalist milieus could be used in war without immediate full incorporation into regular command. It showed how local myth and battlefield reputation could substitute, for a time, for more formal institutional credibility. It also showed that the state could profit from opacity. The less precise the structure looked from the outside, the more room there was for deniability and adaptation.

The anti-Azov frame also becomes legible here. Even if later promotional language exaggerated the symmetry, the idea of a Russian formation emerging from hooligan and far-right milieus in opposition to a Ukrainian formation often portrayed the same way was not accidental. Donbas provided the first theater in which that mirrored logic could develop. It offered a story not only of combat, but of rival models of subcultural militarization.

By the time the full-scale invasion began, then, the raw elements of Espanola already existed. There was a founder whose callsign could become a brand, a precedent for turning ultras into combatants, a memory of Donbas as formative war, and a set of administrative and political networks linking militants to state-adjacent structures. What 2022 changed was scale. The war no longer needed only covert experiments and local detachments. It needed reproducible models of mobilization.

To call Donbas a laboratory is also to reject the flattering narrative of spontaneous emergence. Espanola did not appear from nowhere when the invasion widened. It had been rehearsed in smaller form years earlier, in a conflict where ambiguity was not a byproduct but a method. The later brigade would inherit that method: part volunteer legend, part military instrument, part political object.

What remained was to turn this inheritance into a name, an emblem, and a recognisable identity that could travel beyond the battlefield.
