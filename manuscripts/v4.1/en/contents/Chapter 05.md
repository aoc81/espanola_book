# Chapter 5 — Rebranding Violence: The Birth of Española

Consider the problem of naming a wartime unit. A numbered formation — the 88th this, the 7th that — belongs immediately to a bureaucracy. It conveys function and assignment. It does not convey personality, story, or a reason to choose this unit over another. In a war where volunteer formations were competing for recruits, donors, and attention, the name Española solved that problem at a stroke. It sounded personal. It was built around a man — or rather around the alias of a man, which is even better, since an alias already implies a story, a history of use, a persona refined through experience. A battalion called Española was not asking you to join a military structure. It was asking you to enter a world that already had a character.[^ch05-002]

That naming logic was visible in every element of the brigade's early self-presentation. In February 2023, the unit publicly adopted the language of a PMC — a branding step rather than a legal status, since Russia has no formal PMC category in the ordinary sense, but one that signaled the formation was now presenting itself in terms that Western sanctions regimes and international media would find legible. Across Telegram channels, on patches and insignia, in the photography that affiliated channels circulated and archived, the same elements recurred with too much regularity to be coincidental: pirate motifs, skull imagery, black and white as dominant colors, a rough aesthetic that suggested outlaw autonomy rather than institutional belonging. The visual language did not invent itself. It was drawn from a recognizable repertoire — part supporter culture, part criminal subculture, part the general iconography of masculine marginality that has traveled across different violent milieus for decades — and it was applied with a consistency that indicated someone had thought about the effect.[^ch05-001] [^ch05-003]

What that effect accomplished was specific. It gave the formation a look that could do several things at once: attract men from the supporter milieu who already read these symbols fluently; signal to a wider audience of wartime nationalists and online patriots that this unit was not a conventional assignment; and preserve a sense of chosen distinctiveness even as the brigade operated inside the Russian military system. The pirate flag on a wartime channel header is not an ideological statement in any precise sense. It is a mood, a posture, an invitation to imagine yourself as someone outside ordinary rules. In a war being conducted by a state, that invitation was carefully useful. It made the formation feel less like an arm of the state than the state's alternative.[^ch05-003]

The anti-Azov framing completed the picture. Española's channels and its sympathizers returned repeatedly to the idea that the brigade was a Russian answer to Azov — a Ukrainian formation that had built its own brand out of nationalist symbolism, supporter-world roots, far-right ideology, and military professionalism, and that had become a central object in Russian official propaganda. The comparison was propagandistic and should not be accepted at face value. But its function is clear and worth taking seriously. By positioning the brigade as a counterpart to Azov, its proponents were making a claim about seriousness, not merely about opposition. They were saying: we are the side that can produce this. We have our own men who came from the streets and made themselves into fighters, our own symbols, our own mythology, our own martyrs. The war was not only military. Its outcome depended partly on which side could produce the more compelling story of itself.[^ch05-005]

The name Española — Spanish — carried its own layer of history into that competition. It referred to Orlov's callsign, but the callsign itself is said to have arisen from his Spanish-language ability and his connection to foreign volunteers. In a war in which Russia maintained that it was fighting on behalf of a broader civilizational interest, the presence of foreigners inside Russian formations was politically legible, and a formation named after a callsign with Spanish resonance was readable, however loosely, as a cosmopolitan entity rather than a narrowly Russian one. Whether that reading was intentional or merely convenient is less important than the fact that it was available.[^ch05-002]

The visual consistency that ran through the brigade's public materials also had practical organizational utility that is easy to underestimate. A group of men from different clubs, different cities, and different networks needed a common visual language to recognize one another as part of the same project. Uniforms and insignia do this in conventional armies through bureaucratic standardization. Española did it through a shared aesthetic: men who wore the formation's patches, who recognized its skull imagery, who posted from its affiliated channels, were performing membership in a way that needed no official registry. They belonged to the brand before they were processed into the legal shell. The brand did the organizing work.[^ch05-004]

There was also, importantly, a youth dimension to the project. Reporting and archival material point to the Horlivka football project — a boys' football program organized under occupation in the city where Orlov had first operated in 2014 — as bearing Española's visual signature. The same pirate-coded imagery that appeared on patches and channel headers also appeared in connection with children's football. This is a deliberate extension of the brand into civic space: the formation was not only a wartime unit but an institution with a presence in education, sport, and local life. That civic face served the brigade's long-term interests in ways that pure military branding could not. It made the formation look durable and rooted, not merely instrumental and temporary.[^ch05-004]

Two interpretive errors are available here, and both should be resisted. The first is to dismiss the visual world of Española as superficial decoration, a skin over an ordinary military operation. The second is to read every symbol as a coherent ideological confession, a fully articulate statement of political doctrine. The more defensible position is organizational. The brand solved problems. It gave men a common identity across the fault lines of club loyalty. It made the formation legible to potential recruits and donors from a distance. It produced continuity between the trench and the rear, between the fighter and the sympathizer. It turned violence into something followable. That is what brands do, and that is what Española's brand did — in a war, for a formation, at scale.
