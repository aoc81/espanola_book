# Chapter 11 — The Far Right in Uniform

In February 2022, the Russian state justified its invasion of Ukraine with the word "denazification." The claim was that Ukraine's government was controlled by or had been infiltrated by neo-Nazis, that the Azov regiment and formations like it represented a genuine ideological threat that Russian military force would eliminate. The word was chosen carefully. It invoked the moral authority of the Soviet victory in the Great Patriotic War and cast the invasion as a continuation of the historical fight against fascism. It was also, on the evidence available about formations like Española, structurally absurd.[^ch11-005]

Española grew out of milieus in which far-right symbolism, racialized rhetoric, and violent masculine nationalism were not peripheral elements to be explained away. They were atmospheric. Men from CSKA's hooligan environment and from other Russian supporter firms had spent years inside a subculture where xenophobic chants were common, where racial violence at and around matches was documented, and where far-right political currents ran alongside sports tribalism in ways that blended easily. The nationalism of the terrace was not the same as the ideology of a political party, but it was also not ideologically innocent — and it was not ideologically distant from the currents that a formation like Española would later channel.[^ch11-001]

The visual evidence is the clearest starting point because it does not require interpreting intent. Reporting and archived material, including the analyses produced by GNET and the Jamestown Foundation, document tattoos and insignia on visible figures around the brigade that investigators and anti-fascist monitoring organizations identified as associated with neo-Nazi-adjacent subcultures: runic imagery, coded numerical references, symbols with long histories in the European and Russian far right. Turkanov's public image was the most prominent instance. His visible body was, for investigators with the appropriate literacy, a text. It said specific things about where he had been and what worlds he had moved through. For his admirers inside the formation, those same markings read as toughness, authenticity, and the marks of a man who had not softened himself for outside comfort.[^ch11-002]

That reading is reinforced by a documented fact that predates the war. In 2019, according to Cherta Media's reporting, Turkanov was fined for the public display of Nazi symbols — a charge distinct from the broader impression created by tattoos and aesthetic associations, because it involved a legal proceeding and a finding. He was not being categorized by investigators studying photographs from a distance. He was being adjudicated by the Russian legal system for a specific public act. The fine was not the outcome of a political prosecution against a war hero. It preceded Española's public formation by several years. Whatever the ideological content of that act, it sits in the record as the kind of concrete, datable, legally documented fact that moves a claim from "investigators say" into a more verifiable register.[^ch11-002b]

The brigade's later designation as the 88th brigade introduced a related question that the available evidence does not resolve cleanly. In the lexicon of far-right numerology, "88" encodes a specific salute; the association is long established and is well known to anyone who monitors extremist symbolism. Whether that association was deliberate at the institutional level — whether the 88th designation was a coded signal embedded by someone who intended it to be read that way — is a different question from whether the number carried that meaning to some of those who served under it. The honest assessment, on the evidence available, is that intent at the institutional level cannot be established from open sources. Members with far-right affiliations may have appreciated the number; state structures assigning organizational labels do not typically publish their numerological reasoning. Low confidence is the correct calibration here.[^ch11-002c]

Beyond individual markers, journalistic and analytical reporting placed the formation in a wider ecological context. GNET and Meduza material drew connections between Española and other Russian far-right formations and environments, including Rusich — a formation with overtly neo-Nazi leadership and symbols — and circles adjacent to the Russian Imperial Movement. The connections described were not of the kind that would make every Española fighter ideologically identical to the most extreme elements in those networks. The formations were distinct, and Española was more adaptable as a public brand than some of the more doctrinaire actors in the same ecosystem. But the connections were real enough to indicate that the brigade existed within a larger world of Russian violent nationalism from which it drew personnel, symbolism, and social recognition.[^ch11-003]

A September 2024 Searchlight piece, surveying the fortunes of Russia's far-right assets after 2022, placed Española within this broader account. Searchlight is an explicitly anti-fascist monitoring publication, which means its framing should be contextualized accordingly — it is advocacy journalism rather than neutral registry material — but its comparative and ecological observations about how different Russian far-right formations fared during the war provide a useful frame for understanding what made Española unusual among its peers. The answer, in that frame, was flexibility. It could speak to the far-right milieu through symbols and personnel while maintaining a public-facing register broad enough to attract donors and followers who would not have described themselves as ideologues.[^ch11-007]

That flexibility was not a contradiction of the brigade's far-right dimension. It was the operationally useful form of it. A state prosecuting a war under a denazification banner needed men who were militant enough to fight at the intensity the war required, nationalist enough to be motivated by the war's stated purpose, and adaptable enough not to make that purpose visibly absurd. Men from far-right-adjacent milieus fitted that requirement — provided the most explicitly ideological language was managed carefully rather than suppressed entirely. The solution Española modeled was not to purge the far-right element but to translate it: pirate imagery rather than swastikas, coded symbols rather than explicit doctrine, roughness rather than declared politics.

Two positions are available that this chapter must decline. One is to describe the brigade as ideologically neutral with unfortunate aesthetics. The available evidence does not support that reading. The symbolism was not accidental, the networks were real, and the milieu was not innocent of its own history. The other position is to read every fighter as a committed ideologue, every symbol as a precise doctrinal statement, every channel post as evidence of a coherent political program. That reading would also be wrong. The brigade was not a party. Its members did not all share identical beliefs. The football-derived nationalism of most of its recruits was ambient rather than programmatic.

What the evidence does support is a description that sits between those extremes: Española emerged from, and remained comfortable inside, a far-right-adjacent social world even when its public language reached for a broader patriotic audience. That combination was one of its defining features. It also produced the structural contradiction that runs through the entire story — a formation whose internal culture had one character fighting a war whose official rhetoric demanded another.
