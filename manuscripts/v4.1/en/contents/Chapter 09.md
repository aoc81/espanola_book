# Chapter 9 — The Propaganda Battalion

A memorial post appears in the Española Telegram channel. A fighter's photograph, callsign listed below it, date of death, brief words about his service. The image is composed. The grief is formal. Below it, within hours, appear responses: condolences from followers who did not know the man personally but who knew the channel, the symbol, the world it represented. Then, a day later, another post: a recruitment notice. Drone operators wanted. Experienced personnel preferred. Apply through the contact listed. The channel moves on.[^ch09-001]

What that sequence illustrates is not merely a communications strategy. It is an organizational system. The memorial held existing followers in place. The grief kept the channel emotionally alive, maintained the sense that real stakes were involved and real people were paying them. The recruitment notice arrived into that charged atmosphere and harvested its energy. A potential recruit who had spent days following the channel — absorbing the memorials, the combat clips, the equipment appeals, the commander messages — already knew the symbols, the moral language, the kinds of men the brigade wanted, and the kinds of roles it was trying to fill. The channel had done his pre-enlistment socialization before any formal contact occurred.[^ch09-003]

Española operated across a network of affiliated channels rather than from a single account. The primary channel at t.me/spainrus served as the institutional voice, but accompanying channels focused on specific personalities, specific functions, or specific audiences. One carried material associated with Turkanov, the fighter known as Pitbull. Another, named something like "our guys," offered more intimate, personal content — the everyday life of the formation rather than its official output. A channel associated with a figure called Valkyria pushed toward a female-facing or gender-diverse recruitment register. Each served a different segment of a potential audience while all operated within the same visual and emotional vocabulary.[^ch09-001] [^ch09-004]

The segmentation was deliberate, and it shows the sophistication of the operation. Supporter-coded content and rough aesthetics attracted men from the hooligan and nationalist milieus who would recognize the references and read the roughness as authenticity. Memorial culture and family-facing material brought in a softer patriotic audience — people invested in the war's meaning who were not recruits themselves but who were potential donors, amplifiers, and emotional supporters. Posts about drone technology, electronic warfare, and reconnaissance specializations projected a different image: not a fraternity of hard men, but a technically competent force engaged with the realities of modern warfare. The foreign-facing or internationally distributed content carried the brand into pro-Russian and far-right networks outside Russia, extending the formation's reach into audiences that added prestige even when they added no fighters.[^ch09-004]

The clips and photographs that circulated through these channels were not documentation in any straightforward sense. They were edited. They were selected. Losses were minimized in the public presentation: deaths appeared in memorial format, which treated individual sacrifice as meaningful and contained rather than as evidence of operational failure. Tactical language was promotional: claims about enemy positions taken, about the effectiveness of particular weapons or methods, about the brigade's role in larger battles, should all be treated as self-serving unless independently corroborated. The public record of the brigade's battlefield performance, as it exists in its own channels, is a record of the image the brigade wanted to project rather than an audited account of what it did.[^ch09-005]

Beyond the channels themselves, the formation used live events to extend its social reach into the sports world in ways that the platform record could amplify but not create on its own. On 1 September 2023, a CSKA Moscow hockey evening was organized in connection with the brigade, with Russian ice hockey player Ivan Fedotov among those present according to the Ukrainian War and Sanctions tracking portal. The event is the kind of thing that a Telegram post alone cannot accomplish: a moment of shared physical presence between figures from the recognized sporting world and a wartime formation, documented and then distributed as evidence of that connection. It gave the brigade something valuable — visibility in a register that football-ultra culture and pro-war media could not supply by themselves.[^ch09-005b]

The formation's intelligence dimension also deserves notice. GNET's analysis identified a structure called Melodiya — an OSINT and analytical center using open sources, pro-Russian human contacts, and liaison — as part of the broader Española ecosystem. By October 2025, when the 88th brigade announced its reorganization, the formation's channels indicated that Melodiya had already been separated into its own independent structure. Whether that separation represented institutional survival or organizational dispersal was ambiguous in the official messaging. What it confirmed was that the formation had invested in intelligence and analytical capacity, not merely in combat and propaganda functions.[^ch09-005c]

That methodological caution does not diminish the channels' evidentiary importance — it specifies what they are evidence of. They show how a wartime formation constructed its public self, what emotional and ideological registers it drew on, how it managed the relationship between loss and recruitment, between combat claim and follower loyalty. The pattern of output across these channels over time is itself an important source: it reveals the brigade's priorities, its intended audience, and the narrative it was trying to sustain through the accumulation of posts.

There was also a practical dimension to the media operation that is easy to overlook from outside. A Telegram channel that runs a successful drone-funding appeal is not only raising money. It is demonstrating organizational seriousness to a watching audience. Every successful campaign — every vehicle funded, every piece of equipment named and acquired — was narrated as evidence that the formation worked, that it could be trusted with donations, that its claims about need were accurate and its gratitude was genuine. The propaganda function and the logistics function were the same function.[^ch09-002]

Seen in this light, publicity was not a supplement to organization. It was organization. The followers in Moscow, Saint Petersburg, Yekaterinburg, and across the Russian diaspora who engaged with these channels were not passive audiences being entertained by a wartime drama. They were participants in a social structure that the formation needed: for money, for prestige, for the emotional continuity that keeps a fighting unit symbolically alive even when its fighters are under fire and its channel goes quiet for a day. The brigade could not have operated at the scale it did without the audience. And the audience would not have remained the audience without the media system that gave it something to follow.
