Chapter 2 — Building the Machine
How Española became an organization
A violent subculture can produce fighters. That alone does not make it a durable formation. Plenty of men can be gathered by grievance, adrenaline, or myth for a few weeks, sometimes even for a season of war. The harder task is to turn that energy into structure: command, recruitment, logistics, role specialization, discipline, financing, public messaging, and a story persuasive enough to keep the whole apparatus coherent. That was the next transformation in the history of Española. If Chapter 1 was about the making of “Ispanec,” this chapter is about the making of the machine that would take his name and outgrow him.
By the time Española emerged publicly in the early phase of the full-scale invasion, it was already presenting itself as more than a volunteer detachment. Multiple source accounts describe a formation made up largely of football hooligans and ultras from across Russia and occupied territories, brought together under Orlov’s command and initially attached to existing structures in the Russian-controlled Donbas battlespace. Some sources place the unit first under the “Tavrida” detachment and later within or alongside the Vostok system; others emphasize its progression out of earlier Donbas networks into a more self-conscious formation of its own. The details vary, but the pattern is consistent: Española began not as a fully sovereign military body, but as a structure incubated inside a broader ecosystem of Russian and separatist irregular formations.
That distinction matters. Movements like Española often claim origin myths of spontaneity—brothers gathering, volunteers answering the call, a unit being born from loyalty rather than paperwork. But war punishes spontaneity quickly. To survive, even an ideologically charged volunteer formation has to find institutional shelter. In Española’s case, that shelter first appears to have come through older Donbas frameworks, and later through Redut, the opaque but increasingly important paramilitary shell widely described by independent reporting as a recruitment and contracting proxy for the Russian Defense Ministry. Multiple sources in the material here state that Española fighters signed contracts with Redut, not with some fully independent company of their own. That fact is central. It means that however intensely the unit cultivated the image of autonomy, it was operating inside a state-managed or state-tolerated system of irregular force.
The contradiction was visible almost from the beginning. On one level, Española wanted to be seen as exceptional: self-selecting, elite, ideologically charged, different from the anonymous mass of contract soldiery and different too from the deadened hierarchy of regular military bureaucracy. On another level, it depended on the very system from which it sought symbolic distinction. The unit could posture as a brotherhood rather than a bureaucracy precisely because other institutions handled pieces of the bureaucratic burden. Redut, according to several reports, was one of the mechanisms that made this possible. Contracts, financing pathways, and organizational legality could be externalized upward, while the brigade itself cultivated the image of organic coherence below. That arrangement gave Orlov and his associates something rare in Russian wartime organization: a hybrid structure that could feel informal from the inside while remaining legible to the system above it.
If that solved one problem, it created another. A formation made from ultras and militants cannot survive on charisma alone. It needs internal differentiation. And here, one of the striking features of Española’s evolution is how quickly it came to describe itself not as a single blob of armed enthusiasm, but as a set of specialized components. Independent retellings and analytical sources describe a unit that by 2023–2024 claimed to include reconnaissance, assault, artillery, electronic warfare, drones, snipers, communications, medics, and other specialist functions. Some of this comes from the unit’s own self-description or from sympathetic media, so it cannot simply be accepted at face value. But the fact that these specializations recur across multiple sources is important in itself. It suggests that Española understood early that reputation in modern war no longer comes only from raw aggressiveness. It comes from technical competence, from being seen as adaptive, and from inhabiting the prestige economy of drone warfare, electronic warfare, and highly mediated battlefield effectiveness.
This is where the unit begins to look less like a gang at war and more like a wartime startup with ideology. It did not simply gather men. It assembled functions. It did not simply promise combat. It promised a model of combat—leaner, more agile, less bureaucratically suffocated, more in tune with the battlefield’s technological drift. One source, clearly sympathetic to the brigade, portrays Española as a kind of experimental formation, half combat unit and half workshop, with a design-bureau mentality around drones, communications, and electronic warfare, plus an internal OSINT-analytical component called “Melody.” Because this description comes from a supportive narrative environment, it must be treated as a self-idealizing portrait rather than neutral fact. But even read skeptically, it reveals the aspiration: Española wanted to be seen not only as brave, but as modern. Not only as ideological, but as efficient. Not only as fighters, but as innovators.
That aspiration was reinforced by the way the unit described its own autonomy. Jamestown, citing Orlov’s own account and Russian reporting, notes that after the expiration of Española’s relationship with Vostok in spring 2023, Orlov claimed the new status gave the unit greater freedom to determine where it fought and how it organized its internal life, including discipline, bureaucracy, and training—with a stated emphasis on anti-drone warfare. This is exactly the sort of self-description that demands careful handling. It may capture a real shift in organizational freedom; it also plainly serves a promotional function, presenting Española as the kind of streamlined force others could envy. Still, even promotional claims can be analytically useful. What Orlov chose to boast about tells us how he wanted the unit understood: not as cannon fodder, not as another anonymous auxiliary, but as a formation with unusual internal sovereignty.
That sovereignty, however, was always partial. And partial sovereignty requires brokers.
One of the names that begins to surface in reporting on the organizational side of Española is Ilya Khanin, described in Meduza’s retelling of iStories reporting as the manager of the unit’s supplies and the brother of Nikolai Khanin, the head of Moscow’s Ramenskoye district. That detail is more than gossip around a quartermaster. It hints at the bridge between subcultural militancy and administrative proximity. A war unit built from hooligans still needs people who can source equipment, move material, organize support, and do so through networks that extend beyond trench loyalty. In the mythology of volunteer war, such figures are often relegated to the background. In reality, they are indispensable. A brigade that wants to seem self-sufficient must have a supply architecture, and supply architectures almost always reveal who is connected to whom.
Then there was the “humanitarian” wing—a term that sounds benign until one looks closely at how such structures actually function. By March 2023, according to Jamestown citing Meduza, Russian media were describing Española as effectively split into two wings: a military one for battlefield tasks and a “humanitarian” one for publicity, recruitment, and non-military activities. That division is one of the most revealing features of the organization. It shows that from relatively early on, Española was not only thinking about combat output. It was thinking about self-reproduction. The humanitarian wing was not just charity. It was a public-facing logistics and image-management apparatus: a way to turn war into civic activity, to normalize the unit’s existence, to recruit, to cultivate patrons, and to make armed militancy look like community participation. It is in that shift—from unit to ecosystem—that Española begins to resemble a durable political-military project rather than a battlefield improvisation.
The rhetoric around this wing makes the logic clearer still. Source descriptions refer to a broad support base of admirers, fans, and helpers across Russia, people sending supplies, equipment, food, and money. In one sense, this fits the now-familiar wartime pattern of patriotic donation networks. In another, it exposes the degree to which Española functioned as a social brand. People did not merely support “the front.” They supported a named formation with recognisable symbols, leaders, and media. That distinction matters because personal loyalty and symbolic capital often convert more efficiently into money and manpower than abstract patriotism does. A war can ask for sacrifice in the general sense; a branded formation can ask for it with greater intimacy. The supporter does not imagine helping an institution. He imagines helping these men, this commander, this brigade.
This is also where the patronage question enters in earnest. One of the most consequential reports in the source base is the Meduza/iStories account alleging that Viktor Shendrik, the head of security at Russian Railways and a figure reportedly close to the Rotenberg orbit, was involved in sponsoring or helping shape Española as the basis for a kind of private army under influential patronage. This must be framed exactly as reported, not as proven institutional fact. The reporting is strong enough to be taken seriously, but it remains reporting based on sources and reconstruction, not a transparent official record. Even so, it points to something larger than one sponsor. It suggests that formations like Española were attractive precisely because they could serve as off-the-shelf armed brands for elite interests—units with their own esprit de corps, their own loyal core, their own public identity, and yet enough formal embedding in the Redut/Defense Ministry ecosystem to remain governable.
If that was the capital structure beneath the unit, propaganda was the oxygen around it.
Several sources emphasize the strength of Española’s media apparatus and domestic outreach. Jamestown notes its “robust online propaganda apparatus” and community programs; Molfar goes further, arguing that the media promotion of the “Española” brand may have been part of a wider effort to produce a highly visible volunteer formation capable of competing in attention with other war brands such as Akhmat, or even of filling some of the symbolic space left by Wagner. That argument should not be overstated, especially because Molfar is itself an adversarial analytical source. But the underlying observation is persuasive. Española did not simply receive media attention. It was built to be media-visible. It understood that in Russia’s wartime public sphere, visibility is a form of power—one that brings recruits, donations, patrons, and leverage. A unit with a strong battlefield reputation but no media face may be respected. A unit with both can become a political object.
The men chosen to embody that visibility were part of the design. Mikhail “Pitbull” Turkanov, for example, emerged as one of the most visible personalities associated with the brigade: decorated, combat-wounded, media-savvy, rooted in football hooliganism yet legible to mainstream audiences. His prominence illustrates a larger organizational truth. Española was not content to have commanders. It needed characters. A wartime brand requires faces who can collapse several audiences into one: the ultra-hooligan milieu, the patriotic donor class, state media viewers, and young men looking for meaning in violence. Orlov may have founded the template, but figures like Turkanov helped scale it.
Recruitment reveals the organizational confidence that followed. By late 2023, according to reporting relayed through Ukrainska Pravda from Vazhnye Istorii and echoed in Jamestown, the unit had begun recruiting women for roles including sniping, medicine, communications, assault positions, electronic warfare, signals intelligence, and UAV operation. Again, this was not recruitment directly into some transparent independent company: the first contracts were reportedly for six months and tied to Redut. But the significance lies elsewhere. A formation built from male ultra culture was widening its intake and presenting itself as a place where even women could “make a career” in modern paramilitary war. That is the language of institutionalization. Gangs do not recruit by career pathway. Organizations do.
At the same time, another version of the brigade persisted: the closed, self-selecting brotherhood described in sympathetic coverage. One source claims Española recruited its own volunteers, trained them itself, supplied itself with communications, drones, motorcycles, and other gear, and was resented by some military officials precisely because it was too independent, too unlike everyone else. This should be read with skepticism. It is plainly celebratory and designed to elevate the brigade’s mystique. But the importance of such language lies in what it tells us about internal identity. Every organization needs a story about itself. Española’s story was that it was chosen rather than assigned, smart rather than merely brave, efficient rather than wasteful, self-directing rather than bureaucratically crushed. In a Russian wartime environment marked by stories of chaos, meat assaults, corruption, and indifference from above, that narrative had obvious appeal.
By this point, the architecture of Española is visible in outline. At the core stood Orlov and the founding myth of the hooligan commander. Around him was a fighting nucleus drawn from fan and ultra networks. Around that nucleus formed layers of specialization: assault, recon, drones, EW, snipers, medics, communications. Parallel to the fighting structure grew a humanitarian-publicity wing that handled recruitment, outreach, legitimacy, and perhaps some of the movement’s soft infrastructure. Beneath it all ran contracting and administrative channels tied to Redut and, by extension, to the larger state-managed ecosystem of Russian volunteer formations. And moving through the whole organism were patrons, supply managers, propagandists, and charismatic public faces. In other words: not a battalion in the narrow sense, but a machine.
What made it potent was not only its battlefield role. It was the way all of these layers reinforced one another. Technical specialization strengthened battlefield claims. Battlefield claims strengthened propaganda. Propaganda attracted patrons and recruits. Patronage sustained logistics. Logistics sustained autonomy, or at least the appearance of autonomy. And the appearance of autonomy, in turn, deepened the myth that Española was something rarer than an ordinary war unit: a formation with soul, discipline, and purpose in a system thought to have squandered all three.
That was the organizational achievement. Not perfection, not total independence, and certainly not ideological coherence in any noble sense. Something more practical than that, and perhaps more dangerous: the successful conversion of a fringe violent milieu into a semi-institutional wartime brand that could fight, recruit, fundraise, self-mythologize, and remain useful to power.
In the next chapter, that utility widens into money and influence. We turn from the internal machine to the external hands that sustained it: patrons, oligarchic adjacency, the “humanitarian” economy, and the hidden political value of a brigade that could turn violence into loyalty and loyalty into an asset.
