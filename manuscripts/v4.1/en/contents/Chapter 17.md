# Chapter 17 — The State Reclaims the Monopoly

No order was announced. No press conference identified the moment of decision. What happened to Española in its final phase happened, as these things tend to happen in modern authoritarian systems, through accumulation rather than declaration: administrative pressure applied at multiple points, organizational labels changed, command relationships clarified, the space available for independent symbolic life narrowed without any single actor being required to say in public that this is what was happening.[^ch17-001]

This is the mechanism that rewards attention, because it is the mechanism that Russia has used, repeatedly and across different domains, to manage entities that it initially permitted and eventually found inconvenient. Prigozhin's Wagner was the extreme case — a formation that became so genuinely autonomous that the recapture, when it came, was violent and could not be disguised. Española was a different case: a formation with more administrative handles, more compliant contracting, and a founder who lacked Prigozhin's resources and political reach. The recapture could be achieved through tightening rather than through destruction. It still amounted to the same thing.

The changing labels around the formation are one of the clearest public indicators of the shift. In the period approaching the autumn of 2025, reporting and the formation's own language increasingly placed it inside the 88th reconnaissance and sabotage brigade, the Volunteer Corps, and the Dobrokor environment — labels that attached the unit more explicitly to formal state structures rather than allowing it to float in the register of an independent volunteer formation. The exact legal implications of each label were sometimes murky in public sources. The direction of travel was not. Each new administrative designation was a step further into the institutional order and a step further away from the branded independence that had made the formation recruitable and memorable in the first place.[^ch17-004]

Later analytical reporting gave that direction a name. Meduza's July 2025 summary of a Sistema investigation described the Dobrocor scheme as a reorganization of the Redut recruitment pipeline after Wagner's mutiny had made the political risks of unmanaged private military force impossible to ignore. The scheme was linked to GRU oversight and to General Vladimir Alekseev as the senior figure journalists identified as overseeing the system. Among the units listed as part of this architecture was Española itself. The report should be read as attributed journalism rather than a fully verified organizational map — the precise relationships remain partially opaque in public sources — but its placement of Española inside a GRU-adjacent supervised mercenary deployment scheme is consistent with everything else the public record shows about the formation's administrative trajectory.[^ch17-009]

The key figure in later analytical reporting on the post-Wagner consolidation, General Alekseev, also appeared in a different context — a reported assassination attempt against him covered by the Jamestown Foundation — that underlined how contested and how dangerous the management of this ecosystem was. The conflicts inside the Russian irregular-force world did not end with Wagner's implosion. They continued through different actors, different formations, and different nodes of patronage. Española's situation was shaped by those larger conflicts as well as by its own internal dynamics.[^ch17-003]

That complexity should not be allowed to obscure the simpler structural point. The same Redut-linked arrangements that had, in the formation's earlier phase, provided administrative cover while leaving the cultural life of the brigade largely intact had, by the late phase, become instruments of recapture. The mechanisms that made the formation legible to the state — its contracts, its command relationships, its administrative registration — were the same mechanisms that made it reducible by the state when the balance of interests changed. That is what "administrative dependence" means in practice. Not an obvious cage, but a structure whose constraints become visible only when someone outside the formation decides to tighten them.

There is a lesson here that extends well beyond Española. States that govern through informal and semi-private military structures do not relinquish control when they outsource to those structures. They defer it. They allow a period of useful autonomy — during which the formation recruits, fights, brands itself, and builds the loyalty networks that make it valuable — and then they reclaim, as conditions require, the authority they had never formally surrendered. The informal sector serves the state's interests precisely because it allows things to be done that the formal sector cannot do elegantly: it can reach populations that official recruitment cannot motivate, operate in legal gray zones, generate propaganda energy that official sources lack the authenticity to produce. But the state that benefits from those capabilities has not stopped being a state. It has simply found a temporarily useful form of distributed operation. When the distribution stops being useful, the reclaim follows.

For Española, the reclaim did not look like a victory for the state. It looked like dissolution, reorganization, and eventually death. Those are not the signs of a clean administrative triumph. They are the signs of a system managing a formation whose integration into the official order had become more costly than its continued semi-independence — a formation that had to be reduced precisely because it had worked.
