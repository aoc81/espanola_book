# Chapter 4 — Donbas as Laboratory

Horlivka in 2014 was a city in the process of becoming something different from what it had been. A city of roughly 250,000 in the Donetsk region, it had been an industrial center under Soviet rule and an economically declining one after it. In the spring and early summer of 2014, as the conflict in eastern Ukraine accelerated, it was becoming a contested space: neither fully controlled by Ukrainian authorities nor fully organized under any coherent separatist command. In that gap, a dense and sometimes violent traffic of local strongmen, Russian irregular fighters, adventurers, former soldiers, nationalist volunteers, and intelligence-adjacent figures competed for territory, loyalty, and meaning.[^ch04-002]

Igor Bezler operated at the center of that traffic. A former Russian military officer, he acquired control over Horlivka and its surroundings through a combination of local organizing, armed intimidation, and patron relationships that outside observers found difficult to map precisely, not least because the lines between Russian state structures, Donetsk People's Republic administration, and personal fiefdom were deliberately blurred. Reporting on the period described an environment in which a capable fighter with the right connections could find space to operate — and even to build something recognizable as a unit — without requiring formal registration or official sanction.[^ch04-002]

Multiple reporting chains and later analytical accounts place Stanislav Orlov inside that environment. The unit most often associated with him in this period, referred to in available accounts as Skull and Bones, appears in sources as a reconnaissance-oriented detachment operating around Horlivka and later in the fighting around Debaltseve, a railway junction city whose capture in early 2015 became one of the most significant Ukrainian military setbacks of the first phase of the war. The admiring accounts describe Skull and Bones as a tight, capable group that drew deliberately on men already habituated to risk and group discipline — men from the supporter world, the nationalist milieu, and adjacent circles. The skeptical accounts note that many of the most flattering descriptions of the unit originate with its own veterans or with commentators invested in the legend's prestige. Both observations are accurate, and neither cancels the other.[^ch04-003]

What Donbas provided, beyond any single unit or episode, was a kind of institutional education. The conditions of the first phase of the conflict were, from the perspective of anyone wanting to build something durable, unusually instructive. Because lines of authority were blurred by design, a talented organizer could experiment with forms that more formalized environments would not permit. The question of whether a group of football-adjacent men could function under battlefield pressure could be tested empirically, in conditions where failure was costly but improvisation was tolerated. If the answer was yes — and the later career of the man who went through that environment suggests it was received as such — the experiment established something important: that subcultural cohesion, properly organized, was transferable to war.[^ch04-003]

But Donbas also taught a lesson that would shape everything that came afterward, one less comfortable for those who preferred the romantic version of voluntary militancy. Irregular intensity alone was never sufficient. Men needed supply, legal shelter, and patrons who could make them administratively legible without demanding that they become ordinary soldiers. Whatever freedom a small detachment enjoyed at the level of style, symbolism, and internal hierarchy, its survival depended on relationships upward — into the command structures, procurement networks, and political intermediaries that had the power to make a unit possible or to shut it down. The first Donbas war was full of half-visible arrangements of exactly this kind. Operating inside them was not a failure of independence. It was a condition of survival.[^ch04-004]

The reported move of Orlov into the Union of Donbas Volunteers after the most active fighting phase should be read in that light. The organization connected veterans, administrators, and political brokers in a more formalized post-2014 environment. It was one of the channels through which fighters who had proved useful in the field became legible to larger wartime networks — and through which those networks could, over time, make those fighters available again. It was not a straightforward state structure, but it was not independent of the state either. It was one of the intermediate layers through which Russia's irregular wartime system organized itself.[^ch04-005]

There is one more element of Donbas that is easy to miss from the distance of the full-scale war. It was in this period, and in this place, that the anti-Azov mirror began to take shape as a usable narrative frame. Ukrainian formations that drew on nationalist and far-right-adjacent milieus — Azov most prominently — became well-known during the Donbas fighting. Russian propagandists and state media made their symbolism and ideology central to the denazification rhetoric that would later justify the 2022 invasion. For men on the Russian side who came from football and nationalist milieus, the existence of a counterpart on the Ukrainian side that had followed a similar path from subculture to armed formation offered a kind of mirror logic. They were not oddities. They were an answer to something. Donbas supplied the stage on which that symmetry became emotionally legible.[^ch04-006]

By the eve of the full-scale invasion, the model was ready to be scaled. Not because a plan had been made in advance, but because the elements had been assembled and tested. A founder with battlefield credibility. A precedent for mobilizing men from supporter networks. A reconnaissance lineage that could be advertised. Experience operating inside the opaque jurisdictions that Russian irregular warfare depended on. The 2022 brigades did not appear from nothing. They appeared from this.
