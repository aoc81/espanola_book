# Author's Note

This book began with a name.

A Russian wartime formation had called itself Española — a word that in Spanish points back to Spain itself, in its feminine form — and attached it to a brigade fighting in Ukraine, derived from the callsign of a former CSKA Moscow supporter who had gone to Donbas in 2014 and come back with enough of a legend to lend it to a brigade. For a Spanish analyst working in open-source intelligence, that name was the first hook. It was unusual enough to demand an explanation before anything else about the formation had come into focus.

I work at Tadaweb, a Luxembourg-based technology company that develops an OSINT platform, used by a range of clients, including public sector organisations. Española entered my field of view not as a book project but as part of a broader analytical task: helping to make sense of the Russian paramilitary ecosystem that had taken shape around the full-scale war in Ukraine — the informal structures, the public narratives, the support networks through which armed formations presented themselves, recruited, fundraised, and became operationally relevant. That ecosystem was large and noisy, and Española was one signal inside it. A loud one, as it turned out.

The more I followed it, the more it resisted simple description. It was not merely a unit. It had a media operation, a fundraising architecture, a humanitarian face, a founder mythology, a visual identity, a set of far-right-adjacent connections that its public language carefully managed, and a relationship with Russian state structures that was simultaneously obvious and deliberately obscured. It was the kind of subject that, once you begin pulling the thread, keeps unraveling in directions you did not expect.

The research method was iterative and multilingual. I moved through Russian, Ukrainian, and English sources, checking claims against each other, collecting and archiving articles before they disappeared or changed, following Telegram channels as they cross-referenced, reposted, and pointed outward. Some of that work was manual — the repetitive, careful kind of attention that open-source work requires. Some of it became technical: small scripts to automate collection, comparison, and organization tasks across large source sets. I also used the Tadaweb platform for one-shot investigations, archiving material and monitoring selected state media, state-adjacent actors, and relevant public channels through filtered keyword streams, which helped separate references to Española from the surrounding noise of the broader wartime information environment.

What accumulated, over time, was an a huge notebook within the platform, a vast archive contanining articles, channel posts, images, claims, names, institutional references, and recurring narratives. The archive could be compared against itself. Sources that agreed on a point without apparent coordination were more reliable than those that agreed along obvious information-chain lines. Claims that recurred across hostile and sympathetic reporting were more trustworthy than those that appeared only in one direction. That comparative method is the main tool this book uses to manage a source environment that was, and remains, significantly contaminated by propaganda, self-promotion, and strategic ambiguity.

This was not an institutional investigation. It was not produced under the editorial supervision of a newspaper or the peer review of a university press. It took more than a year to compile, organise and finally understand what today I am presenting in this book. It has not had access to classified material, confidential witnesses, or any source of information beyond the public record. Those limits are real, and this book tries to be transparent about them in both its methodology note and in the prose of every chapter.

What it has, instead, is time, attention, and a particular vantage point. I am a professional analyst, with a journalist background and technical skills, but not an academic. That outsider's angle turned out to be useful. Some things are more visible when you are not entirely inside the culture you are watching.

The book that emerged from that research is an attempt to do something specific: to take a subject that most coverage treated either as a curiosity or as a data point in a larger story, and to treat it as a case worth following in its own right. Española is interesting not primarily because of what it did on the battlefield — the tactical record is difficult to verify and will be filled in more completely by reporting I do not have access to — but because of what it reveals about how the Russian war system works. How it borrows intensity from cultures it did not build. How it manages the relationship between subcultural autonomy and administrative control. How it uses branding, propaganda, and humanitarian language to extend military force into civilian registers. And how it eventually deals with formations that have become more visible than useful.

That is the argument the book makes. Readers should approach it with the same scepticism they would bring to any open-source analytical reconstruction: checking attributed claims against their sources, noting where uncertainty is preserved and where confidence is stated, and remaining alert to the possibility that new reporting may change the picture.

The record will not settle cleanly. It rarely does.
