Chapter 3 — Patrons, Donations, and the Humanitarian Mask
The economy of Española
War stories often flatter themselves with the language of sacrifice. Men go to the front. Brothers stand together. Volunteers collect aid. Patriotic civilians send what they can. At a certain level, this language is true. No modern war can be sustained without a social economy of support, whether spontaneous or organized, whether ideological or transactional. But in the case of Española, the question of support leads somewhere more revealing than a simple tale of patriotic generosity. It leads into a shadow economy of patronage, branding, logistics, and political usefulness. It leads, in other words, to the people and structures that made the brigade viable not just as a battlefield unit, but as an enduring wartime enterprise. Reporting in 2024 and 2025 repeatedly pointed to two layers of sustenance around Española: a broad “humanitarian” network of donors and sympathizers, and a narrower, more opaque level of elite sponsorship allegedly involving politically connected intermediaries and oligarchic patrons.
The first layer was public and culturally intelligible. By spring 2023, independent reporting summarized by Meduza and later analytical work described Española as effectively divided into two wings: a military one and a “humanitarian” one. The military wing fought. The humanitarian wing handled the softer but no less essential labor of wartime reproduction: publicity, recruitment, aid collection, outreach, and the conversion of the brigade into something socially legible. Jamestown later echoed this picture, describing a humanitarian component involved in non-military activity and a broader support environment that extended beyond the front. This was not merely charity in the ordinary sense. It was part of the organizational design. A unit built from hooligan networks and far-right subcultures needed a way to enter polite patriotic space without fully abandoning its origins. Humanitarian language offered that bridge.
Here the vocabulary matters. In sympathetic and movement-adjacent material, “humanitarian” often functions as a moral solvent: a way of dissolving the harder edges of an armed formation into the more acceptable language of civic duty and brotherly care. But once one looks at what this aid actually did, the distinction grows blurry. Reporting in the source set notes that what Española and its backers often called “humanitarian aid” included not only food or ordinary supplies but also battlefield-relevant materiel such as drones and other equipment. A January 2025 text on the brigade’s fundraising channels describes supporters sponsoring drone purchases and receiving highly personalized recognition for doing so. That is not merely a rear-area charity drive. It is civilian participation in the material upkeep of a war unit that had already turned itself into a brand.
This dual use of humanitarian language was one of Española’s sharpest organizational innovations. It allowed the brigade to present military support as civic virtue. It also allowed supporters to imagine themselves as participants in a moral community rather than simply donors to a paramilitary formation. That framing matters because money flows more easily when it is tied to belonging. A citizen may hesitate to underwrite a mercenary company. He is less likely to hesitate if he believes he is helping “our guys,” “our fans,” “our brothers,” or the defenders of a larger patriotic cause. The humanitarian wing, as described by multiple sources, did not simply gather assistance. It translated violence into community and community back into resources.
The men who seem to have operated this bridge are not incidental figures. Meduza’s English retelling of Cherta’s reporting names Ilya Khanin and Alexey Trifonov as the principal actors in Española’s humanitarian wing and notes that they were involved not only in fundraising and public-facing activity but also in outreach projects, including the creation of a boys’ football team in Horlivka named after Española and decorated with a pirate mascot modeled on the brigade’s iconography. It is difficult to imagine a more revealing example of how the brigade attempted to normalize itself. The point was not just to collect aid. The point was to root the brand in daily life, to make an armed formation composed in part of hooligans and extremists appear as a natural and even admirable part of the civic landscape of occupied territory. Children’s sports, pirate imagery, “humanitarian” work—these were not accidental embellishments. They were mechanisms of cultural laundering.
That public layer of support was extensive enough to sustain a mythology of popular backing. One supportive account describes a humanitarian wing made up of “thousands of friends, fans, admirers” across Russia—some rich, some not, all devoted to the brigade’s spirit and eager to help however they could. The prose is openly celebratory, so it should not be mistaken for neutral evidence. But it is still useful, precisely because it shows how the brigade wished to narrate its own support base: not as a set of transactions, but as a brotherhood extending into the rear. The donor was not simply financing war. He was participating in a movement. That self-understanding gave the brigade one of the advantages more bureaucratic formations often lack: intimacy at scale.
Yet popular support, even when real, does not explain everything. A formation like Española needed more than food parcels and patriotic small donors. It needed access—to transport, to procurement, to money, to political protection, perhaps to sponsors capable of underwriting the larger costs of maintaining an organization that aspired to operate as both combat unit and media brand. This is where the second layer of support enters: elite patronage.
The most consequential reporting in the source base comes from iStories, retold by Meduza in English and discussed by Jamestown. According to that reporting, Viktor Shendrik, head of security at Russian Railways, was the sponsor of the Española battalion, with three sources familiar with Shendrik cited in support. The reporting further states that Shendrik is a protégé of the billionaire brothers Arkady and Boris Rotenberg, two of Vladimir Putin’s closest allies, and that one source described the Rotenbergs as having decided to build a private army “on the basis of Española.” Meduza presents this as reported by iStories; Jamestown later summarized the same picture, saying the Rotenbergs may have been the patrons of the formation and likely financed it through Russian Railways, with Shendrik acting as their confidant and channel of control. This must be handled with care: it is reported, not officially acknowledged. But taken seriously on its own terms, it radically changes the meaning of Española. It suggests that the brigade was not only a grassroots war brand. It was also potentially an asset in the broader privatization of force among Russia’s politically connected elite.
If the reporting is broadly correct, then the “humanitarian” economy around Española was only one side of the ledger. The other side was oligarchic adjacency. And that has strategic consequences. A brigade financed through large patrons has a different political value from one surviving on local enthusiasm alone. It can be better equipped, better connected, and more insulated. It can also carry multiple allegiances at once. Jamestown, reading the available reporting, put the point plainly: Española may have had a dual dependence—on the Russian Ministry of Defense through its subordination to Redut, and on the Rotenberg orbit through financial patronage. That is a subtle but important distinction. Such a unit is not fully private and not fully state. It sits in the useful ambiguity between the two.
This ambiguity was not unique to Española. Meduza situates the brigade within a broader wartime pattern in which Russian state agencies and large corporations were linked to quasi-private units of their own: Roscosmos with Uran, Sberbank and Rosatom with Soyuz, Gazprom with PMC-linked formations, and so on. Within that environment, a brigade like Española could function as both a military asset and a patronage vehicle. It offered sponsors not just fighters, but a ready-made identity: a paramilitary formation with cultural roots, media reach, a compelling internal myth, and an existing recruitment base among organized football fans. In that sense, patronage did not merely subsidize the brigade. It amplified its comparative advantage.
The social reach of that advantage can be seen in the way support for Española bled back into sport itself. Jamestown notes that the popularization of Española’s brand spread not only through football but into hockey, with major teams openly displaying the formation’s symbols during official events. Molfar similarly notes gestures of support from clubs and fan structures. This is not a financial fact in the narrow sense, but it belongs to the same economy. Symbols, endorsements, and institutional tolerance help convert cultural legitimacy into material backing. Once a brigade becomes visible in mainstream sporting space, donations and patronage no longer look like support for fringe militants. They begin to look like support for a patriotic cause already halfway normalized.
One of the more telling details in the source set comes from the organization’s own Telegram metadata, which describes Española not only as a “union of veteran hooligans” but also as the “Española Foundation.” This is exactly the kind of self-description that cannot be treated as neutral, yet it reveals how the organization wanted to institutionalize its support channels. A “foundation” suggests more than a chat-based donation stream. It suggests durability, legitimacy, and a semi-civic frame around the collection of money and resources. Again, the key point is not whether the term reflects a fully transparent legal structure. The point is that the brigade wanted to present itself as an entity around which structured support could be organized.
There is a cynical elegance to all this. The brigade’s mythology depended on appearing fiercely independent—made by fighters, sustained by fans, guided by a commander rather than by faceless bureaucracy. But sustaining that mythology required exactly the kinds of structures independence usually disavows: contracting channels, sponsors, administrators, propagandists, donors, clubs, media surrogates, and possibly elite patrons with their own reasons for investing in a private army-shaped object. The brigade thus performed authenticity while operating through layered systems of support. That was not hypocrisy in the accidental sense. It was its operating model.
Even the language of patriotism, when viewed through this lens, takes on a different cast. Many supporters of Española almost certainly saw themselves as serving the war effort in good faith. That should not be denied. But sincerity does not cancel structure. It often enables it. The small donor who sponsored a drone, the local club that signaled approval, the administrator who moved supplies, the humanitarian organizer who staged a children’s sports event, the patron who allegedly financed the unit through high-level networks—each belonged to a different rung of the same economy. And each helped transform a brigade of hooligans into a paramilitary formation with unusually deep roots.
That helps explain why the humanitarian mask was so valuable. It did not merely soften perception. It linked the lower and upper tiers of support. For ordinary sympathizers, it offered a moralized vocabulary of aid. For sponsors, it offered a socially insulated mechanism through which money and influence could reach a branded fighting formation without appearing as naked patronage. For the brigade itself, it turned war into a social ecosystem. The front was not only where men fought. It was where the organization justified its existence. The rear was where that existence was financed, narrated, and renewed.
In the end, the importance of Española’s economy lies not just in who may have paid for it, but in what kind of organization such funding made possible. This was not simply a militia living off enthusiasm. It was a layered war enterprise, at once intimate and hierarchical, popular and elite, civic in language and military in purpose. The men at the front carried rifles, drones, and symbols. Behind them stood clubs, donors, organizers, media channels, alleged patrons, and the careful rhetoric of humanitarian virtue. Remove that supporting world, and the brigade becomes much smaller. Understand it, and Española begins to look less like an aberration and more like a model.
In the next chapter, we follow that model into the public sphere: the images, channels, performances, and personalities through which Española learned not merely to fight the war, but to sell it—first to its own milieu, then to a broader Russian audience.
