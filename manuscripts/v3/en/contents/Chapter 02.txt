Chapter 2 — Stanislav Orlov and the Making of "The Spaniard"

Stanislav Orlov is easiest to understand when treated not only as a man, but as a constructed figure. The record around him contains enough agreement to establish a broad trajectory and enough uncertainty to prevent hagiography. Multiple accounts place him in the CSKA Moscow ultra milieu, specifically around the Red-Blue Warriors. Multiple accounts also describe his later service in the Russian armed forces and his entry into the Donbas war after 2014. But the closer the story moves toward personality and motive, the murkier it becomes. That uncertainty is not incidental. It is part of how figures like Orlov become usable.

What can be said with relative confidence is that Orlov did not arrive in war as a blank civilian. The supporter scene had already taught him hierarchy, loyalty, and the prestige economy of confrontation. Biographical accounts circulated later by both sympathetic and hostile sources present military service in the late 1990s as the next stage in that formation, often linking him to the Airborne Troops and to service in the North Caucasus. Those details should remain carefully attributed. Some come through movement-adjacent profiles, others through later analytical writing. What matters here is less the exact roster of deployments than the continuity the biographies try to establish: the terrace militant becomes the soldier, and the soldier becomes legible as a future commander.

The decisive turn came in 2014, when eastern Ukraine became a zone where Russian state interests, separatist structures, adventurism, and ideological militancy overlapped. Independent reporting and later analysis agree that Orlov traveled to Horlivka and joined forces under Igor Bezler. Several sources say his language skills helped place him around foreign volunteers or mercenaries. It was in that setting, according to the overlapping accounts, that he acquired the callsign "Ispanec" — the Spaniard. Different sources explain the nickname differently. Some stress his Spanish language ability. Others tie it more directly to his work around foreigners. The disagreement matters less than the function of the alias. It was memorable, transportable, and slightly theatrical. It gave the man a persona that could outgrow biography.

That persona hardened further in Donbas. Sources describe Orlov as helping to build or lead a reconnaissance unit known as "Skull and Bones," which operated around Horlivka and Debaltseve and drew on football-hooligan networks. Here again the evidentiary posture matters. The existence of the unit appears across multiple accounts, but many of the more flattering claims about its skill and originality come from later narratives sympathetic to Orlov's legend. Even so, the basic significance is clear enough. Donbas gave him more than battlefield experience. It gave him a laboratory in which subcultural cohesion could be tested under military conditions.

In 2015, according to several later accounts, Orlov moved into the Union of Donbas Volunteers, the Surkov-linked structure led by Alexander Khodakovsky. This shift is important because it suggests his trajectory was already moving beyond improvised militancy. He was entering a more state-adjacent sphere in which fighters, veterans, administrators, and political intermediaries overlapped. The point is not that he was now fully institutional. He was not. The point is that he was becoming legible to systems of power larger than the battlefield group he had started in.

One of the most delicate episodes in his biography concerns Montenegro in 2016. Some later analytical and intelligence-style sources allege that Orlov was involved in or connected to the failed coup attempt against Milo Djukanovic. The claim recurs often enough to matter, but the evidence in the available source base does not support presenting it as established fact. It belongs in the story as an allegation that reveals how Orlov was understood by later observers: not simply as a Donbas veteran, but as someone whose name could plausibly surface in the gray space where covert action, irregular force, and state interest meet.

For a time, Orlov then recedes. The gap matters because it interrupts the temptation to tell a smooth heroic story. When he reemerges after the full-scale invasion of 2022, he does so with something larger than a biography. He has a war, a network, a callsign that can be turned into a label, and a set of lessons drawn from Donbas: hooligan loyalty can be militarized, informal structures can be made useful to power, and a commander who is also a symbol can recruit more effectively than an anonymous officer.

The name Espanola completed that conversion. A brigade named after the alias of its founder fuses person and institution from the start. It invites followers to see the formation not as a unit number in a bureaucratic chain, but as the extension of a story already in motion. That was one of Orlov's real achievements, whether by design, instinct, or both. He understood that in modern irregular war, a name can function as logistics, recruitment, and myth at once.

By the time the brigade became visible in 2022, the key elements of the founder's myth were already in place: terrace violence, military service, Donbas legitimacy, an alias, a reconnaissance lineage, and enough ambiguity to let admirers and investigators tell different versions of the same life. The next step was to widen that myth beyond one man and make it durable enough to absorb men who had once regarded each other as enemies.
