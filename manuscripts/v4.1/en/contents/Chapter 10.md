# Chapter 10 — Pitbull and the Faces of the Formation

Mikhail Turkanov — known by the callsign Pitbull — circulated through Española's public record as something close to an emblem. He appeared in photographs and channel headers, in reporting by the Jamestown Foundation and others, in visual materials that made the milieu around the brigade legible at a glance. He was stocky, heavily tattooed, bearing imagery that investigators and anti-fascist monitoring groups identified as associated with far-right subcultures. For followers of the brigade, the same image carried a different reading: not ideology so much as credibility, the visible mark of a man who had come from a real place and arrived by a real route. That tension — between what the image said to sympathizers and what it said to critics — was not incidental to Turkanov's function inside the formation. It was his function.[^ch10-003]

The place Turkanov came from was the Zenit Saint Petersburg hooligan scene — a milieu distinct from CSKA's Red-Blue Warriors but adjacent to it in the shared landscape of Russian ultra culture. His path into Española's command structure was not merely that of a tough man making himself available. He arrived with social capital from a specific, recognized corner of the supporter world. Before the war, Cherta Media's reporting noted, he had accumulated a legal record to match: a prior imprisonment for extortion, and a 2019 fine for the public display of Nazi symbols. The symbols charge is not trivial in an account of the formation's ideological environment. It was not an allegation that investigators were reaching for a connection where none existed. It was a documented incident, on the record, that preceded his public career as one of Española's most visible faces by three years.[^ch10-003b]

International compliance systems eventually arrived at their own reading. OpenSanctions lists Mikhail Victorovich Turkanov, born 1987 in Saint Petersburg, under the alias "The Pitbull," as a sanctioned and export-controlled individual. The U.K. designated him on 7 November 2024. The database entry is not a portrait; it is a record of official exposure. By the time such an entry exists, a face that once operated inside a subcultural register has become legible to a different administrative apparatus entirely: the banks, the compliance officers, the investigators, and the governments that use such lists to track actors they consider relevant to their interests. A man whose public image was built for one audience had been catalogued by another.[^ch10-007]

Española did not hide behind anonymity, and the decision not to was structural. A formation built around a founder myth and a subcultural identity needed faces the way a sports team needs players fans can name. Without recognizable individuals, the brand has no scale model — no way to demonstrate in a single person what the formation is supposed to represent. The faces that emerged around Española served different functions for different audiences, and their diversity was itself part of the design.[^ch10-001]

Orlov was irreplaceable at the center. A brigade named after his callsign was by definition organized around his biography: the CSKA past, the Donbas service, the legend of Skull and Bones, the alias that had become famous enough to lend its name to a wartime brand. He was not merely a commander whose authority derived from rank. He was the organizing fact of the formation's story, the answer to the question of why this brigade and not another. His public presence — in channel messages, in photographs, in the accounts that circulated about him — kept that story active. Every post associated with him was evidence that the founder was still present, still invested, still available as the point of reference around which everything else was arranged.[^ch10-002]

Andrei Solomatin — callsign Soloma — was a former professional footballer, a figure associated with CSKA and Lokomotiv Moscow, who joined the brigade publicly in 2023. A professional sports figure in the orbit of a wartime formation built from supporter culture offers a kind of endorsement that crosses a significant social boundary. He was not from the hooligan milieu. His presence said something about the formation's reach into wider Russian football culture — a signal that Española was not merely a vehicle for the hard fringe, but something broader, something that a man with a professional athletic career had chosen to associate himself with. That bridge-building did not require Solomatin to match Turkanov's symbolic roughness. It required only his proximity.[^ch10-004]

Another figure in the formation's amplification network requires naming separately. Alexey Zhivov — identified on the Ukrainian War and Sanctions portal as a pro-war propagandist and material supporter of the formation, with sanctions noted across partner jurisdictions — was not a fighter but a promoter and a logistics enabler: active in UAV and electronic-warfare fundraising campaigns, visible in the pro-war media ecosystem, and connected to the charity and organizational structures around the brigade. The Ukrainian sanctions-tracking portal treated him as a serious node in the formation's support architecture rather than a peripheral commentator. That reading, coming from an adversarial intelligence apparatus, should be treated as an attributed claim rather than an established legal finding; but the underlying pattern of public activity that generated the claim is itself documented.[^ch10-004b]

The auxiliary figures extended the range further. Khanin, active in the humanitarian and logistics sphere as described earlier, represented the formation's civic and rear-area face. His visibility allowed people who were not fighters and not supporters to find a role they could occupy: the organizer, the aide worker, the person who helped from behind the lines. Foreign-adjacent propagandists and recruits widened that range again. Among the most legible examples was Gregory Rossel, better known as Teddy Boy Greg: an American rockabilly barber who relocated to Moscow, opened Teddy's Rock 'n' Roll Barbershop, and publicly framed Russia as a refuge from what he described as liberal political correctness and the erosion of traditional male space. By the time Searchlight later identified him as the formation's most prominent American recruit, that earlier self-fashioning had become politically useful. He was not merely an eccentric foreigner in the orbit of the brigade; he was a ready-made propaganda asset whose biography translated Española's worldview for a Western dissident audience already primed to hear it.[^ch10-005][^ch10-005b]

Together, these figures composed a kind of cast: founder, warrior, sporting bridge, civic broker, foreign amplifier. None needed to perform all roles at once. Each extended the formation's range into an audience it would otherwise have struggled to reach. That range was the product not of a single charismatic leader operating alone, but of a distributed roster of visible archetypes that made the ecosystem legible from multiple directions.

There was a cost embedded in this strategy, visible in retrospect. A formation that circulates through recognizable individuals accumulates symbolic weight faster than an anonymous unit — but it also becomes harder to control and easier to scrutinize. Every face is a point of entry for investigation. Every public figure is a site where the formation's ideology, connections, and internal culture can be read by people whose intentions are not sympathetic. Compliance databases, sanctions listings, journalistic profiles, and intelligence assessments all follow from the decision to make a formation's people visible. The same openness that made Española recruitable made it, eventually, documentable in ways the formation's patrons may not have found comfortable.
